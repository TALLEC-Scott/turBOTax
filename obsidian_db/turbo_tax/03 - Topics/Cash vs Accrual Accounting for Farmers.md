# Cash vs Accrual Accounting for Farmers

## Overview

Farmers must choose an accounting method that clearly shows income and expenses. The choice affects when income and expenses are reported and can have significant tax implications.

## Cash Method

### How It Works
- Report income when actually or constructively received
- Deduct expenses when actually paid
- Most common method for individual farmers

### Advantages
- Simpler recordkeeping
- Flexibility in timing income and deductions
- May defer tax by delaying sales or prepaying expenses

### Limitations
- Prepaid expenses subject to limitations
- Inventory items deducted when sold, not when purchased
- May need to use accrual for certain items

### Eligibility
- Most farmers can use cash method
- Exceptions for certain corporations and tax shelters
- Special rules for farming corporations with gross receipts over $31 million

## Accrual Method

### How It Works
- Report income when earned (all events test)
- Deduct expenses when incurred
- Must maintain inventories

### When Required
- Certain C corporations
- Tax shelters
- Partnerships with C corporation partners
- Farming businesses with gross receipts exceeding $31 million

### Uniform Capitalization Rules
- Must capitalize certain costs into inventory
- Required for accrual method farmers
- Exceptions for small farmers

## Crop Method

### Special Method for Farmers
- Deduct entire cost of raising a crop in year crop is sold
- Requires IRS approval to use
- Useful for crops with long growing seasons

## Changing Accounting Methods

### Requirements
- File Form 3115 to request change
- Some changes require IRS approval
- Others have automatic consent procedures

### Section 481(a) Adjustment
- Prevents items from being duplicated or omitted
- May need to spread adjustment over multiple years
