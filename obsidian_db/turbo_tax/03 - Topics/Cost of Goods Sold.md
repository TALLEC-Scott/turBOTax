# Cost of Goods Sold

## Overview

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) represents the direct costs attributable to the production or purchase of goods sold by a business. COGS is calculated on [[Schedule C (Form 1040)]] lines 35-42 and reported on Line 4, reducing gross receipts to arrive at gross profit.

Per [[Pub 334 - Tax Guide for Small Business]], if your business produces, purchases, or sells merchandise, you generally must account for inventory and calculate COGS.

## Who Must Calculate COGS

You must calculate COGS if:
- Your business involves selling products
- You maintain inventory
- [[Cost of Goods Sold|Income-producing factor]] involves merchandise

**Service businesses** without inventory typically do not calculate COGS—gross profit equals net receipts.

## COGS Formula

```
Beginning Inventory
+ Purchases
+ Cost of Labor
+ Materials and Supplies
+ Other Costs
= Goods Available for Sale
- Ending Inventory
= Cost of Goods Sold
```

## Schedule C Lines 35-42

| Line | Description | Details |
|------|-------------|---------|
| 35 | Inventory at beginning of year | Must match prior year ending inventory |
| 36 | Purchases less personal withdrawals | Cost of merchandise bought for resale |
| 37 | Cost of labor | Direct/indirect labor in manufacturing |
| 38 | Materials and supplies | Used in manufacturing process |
| 39 | Other costs | Freight-in, containers, overhead |
| 40 | Total | Goods available for sale |
| 41 | Inventory at end of year | Value on last day of tax year |
| 42 | Cost of goods sold | Line 40 minus Line 41 |

## Inventory Valuation Methods

Per [[Pub 334 - Tax Guide for Small Business]] Chapter 2:

### Cost Method
- Actual cost of items in inventory
- Includes invoice price, freight-in, handling

### Lower of Cost or Market (LCM)
- Compare cost to replacement cost
- Use lower value

### Retail Method
- Estimate cost using retail price ratio
- Useful for retailers

## Beginning Inventory (Line 35)

For merchants: Cost of merchandise on hand at year start
For manufacturers: Raw materials + work-in-process + finished goods

**Important:** Beginning inventory must equal prior year ending inventory. Explain any difference on attached schedule.

### Donation of Inventory
- Remove donated items from opening inventory
- Charitable deduction limited to FMV or basis (whichever is less)
- See [[Pub 526 - Charitable Contributions]]

## Purchases (Line 36)

Include:
- Cost of merchandise bought for resale
- Raw materials for manufacturing
- Parts for production

Reduce by:
- Trade discounts (use actual price paid)
- Purchase returns and allowances
- Items withdrawn for personal use

**Note:** Merchandise withdrawn for personal/family use must be removed from purchases.

## Cost of Labor (Line 37)

Primarily for manufacturing/production businesses:

### Direct Labor
Wages for employees working directly on the product

### Indirect Labor
Wages for factory support staff (maintenance, supervision)

**Do not include:** Your own compensation as sole proprietor

## Materials and Supplies (Line 38)

- Raw materials used in production
- Hardware, chemicals, components
- Items not used in production = business expenses when used

## Other Costs (Line 39)

### Containers and Packaging
- Integral to product: Include in COGS
- Not integral: Shipping/selling expense

### Freight-In
Transportation costs for raw materials and merchandise purchased

### Overhead Expenses
Manufacturing overhead includes:
- Rent for production facility
- Utilities for manufacturing
- Insurance on production equipment
- Depreciation of manufacturing assets
- Factory supervision and maintenance

## Ending Inventory (Line 41)

Value on the last day of the tax year:
- Count all items physically present
- Use consistent valuation method
- Becomes next year's beginning inventory

## Gross Profit Calculation

```
Gross Receipts (Line 1)
- Returns and Allowances (Line 2)
= Net Receipts (Line 3)
- Cost of Goods Sold (Line 4)
= Gross Profit (Line 5)
```

## Testing Gross Profit Accuracy

For retail/wholesale businesses:
1. Divide gross profit by net receipts
2. Compare percentage to your markup policy
3. Large differences may indicate errors in sales, purchases, or inventory

## Common Questions

### Q: Can I deduct COGS without keeping inventory records?
**A:** Generally no. If inventory is an income-producing factor, you must account for inventory and use an accrual method for purchases and sales (unless qualifying as a small business taxpayer).

### Q: What if I use the cash method of accounting?
**A:** Small business taxpayers (average annual gross receipts ≤ $31 million) may use the cash method even with inventory.

### Q: How do I value donated inventory?
**A:** Remove donated items from opening inventory. The charitable deduction is limited to the smaller of FMV or basis. See [[Pub 526 - Charitable Contributions]].
