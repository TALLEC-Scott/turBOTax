# Roth Contributions

## Overview

Roth contributions are after-tax contributions to retirement accounts. Unlike traditional pre-tax contributions, Roth contributions are included in gross income in the year of contribution, but qualified distributions are entirely tax-free. The SECURE 2.0 Act expanded Roth contribution options to SEP and SIMPLE plans.

## Types of Roth Contributions

### Designated Roth Contributions
- Made to designated Roth accounts within 401(k), 403(b), or 457(b) plans
- After-tax elective deferrals
- Subject to same deferral limits as pre-tax contributions

### Designated Roth Nonelective/Matching Contributions
- After-tax employer contributions
- Available for tax years after 2022 (SECURE 2.0)
- Reported on Form 1099-R

### Roth IRA Contributions
- Personal contributions to Roth IRA
- Subject to separate IRA contribution limits ($7,000 for 2025-2026)
- Income limits apply for eligibility

### Roth SEP IRA
- Available for tax years beginning after 2022 (SECURE 2.0)
- SEP contributions can be made to a Roth IRA

### Roth SIMPLE IRA
- Available for tax years beginning after 2022 (SECURE 2.0)
- SIMPLE contributions can be made to a Roth SIMPLE IRA

## Elective Deferral Limits (2025-2026)

The same deferral limits apply to both pre-tax and Roth contributions combined:

| Year | Base Limit | Age 50+ | Ages 60-63 |
|------|------------|---------|------------|
| 2025 | $23,500 | $31,000 | $34,750 |
| 2026 | $24,500 | $32,500 | $35,750 |

**Note**: Can split contributions between pre-tax and Roth, but combined total cannot exceed limits.

## Qualified Distributions

A qualified distribution from a designated Roth account is tax-free if it meets BOTH:

1. **5-Year Requirement**: Made after the 5-tax-year period beginning with the first year a Roth contribution was made to ANY Roth account under the plan

2. **One of the Following**:
   - Made on or after reaching age 59½
   - Made on or after death
   - Attributable to disability
   - Made as part of a series of substantially equal periodic payments

## Non-Qualified Distributions

If distribution does not meet qualified distribution requirements:
- Earnings are taxable (contributions returned tax-free)
- Earnings may be subject to 10% early distribution tax

## Ordering Rules for Distributions

Distributions from designated Roth accounts are treated as made in this order:
1. Regular Roth contributions (tax-free return of basis)
2. Rollover contributions (tax-free)
3. Earnings (taxable if not qualified)

## No Lifetime RMDs for Roth Accounts

- Designated Roth accounts have NO lifetime required minimum distributions
- RMDs only required after death of participant
- Different from Roth IRAs (which never have RMDs)

## Tax Treatment

### Year of Contribution
| Contribution Type | Income Tax Treatment |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Traditional Pre-tax | Excluded from gross income |
| Designated Roth | Included in gross income |

### Payroll Taxes
Both pre-tax and Roth contributions are subject to:
- Social Security tax
- Medicare tax
- FUTA tax

## In-Plan Roth Rollovers

Participants can roll over amounts from a traditional account to a designated Roth account within the same plan:
- Must include rollover amount in gross income
- Cannot spread income over multiple years
- 5-year period begins with year of rollover
- Not subject to 10% early distribution tax

## Reporting Requirements

### Employee Deferrals
- Reported on Form W-2, box 12
- Separate reporting for Roth vs. pre-tax

### Employer Nonelective/Matching Roth Contributions
- Reported on Form 1099-R
- Code 2 or 7 in box 7
- Check IRA/SEP/SIMPLE checkbox if applicable

## Advantages of Roth Contributions

1. **Tax-free qualified distributions**: No tax on earnings if requirements met
2. **No lifetime RMDs** (for designated Roth accounts in qualified plans)
3. **Tax diversification**: Mix of pre-tax and post-tax retirement income
4. **Beneficiary advantages**: Tax-free distributions to beneficiaries if qualified

## Considerations

1. **Current tax cost**: Paying tax now vs. later
2. **Future tax rates**: If rates will be higher, Roth may be beneficial
3. **Estate planning**: Roth accounts can pass tax-free to beneficiaries
4. **Income limits**: No income limits for designated Roth contributions (unlike Roth IRAs)
