# Dual-Status Aliens

## Overview

A dual-status alien is an individual who is both a nonresident alien and a resident alien during the same tax year. This typically occurs in:

- The year of arrival in the United States
- The year of departure from the United States

## Residency Periods

### Year of Arrival
- **Nonresident period:** January 1 through day before residency starting date
- **Resident period:** Residency starting date through December 31

### Year of Departure
- **Resident period:** January 1 through residency ending date
- **Nonresident period:** Day after residency ending date through December 31

## Tax Treatment

### Resident Period
- Taxed on worldwide income
- Use graduated tax rates
- Can claim all deductions available to residents

### Nonresident Period
- Taxed only on U.S. source income
- ECI taxed at graduated rates
- FDAP income taxed at 30% or treaty rate
- Limited deductions (only for ECI)

## Restrictions for Dual-Status Years

| Restriction | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Standard deduction** | Cannot claim standard deduction |
| **Head of household** | Cannot use head of household status |
| **Joint return** | Generally cannot file jointly (unless choosing resident status) |
| **E-filing** | Cannot e-file for 2025 dual-status returns |

## Filing Requirements

### Resident at Year-End
File [[Form 1040]] with "Dual Status Return" written at top. Attach [[Form 1040-NR]] as statement showing nonresident period income.

### Nonresident at Year-End
File [[Form 1040-NR]] with "Dual Status Return" written at top. Attach [[Form 1040]] as statement showing resident period income.

## Choosing Resident Alien Status

A dual-status alien married to a U.S. citizen or resident may choose to be treated as a resident for the entire year by:

1. Filing a joint return [[Form 1040]]
2. Making the election on the return
3. Both spouses agreeing to report worldwide income

**Effect:** Treated as resident for entire year; can file jointly; worldwide income taxation.

## First-Year Choice

If you do not meet the substantial presence test in your arrival year but will meet it the following year, you may elect to be treated as a resident from the arrival date if you:

1. Are present in the U.S. for at least 31 consecutive days
2. Are present for at least 75% of the period from arrival to year-end
3. Meet the substantial presence test in the following year

## Tax Credits

Dual-status aliens can generally claim credits available to resident aliens, except:

- Cannot claim education credits
- Cannot claim Earned Income Credit (unless choosing resident status)
- Cannot claim credit for elderly/disabled (unless choosing resident status)

**Available Credits:**
- Foreign tax credit (for resident period)
- Child tax credit
- Child and dependent care credit
- Retirement savings contributions credit
