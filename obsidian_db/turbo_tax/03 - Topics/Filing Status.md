# Filing Status

Your filing status determines your tax rates, standard deduction amount, and eligibility for certain credits and deductions.

## Five Filing Statuses

### 1. Single
Use if unmarried on the last day of the tax year and do not qualify for another status.

**Standard Deduction (2025):** $15,750

### 2. Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)
- Both spouses report combined income and deductions
- Can file jointly even if one spouse had no income
- Joint responsibility for tax, interest, and penalties
- Often results in lower tax than filing separately

**Standard Deduction (2025):** $31,500

### 3. Married Filing Separately (MFS)
- Each spouse reports own income and deductions
- May benefit if want responsibility only for own tax
- Often results in higher combined tax
- Some credits and deductions are limited or unavailable

**Standard Deduction (2025):** $15,750

### 4. Head of Household (HOH)
Unmarried taxpayers who:
- Paid more than half the cost of keeping up a home
- Have a qualifying person living in the home for more than half the year
- Were a U.S. citizen for the entire year

**Standard Deduction (2025):** $23,625

**Benefits over Single:**
- Higher standard deduction
- Lower tax brackets
- Eligibility for [[Earned Income Credit]]

### 5. Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS)
Available for 2 years after spouse's death if:
- Did not remarry
- Have a dependent child or stepchild
- Paid more than half the cost of maintaining the home

**Benefits:** Uses same tax rates and standard deduction as Married Filing Jointly

## Determining Marital Status

### Considered Married
You are considered married for the entire year if on the last day:
1. Married and living together
2. Living together in a recognized common law marriage
3. Married and living apart but not legally separated
4. Separated under an interlocutory (not final) divorce decree

### Considered Unmarried
You are considered unmarried if on the last day:
- Not married, or
- Legally separated under a divorce or separate maintenance decree

### Spouse Died During Year
- Considered married for the entire year for filing status purposes
- Can still file a joint return for that year

## Choosing the Best Status

If more than one filing status applies, choose the one with the lowest tax.

**Common scenarios:**
- Married couples: Compare MFJ vs. MFS (both methods)
- Unmarried with dependents: Check if qualify for HOH
- Recently widowed: Check if qualify for QSS

## Impact on Tax Benefits

| Benefit | MFJ | MFS | HOH | Single |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|--------|
| Child Tax Credit | Full | Limited | Full | Full |
| Earned Income Credit | Yes | No | Yes | Limited |
| Education Credits | Yes | No | Yes | Yes |
| IRA Deduction Limits | Higher | Lower | Higher | Lower |
| SALT Deduction Cap | $40,000 | $20,000 | $40,000 | $40,000 |

## Military Considerations

Per [[Pub 3 - Armed Forces Tax Guide]]:

### Same-Sex Marriage
For federal tax purposes, marriages of same-sex couples are treated the same as opposite-sex marriages. You must select married filing jointly or married filing separately.

### Registered Domestic Partnerships
If you're in a registered domestic partnership, civil union, or similar relationship not considered a marriage under state law, you're not considered married for federal tax purposes. Select single or head of household (if eligible).

### Spouse in Combat Zone
If your spouse is in a combat zone, you may:
- File a joint return with their written statement (or power of attorney)
- Sign the return with "filing as surviving spouse" notation
- Claim any refund due to a deceased spouse

### Nonresident Alien Spouse
You can choose to treat a nonresident alien spouse as a U.S. resident for tax purposes by filing a joint return. This is indicated by a checkbox in the Filing Status section of Form 1040.
