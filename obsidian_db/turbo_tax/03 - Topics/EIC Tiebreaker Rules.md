# EIC Tiebreaker Rules

When a child meets the tests to be a [[Qualifying Child|qualifying child]] for more than one person, the [[Earned Income Credit|EIC tiebreaker rules]] determine who can claim the child. These rules are found in [[Pub 596 - Earned Income Credit|Publication 596, Rule 9]].

## Key Principle

**Only ONE person** can treat a child as a qualifying child for EIC purposes, even if the child qualifies multiple people. You cannot split the benefits between taxpayers.

## Five Tax Benefits Affected

The tiebreaker rules apply to all five of these tax benefits together:
1. [[Child Tax Credit]] and additional child tax credit
2. [[Credit for Other Dependents]]
3. [[Head of Household]] filing status
4. [[Child and Dependent Care Credit]]
5. [[Earned Income Credit]]

**Important:** If one person uses the child for any of these benefits, no other person can use the same child for ANY of these benefits.

## Tiebreaker Rules Priority

### Rule 1: Parent vs. Non-Parent
If only one of the persons is the child's parent, the child is treated as the qualifying child of the **parent**.

### Rule 2: Parents Filing Jointly
If both parents file a joint return together and can claim the child, the child is treated as the qualifying child of the parents jointly.

### Rule 3: Parents Not Filing Jointly
If parents don't file jointly but both claim the child:
1. The parent with whom the child lived **longer** during the year wins
2. If equal time, the parent with the **higher AGI** wins

### Rule 4: No Parent Can Claim
If no parent can claim the child as a qualifying child:
- The child goes to the person with the **highest AGI**

### Rule 5: Parent Can Claim But Doesn't
If a parent can claim the child but doesn't:
- The child goes to the non-parent with the highest AGI, BUT ONLY IF that person's AGI is **higher than any parent's AGI** who could claim the child

## Definition of Parent

For tiebreaker purposes, "parent" means:
- Biological parent, OR
- Adoptive parent

"Parent" does NOT include:
- Stepparent (unless they adopted the child)
- Foster parent (unless they adopted the child)

## Examples

### Example 1: Child Lives with Parent and Grandparent
You and your 2-year-old child live with your parent all year. Your AGI is $9,000; your parent's AGI is $22,000. The child is a qualifying child of both of you.

**Result:** You (the parent) can claim the child because you are the parent.

### Example 2: Grandparent Has Higher AGI Than Parent
Same as Example 1, but your AGI is $25,000.

**Result:** Your parent cannot claim the child because their AGI is not higher than yours.

### Example 3: Two Parents Claim Same Child
Divorced parents share custody 50/50. Mother's AGI is $35,000; Father's AGI is $42,000.

**Result:** Father can claim the child because he has higher AGI (equal time tiebreaker).

### Example 4: Child Did Not Live with a Parent
You and your nephew live with your parent. Your AGI is $9,300; your parent's AGI is $15,000. The child's parents don't live with you.

**Result:** Your parent can claim the child because they have higher AGI.

## Agreement Between Taxpayers

Subject to the tiebreaker rules, taxpayers who both could claim a child may be able to **agree** which one claims the child:
- The person who doesn't claim the child must not claim ANY of the five tax benefits for that child
- This can be advantageous if one person's credit would be larger

## Special Rule for Divorced/Separated Parents

Under the special rule for children of divorced parents:
- The noncustodial parent may claim [[Child Tax Credit]] if Form 8332 is signed
- BUT the custodial parent retains the right to claim the child for [[Earned Income Credit]]

See [[Children of Divorced Parents]] for more details.

## Self-Only EIC Option

If your qualifying child is treated as another person's qualifying child under the tiebreaker rules:
- You cannot claim EIC with a qualifying child
- You MAY be able to claim "self-only EIC" using the rules for taxpayers without a qualifying child (if you meet those requirements)

See [[Earned Income Credit]] for self-only EIC rules.

## What If the Other Person Cannot Claim EIC?

If the other person could use the child for tiebreaker purposes but cannot claim EIC due to:
- Income too high
- Not eligible for other reasons

You still cannot use that child for EIC if the other person uses the child for any of the five tax benefits listed above.

## Documentation

Keep records showing:
- Who the child lived with and for how long
- Each person's AGI
- Any agreements about who claims the child

The IRS may request documentation to verify eligibility.
