# Excess Contributions

## Overview

If your actual contributions (not including catch-up contributions) to a [[403(b) Plans|403(b) plan]] exceed your Maximum Amount Contributable (MAC), you have an excess contribution. Excess contributions can result in income tax, additional taxes, and penalties.

## Determining Excess Contributions

At the end of each year or beginning of the next year, refigure your MAC based on:
- Your actual compensation for the year
- Actual contributions made to your account

If actual contributions exceed your MAC, you have an excess contribution.

## Types of Excess Contributions

### Excess Annual Addition

An excess annual addition occurs when total contributions exceed your [[Annual Additions Limit]].

**Consequences**:
- Excess amount included in your income for the year
- May be subject to 6% excise tax (custodial accounts only)
- Excise tax applies each year excess remains in account

**Excise Tax Exceptions**:
- Does NOT apply to annuity contracts
- Does NOT apply to retirement income accounts
- Does NOT apply to excess deferrals

### Excess Elective Deferral

An excess elective deferral occurs when your [[Elective Deferrals]] exceed the limit on elective deferrals.

**Consequences**:
- Excess included in gross income for year contributed
- Earnings on excess taxed in year distributed

## Correcting Excess Contributions

### Correction During the Year

A corrective distribution of excess deferrals may be made if:
- Distribution occurs after the date the excess deferral was made
- The plan and you/employer designate the distribution as an excess deferral

### Correction After the Year

For excess deferrals, you may receive a correcting distribution:
- **Deadline**: April 15 of the following year
- The plan can distribute the excess plus allocable earnings

## Tax Treatment of Corrected Excess Deferrals

### Non-Roth Excess Deferrals

If distributed by April 15:
- Excess is included in income for year **contributed**
- Earnings are taxed in year **distributed**

### Roth Excess Deferrals

For excess deferrals attributable to Roth contributions, see Regulations section 1.402(g)-1(e).

## Reporting Requirements

### Excise Tax (Excess Annual Additions)
- File [[Form 5330]] if excess contribution to custodial account
- Required if excess hasn't been corrected

### Income Inclusion
- Excess amounts are included in taxable income
- May affect AGI for various limitations

## Prevention Strategies

1. **Refigure MAC regularly** - Especially if compensation changes
2. **Use conservative estimates** - When planning contributions
3. **Monitor multiple accounts** - Combine contributions from same employer
4. **Coordinate with employer** - Track contribution levels throughout year
