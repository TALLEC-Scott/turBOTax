# Tax Treaties

## Overview

The United States has bilateral income tax treaties (also called conventions) with over 60 countries. These treaties prevent double taxation and provide special benefits for taxpayers who are residents of treaty countries.

**Purpose:** Treaties allocate taxing rights between countries and reduce or eliminate taxes for residents who would otherwise face double taxation.

## Treaty Coverage

The U.S. has treaties with countries including:
- Canada, Mexico, United Kingdom
- Germany, France, Japan, Australia
- India, China, Netherlands, Switzerland
- And many others

**Check current list:** IRS.gov/TreatyTables

**Note:** Some treaties are currently suspended (e.g., U.S.-Russia treaty suspended as of August 2024).

## Common Treaty Benefits

### Personal Service Income
If you're a U.S. resident working temporarily in a treaty country, your income may be exempt from that country's tax if you meet specific requirements:
- Limited time in the country (typically 183 days)
- Services for a non-treaty country employer
- Income not borne by a permanent establishment

### Professors and Teachers
Many treaties provide exemption from host country taxation for teaching income for 2-3 years.

### Students, Trainees, and Apprentices
Grants, scholarships, and limited employment income may be exempt from host country tax.

### Pensions and Annuities
Non-government pensions may be taxable only in your country of residence, or taxed at reduced rates.

### Investment Income
Interest and dividends may be exempt or subject to reduced withholding rates.

### Capital Gains
Many treaties provide exemption for capital gains (except from real property).

### Government Service
Wages from government employment are generally taxable only by the paying government.

## Non-Discrimination Provisions

Most treaties include non-discrimination clauses that protect U.S. citizens residing in treaty countries from being taxed more heavily than citizens of that country.

**This benefit is available even to U.S. citizens who are NOT U.S. residents**—a significant exception to normal treaty rules.

## Saving Clauses

U.S. tax treaties contain "saving clauses" that preserve the U.S. right to tax its own citizens and residents as if the treaty didn't exist.

**Result:** U.S. citizens generally cannot use treaties to reduce their U.S. tax liability.

### Exceptions to Saving Clauses
Certain provisions override the saving clause for U.S. citizens:
- Non-discrimination provisions
- Certain pension provisions
- Social security benefits

Always check the specific treaty's saving clause and exceptions.

## Who Benefits from Treaties

| Situation | Treaty Benefits Available? |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| U.S. citizen living in U.S. | Limited (mostly non-discrimination) |
| U.S. citizen living in treaty country | Yes, from treaty country |
| Foreign resident in U.S. | Yes, from U.S. taxation |
| Foreign resident abroad | Yes, from treaty country |

## Claiming Treaty Benefits

### For U.S. Residents
- File Form 8833 with your tax return to claim treaty-based positions
- Provide required disclosures

### For Foreign Residents
- Provide Form W-8BEN to U.S. payers to claim reduced withholding
- May need to file U.S. tax return to claim refunds

### Residency Certification
Use **Form 8802** to request certification of U.S. residency for claiming treaty benefits from foreign countries.

## Competent Authority Assistance

If you believe actions by the U.S. or a treaty country are causing taxation not intended by the treaty, you can request assistance from the U.S. competent authority.

**When to Request:**
- Double taxation despite treaty provisions
- Treaty interpretation disputes
- Mutual agreement procedure needed

**How to Request:** See IRS.gov/CompetentAuthority for procedures.

## Treaty with Canada (Special Note)

The U.S.-Canada treaty is one of the most frequently used. See Pub 597 for detailed guidance on:
- Pension treatment
- Social security benefits
- Cross-border employment
- Students and teachers

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming all treaties are the same** - Each treaty differs
2. **Not reading the saving clause** - May limit expected benefits
3. **Failing to file Form 8833** when claiming treaty positions
4. **Not certifying residency** when claiming benefits abroad
5. **Missing treaty election deadlines**
