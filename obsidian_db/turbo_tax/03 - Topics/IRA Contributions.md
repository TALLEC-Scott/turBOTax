# IRA Contributions

This topic covers the contribution rules that apply to both Traditional and Roth IRAs, including limits, compensation requirements, deadlines, and special situations.

## Contribution Limits

### Annual Limits (2025)

| Age | Maximum Contribution |
|-----|---------------------|
| Under 50 | $7,000 |
| 50 or older | $8,000 |

### Annual Limits (2026)

| Age | Maximum Contribution |
|-----|---------------------|
| Under 50 | $7,500 |
| 50 or older | $8,600 |

### Key Points
- **Combined limit:** Total contributions to all Traditional and Roth IRAs cannot exceed the limit
- **Per person:** Each spouse has their own limit
- **Per year:** Limit resets each tax year

## What Counts as Compensation

To contribute to an IRA, you must have **taxable compensation**:

### Includes:
- Wages, salaries, tips
- Commissions
- Self-employment income
- Taxable alimony (pre-2019 divorce instruments only)
- Nontaxable combat pay
- Military differential pay
- Taxable non-tuition fellowship/stipend payments (after 2019)

### Does NOT Include:
- Earnings and profits from property
- Interest and dividend income
- Pension or annuity income
- Deferred compensation
- Conservation Reserve Program (CRP) payments
- Foreign earned income exclusion
- Any amounts excluded from income

## Contribution Deadline

- **Regular deadline:** Due date of tax return (April 15, 2026 for 2025)
- **Extensions do NOT apply** - Filing extension does not extend contribution deadline
- **Exception for combat zone:** Extended by 180 days plus any remaining filing period

### Prior Year Contributions
- Can contribute for prior year until April 15
- Must designate which year the contribution is for
- Report on Form 5498 the following May

## Kay Bailey Hutchison Spousal IRA

A working spouse can contribute to a non-working spouse's IRA:

### Requirements
1. Married filing jointly
2. One spouse has compensation
3. Total contributions don't exceed combined compensation

### Limits
Each spouse can contribute up to the annual limit:
- **2025:** Up to $14,000 combined ($16,000 if both 50+)
- **Example:** If spouse earns $50,000, both can contribute $7,000 each

### Special Situations
- If spouse has some compensation, reduce their limit first
- Cannot contribute to spouse's IRA after divorce (but can transfer existing IRA via QDRO)

## Contribution Methods

### Cash Contributions
- Cash, check, or money order
- Property cannot be contributed directly

### Transfer from Tax Refund
- Use Form 8888 to direct refund to IRA
- Must be designated for current tax year

### Rollover Contributions
Different from regular contributions:
- Not subject to annual limits
- Must meet 60-day rule
- Subject to once-per-year rollover rule

## Special Contribution Types

### Catch-Up Contributions (Age 50+)
- Additional $1,000 per year
- Applies to both Traditional and Roth IRAs
- Combined with regular limit

### Qualified Reservist Repayments
Members of reserve components called to active duty can repay qualified reservist distributions:
- Not subject to contribution limits
- Must repay within 2 years of active duty ending
- Reported on Form 8606, Line 1
- Not deductible

### 529 to Roth IRA Rollover (New)
Beginning 2024, beneficiaries can roll over 529 funds to Roth IRA:
- Lifetime maximum: $35,000
- Annual limit: Roth IRA contribution limit
- 529 must be open 15+ years

### Military Death Gratuity
- Death gratuity or SGLI payments can be rolled over to Roth IRA
- Within 1 year of receipt
- Not subject to contribution limits

## Employer-Sponsored IRA Contributions

### SEP-IRA Contributions
- Employer contributions don't count toward your $7,000 limit
- Subject to much higher limits (see [[SEP Plans]])
- You can still make your own Traditional/Roth contribution

### SIMPLE IRA Contributions
- Your salary reduction contributions count toward compensation
- Different limits apply (see [[SIMPLE Plans]])
- Can be Traditional or Roth (after 2022)

## Form 5498 Reporting

Trustee reports contributions annually:
- Box 1: Traditional IRA contributions
- Box 10: Roth IRA contributions
- Box 2: Rollover contributions
- Box 3: SEP contributions
- Box 4: SIMPLE contributions

**Note:** You report contributions on your return (if deductible); Form 5498 is for your records.

## When You Can't Contribute

### No Compensation
- No contribution allowed unless spouse has compensation
- Exception: Nontaxable combat pay counts as compensation

### Exceeds Income Limits (Roth IRA Only)
- Single with MAGI ≥ $165,000 (2025)
- MFJ with MAGI ≥ $246,000 (2025)
- MFS with MAGI ≥ $10,000 and lived with spouse

### Age Issues
- No age limit for Traditional IRA contributions (after 2019)
- No age limit for Roth IRA contributions

## Correcting Contribution Errors

### Excess Contributions
- Withdraw by due date with earnings (no penalty)
- Apply to future year
- Pay 6% excise tax annually until corrected

See [[Excess IRA Contributions]] for details.

### Recharacterization
Change contribution from one type to another:
- Traditional to Roth or vice versa
- Must complete by due date
- Include earnings (or loss)
- Not available for conversions after 2017

See [[IRA Recharacterizations]] for details.

## Planning Strategies

### Front-Loading Contributions
- Contribute early in the year for longer tax-advantaged growth
- January 1 contribution gives 15+ extra months of growth

### Dollar-Cost Averaging
- Regular monthly contributions
- Reduces market timing risk

### Income Management
- Year-end planning to maximize deduction eligibility
- Consider Roth conversions in lower-income years

### Spousal IRA Planning
- Maximize contributions for non-working spouse
- Coordinate with earned income timing
