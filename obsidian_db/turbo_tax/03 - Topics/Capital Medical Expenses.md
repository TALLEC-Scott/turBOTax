# Capital Medical Expenses

## Overview

Capital expenses for medical care—such as home improvements and special equipment—may be deductible as [[Medical and Dental Expenses]] on [[Schedule A (Form 1040)|Schedule A]]. The deduction depends on whether the improvement increases your property's value.

## General Rule

You can include in medical expenses amounts paid for:
- Special equipment installed in a home
- Home improvements
- If their main purpose is medical care for you, your spouse, or your dependent

## Calculating the Deduction

### If Property Value Increases

If the improvement increases your property value, you can deduct only the **cost minus the increase in value**.

| Scenario | Deductible Amount |
|----------|-------------------|
| Cost > Value increase | Cost minus value increase |
| Cost ≤ Value increase | $0 (no deduction) |

### If Property Value Does NOT Increase

If the improvement doesn't increase your property value, the **entire cost** is deductible as a medical expense.

### Worksheet Method

Use this calculation:
1. Enter amount paid for home improvement
2. Enter value of home immediately after improvement
3. Enter value of home immediately before improvement
4. Subtract line 3 from line 2 (increase in value)
5. If line 4 ≥ line 1: No medical expense deduction
6. If line 4 < line 1: Subtract line 4 from line 1 = Deductible amount

## Improvements That Usually Don't Increase Value

Certain improvements to accommodate disabilities typically don't increase home value and are **fully deductible**:

### Qualifying Improvements

- Constructing entrance or exit ramps
- Widening doorways at entrances/exits
- Widening or modifying hallways and interior doorways
- Installing railings, support bars, or bathroom modifications
- Lowering or modifying kitchen cabinets and equipment
- Moving or modifying electrical outlets and fixtures
- Installing porch lifts and other lifts (not elevators—usually add value)
- Modifying fire alarms, smoke detectors, and warning systems
- Modifying stairways
- Adding handrails or grab bars anywhere
- Modifying hardware on doors
- Modifying areas in front of entrance and exit doorways
- Grading ground to provide access to residence

### Requirements

- Must be to accommodate a disabled condition
- Only reasonable costs qualify
- Additional costs for personal motives (architectural, aesthetic) are not medical expenses

## Operation and Upkeep

Amounts paid for operation and upkeep of capital assets qualify as medical expenses if:
- The main reason is medical care
- This applies even if only part of the original cost qualified as medical expense

**Examples of deductible upkeep:**
- Electricity for medical equipment
- Repairs to wheelchair ramps
- Maintenance of special plumbing fixtures

## Examples

### Example 1: Elevator Installation

You install an elevator in your home for medical reasons:
- Cost: $20,000
- Home value increase: $25,000
- **Result:** No deduction (value increase exceeds cost)

### Example 2: Wheelchair Ramp

You construct an entrance ramp for wheelchair access:
- Cost: $8,000
- Home value increase: $0
- **Result:** Full $8,000 deductible as medical expense

### Example 3: Bathroom Modification

You modify a bathroom with grab bars and roll-in shower:
- Cost: $15,000
- Home value increase: $5,000
- **Result:** $10,000 deductible ($15,000 - $5,000)

## Rented Property

Amounts paid to buy and install special plumbing fixtures for a person with a disability in a **rented home** are medical expenses if primarily for medical reasons.

**Example:** You install a first-floor bathroom with shower in your rented two-story house due to arthritis and heart condition. The landlord didn't pay any costs or reduce rent. The entire amount is deductible as medical expenses.

## Car Modifications

### Special Equipment

You can deduct the cost of:
- Special hand controls
- Other special equipment installed for a person with a disability

### Specially Designed Car

You can deduct the difference between:
- Cost of a regular car
- Cost of a car specially designed for wheelchair transport

### Operating Costs

For medical transportation:
- Standard mileage rate: 21 cents per mile (2025)
- Or actual out-of-pocket costs for gas and oil
- Plus parking fees and tolls

**Cannot deduct:** Depreciation, insurance, general repairs, or maintenance.

## Equipment and Devices

Fully deductible medical equipment includes:
- Wheelchairs
- Crutches
- Artificial limbs
- Artificial teeth
- Hearing aids and batteries
- Eyeglasses and contact lenses
- Diagnostic devices (blood sugar monitors)
- Oxygen equipment

## What Is NOT Deductible

- Improvements for general health or comfort
- Aesthetic or architectural upgrades beyond medical necessity
- Elevators (generally add property value)
- Costs of repainting after modifications
- Personal use items in normal form

## Recordkeeping

Keep records of:
- All costs and expenses
- Appraisals of property value before and after improvements
- Medical necessity documentation from physician
- Receipts for equipment and installation
