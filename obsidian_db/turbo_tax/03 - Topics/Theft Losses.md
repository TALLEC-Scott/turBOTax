# Theft Losses

## Overview

A theft is the taking and removing of money or property with the **intent to deprive the owner** of it. The taking must be illegal under state law and done with criminal intent. A conviction for theft is **not required**.

## Types of Theft

Theft includes taking property by:

- Blackmail
- Embezzlement
- Burglary
- Extortion
- Larceny
- Kidnapping for ransom
- Robbery
- Fraud or misrepresentation (if illegal under state/local law)

## Deductibility Rules

### Personal-Use Property (Post-2017)

Theft losses of personal-use property are deductible **only if attributable to a [[Federally Declared Disasters|federally declared disaster]]**.

**Exception**: Theft losses incurred in a **transaction entered into for profit** may still be deductible (e.g., investment fraud, Ponzi schemes).

### Business/Income-Producing Property

Theft losses of business or income-producing property remain deductible regardless of disaster status.

## Special Situations

### Ponzi-Type Investment Schemes

IRS guidance available:
- Revenue Ruling 2009-9
- Revenue Procedure 2009-20 (modified by Revenue Procedure 2011-58)
- Use Form 4684, Section C to determine loss amount

### Financial Scams

Victims of financial scams involving transactions entered into for profit may claim theft loss deduction if:
1. Loss results from criminal conduct classified as theft under state law
2. No reasonable prospect of recovering stolen funds
3. Loss arose from profit-motivated transaction

### Decline in Stock Market Value

**Not deductible as theft loss** - decline in market value of stock due to disclosure of corporate fraud is a capital loss (deductible on Schedule D if stock is sold or becomes worthless).

### Mislaid or Lost Property

Simple disappearance is **not theft**. However, accidental loss may qualify as a [[Casualty Losses|casualty loss]] if from a sudden, unexpected, or unusual event.

## Figuring the Loss

### Amount of Loss
> Loss = Adjusted Basis - FMV after theft (zero) - Reimbursements
>
> For stolen property, FMV after theft = $0

### Recovered Stolen Property

If property is recovered after deducting a theft loss:
1. Refigure the loss using smaller of adjusted basis or decrease in FMV
2. Report difference as income in recovery year (up to amount that reduced tax)

## Proof Required

To deduct a theft loss, be able to show:

- You were the owner of the property
- When you discovered the property was missing
- That the property was stolen
- Whether a reimbursement claim exists with reasonable prospect of recovery

## When to Deduct

Deduct theft losses in the **year you discover the property was stolen**.

If a reimbursement claim exists with reasonable prospect of recovery, wait until you know with reasonable certainty whether reimbursement will be received.
