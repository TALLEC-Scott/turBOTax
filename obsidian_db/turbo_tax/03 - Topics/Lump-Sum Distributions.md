# Lump-Sum Distributions

## Overview

A lump-sum distribution is a payment of your entire balance from a qualified retirement plan within a single tax year. Special tax treatment options may be available for qualifying distributions, potentially reducing your tax burden compared to ordinary income rates.

## What Qualifies as a Lump-Sum Distribution

A lump-sum distribution is the distribution of a plan participant's entire balance from all of the employer's qualified plans of one kind (pension, profit-sharing, or stock bonus plans) within a single tax year.

### Requirements
- Must be the participant's **entire balance**
- From **all** qualified plans of one type with the employer
- Paid within **one tax year**
- Due to one of the following events:
  - Death of the participant
  - Participant reaches age 59½
  - Participant separates from service (employee)
  - Participant becomes totally and permanently disabled (self-employed)

### Not Considered Lump-Sum Distributions
- Partial distributions
- Rollovers
- Required minimum distributions
- Corrective distributions of excess contributions

## Tax Treatment Options

### 1. Regular Taxation (Default)
- Taxed as ordinary income
- Report on Form 1040, lines 5a and 5b
- Simplest method but may result in higher tax

### 2. 10-Year Tax Option (If Qualified)
Special 10-year averaging may reduce taxes for participants born before January 2, 1936:
- Use Form 4972 to calculate
- Applies reduced tax rates to the distribution
- Can only be used once per lifetime after 1986
- Must meet age and plan requirements

### 3. Capital Gain Treatment (If Qualified)
For participants born before January 2, 1936, pre-1974 contributions may qualify for capital gain treatment:
- 20% capital gains rate applies
- Only applies to pre-1974 employer contributions
- Reported on Form 4972

## Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA)

If you receive employer securities in a lump-sum distribution, you may defer tax on the net unrealized appreciation (NUA):

### What is NUA?
NUA is the net increase in the value of employer securities while they were held in the plan.

### Tax Treatment
| Component | Tax Treatment |
|-----------|---------------|
| Your basis in securities | Taxed as ordinary income in year of distribution |
| NUA | Tax-deferred until securities are sold |
| Post-distribution appreciation | Capital gain (short or long-term) when sold |

### Requirements for Full NUA Deferral
Distribution must be a lump-sum and paid due to:
- Death, OR
- Age 59½, OR
- Separation from service

### When Sold
- NUA portion is taxed as long-term capital gain
- Any appreciation after distribution is capital gain based on holding period

## Reporting Lump-Sum Distributions

### Form 1099-R
Box 7 should show code:
- **A** - Lump-sum distribution (participant born before 1936)
- **G** - Direct rollover of lump-sum

### Form 4972
Use Form 4972 to elect:
- 10-year tax averaging
- Capital gain treatment

### Form 1040
- Line 5a: Total lump-sum distribution
- Line 5b: Taxable amount (or 0 if rolled over)

## Rollover Options

You can roll over a lump-sum distribution to:
- Traditional IRA
- Another qualified employer plan
- 403(b) plan
- Governmental 457(b) plan

**Note:** Rolling over forfeits the ability to use 10-year averaging or capital gain treatment.

## Key Rules

1. **Must be entire balance from all plans of one type**
2. **Special treatment only available for those born before January 2, 1936**
3. **NUA rules allow tax deferral on employer securities**
4. **Rollover must be elected within 60 days**
5. **10-year averaging can only be used once after 1986**
