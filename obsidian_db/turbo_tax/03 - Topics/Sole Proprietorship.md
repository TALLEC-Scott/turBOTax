# Sole Proprietorship

## Overview

A sole proprietorship is an unincorporated business owned and operated by one person. Per [[Pub 334 - Tax Guide for Small Business]], it is the simplest and most common form of business organization.

**Key Characteristic:** No legal distinction between the owner and the business.

## Definition

You are a sole proprietor if you:
- Own an unincorporated business by yourself
- Are the sole member of a domestic LLC (unless electing corporate treatment)

**Part-time Business:** Having a part-time business in addition to regular employment counts as self-employment.

## Trade or Business

An activity is a trade or business if:
- Carried on to make a profit
- Profit motive exists (don't need to actually make profit)
- Ongoing efforts to further business interests

## Tax Filing Requirements

### Income Tax
- File [[Form 1040]] with [[Schedule C (Form 1040)]]
- Report business income and expenses
- Net profit taxed as personal income

### Self-Employment Tax
- File [[Schedule SE (Form 1040)]]
- Pay Social Security and Medicare taxes on net earnings
- 15.3% rate (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare)
- Deduct employer portion (50%) on Schedule 1

### Estimated Tax
- Pay quarterly using [[Form 1040-ES]]
- Required if expecting to owe $1,000+
- Due: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15

## Advantages of Sole Proprietorship

| Advantage | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Simplicity | Minimal legal formalities to start |
| Control | Full decision-making authority |
| Tax simplicity | Single tax return (no separate business return) |
| Flexibility | Easy to change or close |
| Losses | Can offset other income (subject to limitations) |

## Disadvantages of Sole Proprietorship

| Disadvantage | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| Unlimited liability | Personal assets at risk for business debts |
| Self-employment tax | Pay full 15.3% SE tax |
| Limited capital | Harder to raise funds |
| Limited continuity | Business ends at owner's death |
| No deductible salary | Cannot deduct owner's compensation |

## Limited Liability Company (LLC)

### Single-Member LLC
- Treated as sole proprietorship for tax purposes
- May elect corporate treatment by filing Form 8832
- Liability protection under state law

### LLC with Corporate Election
- Files Form 1120 or 1120-S
- No longer sole proprietorship for tax purposes
- Separate entity for employment tax purposes

## Business Owned by Spouses

### Community Property States
States: Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin

In community property states, spouses can treat a jointly-owned business as:
- Sole proprietorship (report on one Schedule C), OR
- Partnership (file [[Form 1065]])

### Qualified Joint Venture (QJV)

If spouses jointly own and operate a business:
- May elect QJV treatment instead of partnership
- Each files separate Schedule C and Schedule SE
- Each spouse gets Social Security credit
- Must materially participate

**QJV Requirements:**
- Only members are spouses
- Both materially participate
- File joint return
- Not operated in name of state law entity (no LLC)

See [[Qualified Joint Venture]] for details.

## Statutory Employees

Some workers are "statutory employees" by law:
- Box 13 checked on Form W-2
- Report wages and expenses on Schedule C
- Not subject to SE tax on wages (FICA already withheld)

Examples:
- Corporate officers
- Agent drivers/commission drivers
- Full-time life insurance agents
- Home workers

## Identification Numbers

### Social Security Number (SSN)
- Used if no employees and no pension/excise taxes
- Report on Schedule C

### Employer Identification Number (EIN)
Required if you:
- Have employees
- File pension or excise tax returns
- Have a Keogh plan

Apply for EIN at IRS.gov/EIN or via [[Form SS-4]]

## Recordkeeping Requirements

Per [[Pub 583 - Starting a Business and Keeping Records]]:
- Track all income and expenses
- Keep supporting documents (receipts, invoices)
- Maintain records for 3+ years
- Track assets for depreciation

## Going Out of Business

When closing a sole proprietorship:
- File final Schedule C with Form 1040
- Report disposition of business property on [[Form 4797]]
- File final employment tax returns (if applicable)
- Issue final W-2s and 1099s
- May need [[Form 8594]] if selling business assets

## Common Questions

### Q: Can I be a sole proprietor with another job?
**A:** Yes. A part-time business counts as self-employment. Report on Schedule C regardless of whether you also have W-2 wages.

### Q: Do I need a separate business bank account?
**A:** Not legally required, but highly recommended. Keeps business and personal finances separate, simplifies recordkeeping.

### Q: Can I deduct my salary?
**A:** No. As a sole proprietor, you are not an employee. You pay tax on net profit, regardless of how much you withdraw.
