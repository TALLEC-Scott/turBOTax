# Last-Month Rule

## Overview

The Last-Month Rule allows an individual who is eligible for an HSA on December 1 to be treated as eligible for the entire calendar year for contribution limit purposes. This rule provides significant tax planning opportunities but comes with a testing period requirement. See [[Pub 969 - Health Savings Accounts and Other Tax-Favored Health Plans]].

## How the Rule Works

If you are an eligible individual on the first day of the **last month of your tax year** (December 1 for most taxpayers):
- You are considered eligible for the **entire year**
- You are treated as having the same HDHP coverage for the entire year
- You can contribute the full annual limit based on your December 1 coverage

## Example 1: Late-Year Eligibility

**Situation:** You become an eligible individual on December 1, 2025, with family HDHP coverage.

**Result:**
- You can contribute the full family limit ($8,550 for 2025)
- Treated as eligible all 12 months
- Subject to testing period

## Example 2: Coverage Change During Year

**Situation:** You have self-only HDHP coverage January-October 2025, then change to family coverage November-December 2025.

**Result:**
- Because you have family coverage on December 1, 2025
- Your contribution limit is $8,550 (family limit)
- Must use worksheet to calculate actual limit (may be more favorable than last-month rule)

## Testing Period Requirement

When you use the last-month rule, you must remain an eligible individual during the **testing period**:

### Testing Period Duration
- Begins: Last month of your tax year (December 1 for most)
- Ends: Last day of the 12th month following that month (December 31 of following year)

**Example:** For 2025 tax year, testing period is December 1, 2025 through December 31, 2026.

## Failure to Complete Testing Period

If you fail to remain an eligible individual during the testing period (for reasons other than death or disability):

### Tax Consequences
1. **Include in income** the contributions that would not have been made but for the last-month rule
2. **Pay 10% additional tax** on the income amount
3. Report on Form 8889 for the year you fail to be eligible

### Calculation Method
Use the worksheet in Form 8889 instructions to determine the amount to include in income.

## Example: Testing Period Failure

**Situation:** Age 53, become eligible December 1, 2025, with family HDHP coverage. Contribute $8,550 for 2025. Fail to be eligible in June 2026.

**Calculation:**
| Month | Monthly Limit |
|-------|---------------|
| January-November | $0 |
| December | $8,550 |
| **Total** | $8,550 |
| **Limitation (÷ 12)** | $712.50 |

**Income to Report:** $8,550 - $712.50 = **$7,837.50** (plus 10% additional tax)

## Alternative: General Rule Calculation

If you don't qualify for or don't want to use the last-month rule, calculate your limit using the **greater of**:

1. Monthly limitation using Form 8889 worksheet, OR
2. Maximum annual contribution based on coverage on December 1

**Tip:** If coverage changed during year, the worksheet method may yield a higher limit than the last-month rule.

## Special Rules

### Multiple HSAs
If you have more than one HSA, total contributions to all HSAs cannot exceed the limit.

### Qualified HSA Funding Distribution
Has its own separate testing period. Each funding distribution has its own testing period if multiple distributions occur due to coverage change.

### Changing Coverage Mid-Year
If you change from self-only to family coverage during the year and use the last-month rule:
- Use the family limit based on December 1 coverage
- The monthly calculation method may be more favorable

## Strategies

### Maximizing Contributions
If you know you'll be eligible through the testing period:
- Use last-month rule to maximize contribution
- Contribute full annual limit even if only eligible late in year

### Risk Management
If uncertain about future eligibility:
- Consider using monthly limitation method
- Avoid potential income inclusion and penalty
