# Inherited Property Basis

## Overview

When property is inherited, the beneficiary generally receives a "step-up" (or step-down) in basis to the property's fair market value (FMV) on the date of the decedent's death. This can significantly reduce or increase capital gains tax on later sales.

## General Rule: Step-Up in Basis

### Basis Equals FMV
The basis of inherited property is generally:
- **Fair market value on date of death**, OR
- **Fair market value on alternate valuation date** (if elected by executor)
- **Special-use valuation** for qualifying farm/business property

### Why It Matters
- **Step-up**: If FMV > decedent's basis, beneficiary gets higher basis
- **Step-down**: If FMV < decedent's basis, beneficiary gets lower basis
- Eliminates (or reduces) capital gains on appreciation during decedent's life
- Can create capital loss if property declined in value

## Valuation Dates

### Date of Death Valuation
- Default rule
- FMV as of date decedent died

### Alternate Valuation Date
- 6 months after date of death
- Only if elected by executor on estate tax return
- Property must not have been sold or distributed within 6 months
- Used to reduce estate tax if values have declined

### Property Sold Within 6 Months
- FMV on date of sale (if alternate valuation elected)
- Must be sold by estate, not distributed and then sold

## Special-Use Valuation

### Eligible Property
- Real property used in farming or closely held business
- Must meet specific requirements
- Value based on actual use rather than FMV

### Qualified Heirs
- Ancestors (parents, grandparents)
- Spouse
- Lineal descendants (children, grandchildren)
- Spouses of lineal descendants

### Recapture Rules
- Additional estate tax if property disposed of within 10 years
- Heir can elect to increase basis to FMV if paying recapture tax

## Basis Rules for Specific Situations

### Joint Tenancy Property

**Non-Spouse Joint Tenants**
- Original basis in surviving tenant's share
- FMV of decedent's share added to basis
- Subtract depreciation allowed to surviving tenant

**Example**: A. Maple and F. Maple owned rental property as joint tenants
- Original basis: $15,000 (A's contribution)
- FMV at death: $100,000
- A's new basis = $15,000 + $75,000 (F's share) - $10,000 (depreciation) = $80,000

### Qualified Joint Interest (Spouses Only)

**Tenancy by the Entirety / Joint Tenancy with Spouse**
- One-half value included in gross estate
- One-half cost basis + one-half FMV at death
- Subtract one-half depreciation

### Community Property States

In community property states:
- One-half of community property included in decedent's estate
- **Both halves get step-up** in basis to FMV
- Major advantage over common law states

### S Corporation Stock

- Basis stepped up at death
- Must reduce basis for any IRD attributable to stock
- IRD = undistributed income not yet taxed

## Exceptions to Step-Up

### Appreciated Property Reacquired Within 1 Year
If you give appreciated property to someone who dies within 1 year and you inherit it back:
- Your basis = decedent's basis (no step-up)
- Prevents "deathbed gift" tax avoidance

### Income in Respect of Decedent (IRD)
- IRD items do NOT get step-up in basis
- IRD is taxable to recipient when received
- Examples: IRA distributions, unpaid wages, accounts receivable

## Depreciation of Inherited Property

### MACRS Required
- Must use Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System
- Apply to inherited portion of basis
- Original basis continues under prior depreciation method

### Two Depreciation Calculations
For joint interests:
1. Continue depreciating original basis under prior method
2. Depreciate inherited portion using MACRS

## Holding Period

### Automatic Long-Term Treatment
- Inherited property is always treated as held long-term
- Even if held for one day after inheritance
- Applies regardless of decedent's holding period

## Consistent Basis Reporting

### Form 8971 Requirement
Executors must report to beneficiaries:
- Property received from estate
- Estate tax value reported on Form 706
- Beneficiary must use consistent basis

### Beneficiary's Obligation
If Form 8971, Schedule A shows property increases estate tax:
- Must use reported value as basis
- Adjust for allowed items
- Consistent reporting required on subsequent sale

## Valuation Misstatement Penalties

| Type | Return Type | Threshold |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Substantial | Income tax | 150% of correct value |
| Gross | Income tax | 200% of correct value |
| Substantial | Estate/Gift | 65% or less of actual value |
| Gross | Estate/Gift | 40% or less of actual value |

Penalty: 20% of underpayment if exceeds $5,000
