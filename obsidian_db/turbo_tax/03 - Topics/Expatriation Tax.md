# Expatriation Tax

## Overview

The expatriation tax applies to certain U.S. citizens and long-term residents who give up their citizenship or residency. These individuals may be subject to tax on unrealized gains and other special rules.

## Who Is Subject to Expatriation Tax

### Covered Expatriates

You are a covered expatriate if you meet ANY of these criteria at expatriation:

| Test | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Tax liability test** | Average annual net income tax > $201,000 (2025) for prior 5 years |
| **Net worth test** | Net worth ≥ $2 million on date of expatriation |
| **Compliance test** | Failed to certify 5-year tax compliance on Form 8854 |

### Long-Term Resident (LTR) Definition

A long-term resident is a lawful permanent resident (green card holder) who was a resident alien for at least **8 of the 15 tax years** ending with the year of status change.

**Note:** Treaty tiebreaker elections may affect this counting.

## Exit Tax (Section 877A)

### Mark-to-Market Rules

Covered expatriates are treated as selling all worldwide assets at fair market value on the day before expatriation.

**Key Points:**
- Applies to most assets
- Unrealized gains are taxed
- First $969,000 (2025) of net gain is excluded
- Losses can offset gains (with limitations)

### Deferred Tax Assets

Certain items receive special treatment:
- Eligible deferred compensation items
- Specified tax deferred accounts
- Non-grantor trusts

**Options:** Elect to defer tax or take immediate taxation with waiver of treaty benefits.

### Ineligible Assets

Not subject to mark-to-market:
- Eligible deferred compensation (if deferral elected)
- Specified tax deferred accounts
- Nongrantor trust interests

## Gifts and Bequests from Covered Expatriates

### Section 2801 Tax

U.S. citizens and residents who receive gifts or bequests from covered expatriates are subject to tax:

| Gift Type | Tax Rate |
|-----------|----------|
| Direct gifts | Highest gift tax rate (40%) |
| Bequests | Highest estate tax rate (40%) |
| Trust distributions | Highest rate on distributions |

**Note:** This is a tax on the **recipient**, not the covered expatriate.

### Exceptions
- Gifts/bequests to charity
- Gifts to spouse (subject to gift tax rules)
- Amounts otherwise subject to estate tax

### Reporting
Recipients must file Form 708 to report and pay the tax.

## Expatriation Date

| Status Type | Expatriation Date |
|-------------|-------------------|
| U.S. citizen | Date of relinquishing citizenship |
| Long-term resident | Earlier of: residency termination date OR date of abandonment |

### Residency Termination Date
For LTRs who expatriate after June 16, 2008:
- Last day of calendar quarter in which expatriation occurs
- Earlier if treaty tiebreaker applies

## Form 8854 Requirements

### Initial Filing
Covered expatriates must file [[Form 8854]] for the year of expatriation:

**Information Required:**
- 5-year tax compliance certification
- Asset and liability information
- Exit tax calculations
- Deferred asset elections

**Due Date:** With tax return for year of expatriation.

### Annual Filing
If deferred tax election made, must file Form 8854 annually to report deferred tax items.

## Pre-June 17, 2008 Expatriates

For expatriations before June 17, 2008, different rules apply. See 2018 Instructions for Form 8854 and 2018 Pub. 519 for historical rules.

**Note:** Pre-2008 rules are no longer covered in current publications.

## Relinquishing Citizenship

### Ways to Relinquish
1. Naturalization in foreign country with intent to relinquish
2. Oath of allegiance to foreign state
3. Serving in foreign military under foreign government
4. Formal renunciation at U.S. embassy
5. Determination by U.S. government of expatriating act

### Formal Renunciation
Most common method for tax planning. Process:
1. Appear at U.S. embassy/consulate
2. Sign oath of renunciation
3. Pay $2,350 fee
4. Receive Certificate of Loss of Nationality

## Abandoning Green Card

### Process
1. File Form I-407 with USCIS, OR
2. Submit written statement of intent to abandon status
3. Surrender Permanent Resident Card

**Caution:** Status is not abandoned until USCIS receives and processes the documentation. Mail by certified mail with return receipt.

## Re-Entry Considerations

Covered expatriates who return to the U.S. may face:
- Visa complications
- Continued tax obligations on U.S. source income
- Possible enhanced scrutiny at border
