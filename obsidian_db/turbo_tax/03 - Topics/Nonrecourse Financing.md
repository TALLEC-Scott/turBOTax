# Nonrecourse Financing

## Overview

Nonrecourse financing is debt for which the borrower is **not personally liable** for repayment. If you default, the lender's only recourse is to foreclose on the property securing the loan.

This has significant implications for the [[At-Risk Rules]] and loss deductions.

## Definition

A loan is nonrecourse if:
- You are **not personally liable** for repayment
- The lender's only recourse is to:
  - Your interest in the activity, OR
  - The property used in the activity

## At-Risk Treatment

### General Rule
You are **NOT considered at risk** for your share of any nonrecourse loan used to:
- Finance an activity
- Acquire property used in the activity

This means nonrecourse debt does NOT increase your at-risk amount, limiting your ability to deduct losses.

### Exception: Secured by Property Not Used in Activity
You ARE considered at risk for nonrecourse financing if:
- The loan is secured by property NOT used in the activity
- Amount at risk = Net fair market value of your interest in the pledged property

### Exception: Qualified Nonrecourse Financing
You ARE considered at risk for [[Qualified Nonrecourse Financing]] secured by real property used in an activity of holding real property.

## Impact on Loss Deductions

Since nonrecourse financing generally doesn't increase your at-risk amount:

1. Losses may be limited under the at-risk rules
2. Suspended losses may carry forward
3. If at-risk amount goes negative, recapture may apply

## Example

You contribute $50,000 cash to a real estate partnership and borrow $200,000 nonrecourse to buy property.

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Cash contribution | $50,000 |
| Nonrecourse loan | $200,000 |
| Amount at risk | $50,000 |

If the property generates a $75,000 loss:
- Only $50,000 is currently deductible
- $25,000 is suspended and carried forward

## Types of Nonrecourse Loans

### Traditional Nonrecourse
- Lender can only go after the collateral
- Common in commercial real estate lending
- Borrower has no personal liability

### "Bad Boy" Carve-Outs
Some "nonrecourse" loans have carve-outs that trigger personal liability for:
- Fraud
- Misappropriation of funds
- Bankruptcy filings
- Other "bad acts"

These may affect at-risk analysis.

## Coordination with Basis Rules

Nonrecourse financing affects both:
1. **At-risk amount** - Generally not included (with exceptions)
2. **Basis** - IS included in basis for depreciation and gain/loss calculations

This can create situations where:
- You have basis to deduct losses
- But those losses are suspended due to at-risk limitations
