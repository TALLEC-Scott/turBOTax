# Early Distribution Tax

## Overview

The early distribution tax is an additional 10% tax on distributions from retirement plans made before age 59½. This penalty applies in addition to regular income tax on the distribution. Understanding the exceptions can help you avoid this penalty.

## General Rule

A 10% additional tax applies to the taxable amount of early distributions from:
- [[401(k) Plans]]
- [[403(b) Plans]]
- [[Section 457 Plans]]
- [[IRAs]]
- Qualified pension and profit-sharing plans

### When It Applies
- Distribution received before age 59½
- No exception applies
- Distribution is included in income

### When It Does NOT Apply
- Age 59½ or older at time of distribution
- Exception applies (see below)
- Distribution is not includible in income

## General Exceptions

The following exceptions apply to all retirement plans:

1. **Death** - Distributions made to beneficiaries after the participant's death
2. **Disability** - You are totally and permanently disabled
3. **Terminal Illness** - Certified by physician that death expected within 84 months
4. **Substantially Equal Payments** - Series of substantially equal periodic payments for life or life expectancy

### Substantially Equal Periodic Payment Methods
- Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) method
- Fixed amortization method
- Fixed annuitization method

## Additional Exceptions for Qualified Plans

These exceptions apply only to employer-sponsored plans (not IRAs):

| Exception | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Separation from service after age 55 | If you leave your job in or after the year you turn 55 |
| Public safety employees | Age 50 or 25 years of service for qualified public safety employees |
| QDRO | Distribution to alternate payee under Qualified Domestic Relations Order |
| Medical expenses | Deductible medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI |
| IRS levy | Distribution due to IRS levy of the plan |
| Qualified reservist | Ordered to active duty for more than 179 days |
| Birth or adoption | Up to $5,000 for qualified birth or adoption |
| Domestic abuse | Up to $10,300 for victims of domestic abuse |
| Emergency expenses | Up to $1,000 for emergency personal expenses |

## Additional Exceptions for IRAs

These exceptions apply only to IRAs:

- Unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI
- Health insurance premiums while unemployed
- Disability
- Death
- Higher education expenses
- First-time homebuyer (up to $10,000 lifetime)
- Birth or adoption distribution

## Special Rules

### In-Plan Roth Rollovers
A separate 5-year period applies for determining whether the early distribution tax applies to amounts allocable to an in-plan Roth rollover.

### Recapture Tax
If you modify substantially equal periodic payments before age 59½, a recapture tax applies:
- Amount that would have been imposed had exception not applied
- Plus interest for the deferral period
- Report on Form 5329, line 4

## Reporting the Tax

### Form 5329
Use Form 5329 to report and calculate the additional tax:
- Line 1: Early distributions included in income
- Line 2: Amount excepted from tax
- Line 3: Amount subject to tax
- Line 4: Recapture tax

### Form 1099-R Codes
| Code | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Early distribution, no known exception |
| 2 | Early distribution, exception applies |
| 3 | Disability |
| 4 | Death |

If code 2, 3, or 4 appears in box 7, the payer determined an exception applies.

## Key Rules

1. **10% additional tax applies to early distributions before age 59½**
2. **Many exceptions exist for qualified plans and IRAs**
3. **File Form 5329 if code 1 appears but an exception applies**
4. **Recapture tax may apply if you modify substantially equal payments**
5. **Separate rules apply to designated Roth accounts**
