# Grandfathered Debt

## Overview

Grandfathered debt is a mortgage you took out on or before October 13, 1987. These mortgages receive special treatment under the tax law because they predate the current mortgage interest deduction limits.

## Key Characteristics

### Unlimited Deduction

All interest paid on grandfathered debt is **fully deductible** as home mortgage interest. There is no dollar limit on the amount of grandfathered debt.

### Reduces Home Acquisition Debt Limit

The amount of your grandfathered debt reduces (but not below zero) the limit for [[Home Acquisition Debt]].

## Qualification Requirements

To qualify as grandfathered debt, the mortgage must have been:

1. **Secured by your qualified home** on October 13, 1987
2. **Secured at all times after** October 13, 1987

How you used the proceeds doesn't matter for grandfathered debt qualification.

## Refinanced Grandfathered Debt

If you refinanced grandfathered debt after October 13, 1987:

### Same Amount or Less

If the refinanced amount wasn't more than the mortgage principal left on the debt, you still treat it as grandfathered debt.

### Amount Exceeds Old Principal

The amount exceeding the old principal is treated as [[Home Acquisition Debt]] (if proceeds were used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home). The mortgage becomes a mixed-use mortgage.

### Term Limitation

Refinanced grandfathered debt is treated as grandfathered debt only for the term left on the debt that was refinanced. After that, it's treated as home acquisition debt.

#### Balloon Note Exception

If the original debt was like a balloon note (principal not amortized over the term), the refinanced debt is treated as grandfathered debt for the entire term of the first refinancing (maximum 30 years).

**Example**: You took out a $200,000 first mortgage in 1986 as a 10-year balloon note (entire balance due in 1996). You refinanced in 1996 with a new 30-year mortgage. The refinanced debt is treated as grandfathered debt for its entire 30-year term.

## Exception: Tax-Exempt Securities

You CANNOT deduct home mortgage interest on grandfathered debt if you used the mortgage proceeds to buy securities or certificates that produce tax-free income.

## Mixed-Use Mortgages

If you have a mixed-use mortgage containing both grandfathered debt and other categories:

1. Figure the balance of grandfathered debt for each month
2. Principal payments are applied in this order:
   - First: Home equity debt (not used for home)
   - Second: Grandfathered debt
   - Third: Home acquisition debt
3. Add monthly balances and divide by 12 for average balance
