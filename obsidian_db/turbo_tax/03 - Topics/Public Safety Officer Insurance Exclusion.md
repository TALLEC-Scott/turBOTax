# Public Safety Officer Insurance Exclusion

## Overview

Eligible retired public safety officers can exclude from income up to $3,000 of retirement plan distributions used to pay health or long-term care insurance premiums. This exclusion reduces your taxable income while helping cover essential insurance costs.

## Eligibility Requirements

### Who Qualifies
You must be an **eligible retired public safety officer** who:
- Retired because of disability, OR
- Retired because you reached normal retirement age

### Definition of Public Safety Officer
A public safety officer is someone who served as:
- Law enforcement officer
- Firefighter
- Chaplain
- Rescue squad member
- Ambulance crew member

### Qualified Public Safety Employees (for Early Distribution Exception)
The definition also includes for the age 50/25-year exception:
- Federal law enforcement officers
- Federal firefighters
- Federal customs and border protection officers
- Air traffic controllers
- Members of the U.S. Capitol Police
- Nuclear materials couriers
- Members of the Supreme Court Police
- Diplomatic security special agents

## Eligible Retirement Plans

The distribution must be from one of the following **governmental plans**:
- Qualified trust (pension plan)
- Section 403(b) annuity
- Section 403(a) plan
- Section 457(b) plan

**Note:** The plan must be maintained by the employer from which you retired as a public safety officer.

## Exclusion Rules

### What Can Be Excluded
- Premiums for accident or health plan coverage
- Premiums for qualified long-term care insurance
- Coverage for you, your spouse, or your dependents

### Exclusion Limit
- **Maximum $3,000 per year**
- Limited to the lesser of premiums paid or $3,000

### Payment Methods
The exclusion applies whether premiums are:
- Paid directly from the plan to the insurance provider, OR
- Distributed to you and then paid to the insurance provider

## How to Claim the Exclusion

### On Form 1040
1. Report total pension distribution on **line 5a**
2. Report taxable amount (reduced by exclusion) on **line 5b**
3. Check the "PSO" box on **line 5c**

### If Reporting Disability Pension
If reporting disability pension on line 1h:
- Include only the taxable amount on that line
- Enter "PSO" and the excluded amount on the dotted line

## Important Limitations

1. **Cannot claim medical expense deduction** for the excluded amount
2. **Only for amounts that would otherwise be taxable**
3. **Must be from governmental plan of former employer**
4. **Box 2a of Form 1099-R does not reflect this exclusion**

## Example

John is a retired firefighter receiving a $30,000 annual pension. He pays $2,400/year for health insurance premiums through his former employer's plan.

- Total pension: $30,000
- Exclusion amount: $2,400 (less than $3,000 limit)
- Taxable pension: $27,600

John reports $30,000 on line 5a, $27,600 on line 5b, and checks the PSO box on line 5c.

## Direct Payment Repeal (Effective 2023)

For distributions made after December 29, 2022, the direct payment requirement was repealed. This means:
- Previously, premiums had to be paid directly from plan to insurer
- Now, distributions made to the officer to pay premiums also qualify
- Provides more flexibility in how payments are made

## Key Rules

1. **Maximum $3,000 exclusion per year**
2. **Must be retired public safety officer**
3. **Plan must be governmental and from former employer**
4. **Cannot also claim medical expense deduction**
5. **Check PSO box on line 5c of Form 1040**
