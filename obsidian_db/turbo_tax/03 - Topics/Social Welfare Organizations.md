# Social Welfare Organizations

## Overview

Social welfare organizations are exempt under Section 501(c)(4). They are organizations that operate exclusively to promote social welfare, including civic leagues and organizations operated exclusively for the promotion of social welfare.

## Definition

A social welfare organization is an organization:
- That is operated exclusively for the promotion of social welfare
- Whose net earnings do not inure to private shareholders or individuals
- That is not organized for profit

## Promoting Social Welfare

### Qualifying Activities
- Improving community conditions
- Lessening neighborhood tensions
- Eliminating prejudice and discrimination
- Defending human and civil rights
- Combating community deterioration
- Environmental protection and conservation

### Examples
- Community associations improving public services
- Organizations encouraging industrial development
- Volunteer fire departments and rescue squads
- Civic leagues promoting community welfare
- Organizations preserving community traditions

## Political Activity

### Key Advantage Over 501(c)(3)
- May engage in political campaign activity
- Primary purpose must still be social welfare
- Political activity cannot be primary purpose

### Limits
- Primary purpose test: Less than 50% of activities can be political
- No contribution limits for candidates (but must report)
- Lobbying can be substantial without limit

### Notification Requirements
Must notify members of nondeductibility of dues used for political activities.

## Application Process

### Form 1024
File Form 1024 to apply for recognition:
- Not required for exemption (self-declaration possible)
- Required for IRS determination letter
- Must file within 27 months of formation for retroactive recognition

### Alternatives
- May operate without determination letter
- May not be required to file Form 1024
- Must still meet operational requirements

## Contributions

### Not Deductible
Contributions to 501(c)(4) organizations are NOT deductible as charitable contributions.

### Exception for Government Entities
Contributions for exclusively public purposes to government entities described in Section 170(c)(1) may be deductible.

### Business Expense Deduction
Dues and contributions may be deductible as business expenses if:
- Ordinary and necessary business expense
- Not for political or lobbying activities

## Filing Requirements

### Form 990
Must file annual information return:
- Form 990 for larger organizations
- Form 990-EZ for mid-sized organizations
- Form 990-N for small organizations (≤$50,000)

### Form 990-T
Must file if unrelated business income exceeds $1,000.

### Form 1120-POL
May be required if political organization income exists.

## Difference from 501(c)(3)

| Feature | 501(c)(4) | 501(c)(3) |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Political activity | Permitted (not primary) | Prohibited |
| Lobbying | Unlimited | Limited |
| Contribution deduction | Not deductible | Generally deductible |
| Application required | No (optional) | Yes |
| Private inurement | Prohibited | Prohibited |

## Donor Disclosure

Unlike 501(c)(3) organizations, 501(c)(4) organizations generally do not have to disclose contributor names to the IRS (except on Form 990-T for unrelated business income).

## Related Organizations

- May establish related 501(c)(3) for charitable activities
- May establish related political organization (Section 527)
- Related organizations may have different rules
