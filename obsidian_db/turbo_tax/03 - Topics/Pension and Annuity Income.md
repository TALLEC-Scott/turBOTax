# Pension and Annuity Income

## Overview

Pension and annuity income represents payments received from retirement plans after you stop working. The tax treatment depends on whether payments are periodic (regular payments over time) or nonperiodic (lump-sum distributions). Understanding the taxation rules helps you properly report this income and minimize taxes.

## Types of Pension and Annuity Payments

### Pension
A pension is a series of definitely determinable payments made after you retire from work. Payments are made regularly and are based on factors such as years of service and prior compensation.

### Annuity
An annuity is a series of payments under a contract made at regular intervals over a period of more than 1 full year. Annuities can be:
- **Fixed** - You receive a definite amount
- **Variable** - Payments vary based on investment results

### Types of Annuities
- **Fixed-period annuities** - Payments for a specified length of time
- **Single life annuities** - Payments for life, ending at death
- **Joint and survivor annuities** - Payments continue to a second annuitant after the first dies

## Taxation of Periodic Payments

### Simplified Method
For most qualified plans, use the Simplified Method to figure the tax-free part of each payment:
1. Determine your cost (investment in the contract)
2. Find your expected return using IRS tables based on age
3. Divide cost by expected number of payments
4. The result is your tax-free amount per payment

### General Rule
Used for nonqualified plans and contracts with annuity starting dates before November 19, 1996. See [[Pub 939 - General Rule for Pensions and Annuities]] for details.

### Cost (Investment in the Contract)
Your cost is generally your net investment in the plan as of your annuity starting date. It includes:
- After-tax contributions you made
- Contributions your employer made that were taxable to you
- Doesn't include pre-tax contributions

## Fully vs. Partly Taxable Payments

### Fully Taxable Payments
Payments are fully taxable if:
- You didn't contribute to the cost of the pension
- You recovered your entire cost tax-free in prior years

### Partly Taxable Payments
If you contributed to your pension, part of each payment is tax-free (return of your investment) and part is taxable (return of earnings and employer contributions).

## Key Rules

1. **Report periodic payments on Form 1040 lines 5a and 5b**
2. **You recover your cost tax-free over your expected payment period**
3. **The Simplified Method worksheet is in the Form 1040 instructions**
4. **Variable annuities follow the same tax rules as fixed annuities**
5. **Net Investment Income Tax may apply to nonqualified annuities**

## Common Questions

### Q: How do I know if my pension is taxable?
**A:** Your pension is taxable to the extent it exceeds your investment in the contract. If you made no after-tax contributions, the entire pension is taxable.

### Q: What if I contributed after-tax dollars to my pension?
**A:** Part of each payment is a tax-free return of your investment. Use the Simplified Method to calculate the tax-free portion.
