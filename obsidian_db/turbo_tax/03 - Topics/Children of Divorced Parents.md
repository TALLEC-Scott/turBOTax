# Children of Divorced Parents

## Overview

Special rules determine which parent can claim a child as a [[Qualifying Child]] when parents are divorced, legally separated, or live apart. Generally, the custodial parent claims the child, but exceptions exist.

## Custodial vs. Noncustodial Parent

### Custodial Parent
The parent with whom the child lived for the **greater number of nights** during the year.

### Noncustodial Parent
The other parent.

### Tiebreaker for Equal Nights
If the child lived with each parent for an equal number of nights:
- The parent with the **higher adjusted gross income (AGI)** is the custodial parent

## Default Rule

Because of the residency test, a child of divorced or separated parents is generally the [[Qualifying Child]] of the **custodial parent**.

## Exception: Noncustodial Parent Can Claim Child

The child is treated as the qualifying child of the **noncustodial parent** if ALL four conditions are met:

1. **Parents are:**
   - Divorced or legally separated under a decree of divorce or separate maintenance, OR
   - Separated under a written separation agreement, OR
   - Lived apart at all times during the last 6 months of the year

2. **Child received over half of support from parents**

3. **Child was in custody of one or both parents for more than half the year**

4. **Written declaration or decree:**
   - Custodial parent signs Form 8332 (or similar statement) releasing the exemption claim, OR
   - Pre-1985 decree states noncustodial parent can claim child and provides at least $600 support

## Form 8332 Requirements

The custodial parent must provide a written declaration to release the claim:
- Use [[Form 8332]] - Release/Revocation of Release of Claim to Exemption for Child by Custodial Parent
- Or a similar statement containing the same information
- Must specify the year(s) for which the release applies
- Can be for one year, multiple years, or all future years

**Noncustodial parent must attach the form to their tax return each year.**

### Post-2008 Divorce Decrees
If the decree/agreement was executed after 2008, you CANNOT attach pages from the decree instead of Form 8332. You must have a signed Form 8332 or similar statement.

### 1985-2008 Divorce Decrees
For decrees executed after 1984 but before 2009, you may attach pages from the decree if it contains all required statements.

## What the Release Allows

The release allows the noncustodial parent to claim:
- [[Child Tax Credit]]
- [[Credit for Other Dependents]]
- Additional child tax credit

**The release does NOT allow the noncustodial parent to claim:**
- [[Head of Household]] filing status
- [[Child and Dependent Care Credit]]
- Exclusion for dependent care benefits
- [[Earned Income Credit]]

These benefits remain with the custodial parent (if otherwise eligible).

## Tiebreaker Rules for Multiple Claimants

If a child is a qualifying child of more than one person, only one can claim the child for:
- Child tax credit
- Head of household filing status
- Child and dependent care credit
- Exclusion for dependent care benefits
- Earned income credit

### Tiebreaker Priority
1. If only one is the parent → The parent
2. If parents file jointly → The parents together
3. If parents don't file jointly but both claim → Parent with whom child lived longer
4. If equal time and parents don't file jointly → Parent with higher AGI
5. If no parent can claim → Person with highest AGI

## Special Situations

### Emancipated Child
An emancipated child is treated as not living with either parent. The special rules for divorced parents don't apply.

### Kidnapped Child
May still qualify as a dependent if certain conditions are met.

### Parent Works at Night
If a child lives more days (but not nights) with a parent who works nights, that parent is treated as the custodial parent.

## Revocation of Release

The custodial parent can revoke a previously signed release:
- Must give (or make reasonable efforts to give) written notice to noncustodial parent
- Must attach a copy of the revocation to their return

## Example

You and your ex-spouse divorced in 2020. Your child lived with you for 210 nights and with your ex-spouse for 155 nights in 2025. You are the custodial parent.

You sign Form 8332 releasing the exemption claim to your ex-spouse for 2025. Your ex-spouse:
- CAN claim: Child tax credit, credit for other dependents
- CANNOT claim: Head of household, earned income credit, dependent care credit

You (the custodial parent) may still claim head of household (if otherwise eligible) and earned income credit.
