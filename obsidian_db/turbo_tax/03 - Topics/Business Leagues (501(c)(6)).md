# Business Leagues (501(c)(6))

## Overview

Business leagues are exempt under Section 501(c)(6). They include associations of persons having a common business interest, where the purpose is to promote that common interest rather than to engage in a regular business for profit.

## Definition

A business league is an organization that:
- Is an association of persons with common business interest
- Has purpose to promote common interest
- Does not engage in regular business for profit
- Does not allow net earnings to inure to private benefit
- Is primarily engaged in activities that are basis for exemption
- Is primarily supported by membership dues

## Types of Organizations

### Business Leagues
- Trade associations
- Industry groups
- Professional associations

### Chambers of Commerce
- Organizations of merchants and traders
- Promote economic interests of commercial community

### Real Estate Boards
- Members interested in improving real estate business conditions
- Not organized for profit

### Boards of Trade
- Persons engaged in similar lines of business
- Regulate sale of commodities

### Professional Football Leagues
Specifically defined as exempt under 501(c)(6) whether or not they administer pension funds.

## Requirements

### Common Business Interest
Must establish common business interest of all members:
- Entire industry or industry component
- Geographic area industry
- NOT a group marketing a particular brand

### Improvement of Business Conditions
Purpose must be to improve business conditions:
- Not perform particular services for individuals
- Not provide convenience or economy to members

### Non-Profit Operation
- Not organized for profit
- May produce sufficient income to be self-sustaining
- No inurement to private benefit

## Permitted Activities

### Examples of Qualifying Activities
1. Promotion of higher business standards
2. Education of public in use of credit
3. Establishment of uniform casualty rates
4. Maintenance of commercial market integrity
5. Operation of trade publication for industry benefit
6. Encouragement of industry goods/services use

### Lobbying
- Permitted for common business interests
- Unlimited lobbying allowed
- Must report lobbying expenditures

### Political Activity
- Permitted with limitations
- Subject to reporting requirements
- May affect dues deductibility

## Prohibited Activities

### Not Permitted
- Stock or commodity exchanges (not exempt under 501(c)(6))
- Activities primarily benefiting individual members
- Operation of multiple listing service as primary activity
- Credit reporting agency as primary activity
- Assigning exclusive franchise areas

## Dues Deductibility

### Business Expense Deduction
Dues may be deductible as business expense if:
- Ordinary and necessary in trade or business
- Subject to limits for political/lobbying use

### Nondeductible Portion
Cannot deduct portion used for:
1. Influencing legislation (lobbying)
2. Political campaign participation
3. Grass roots lobbying
4. Direct contact with executive branch officials

### De Minimis Exception
In-house expenditures of $2,000 or less for these activities won't prevent deduction.

### Notification Requirement
Must notify members of nondeductible portion of dues.

## Contributions

### Not Deductible as Charitable
Contributions to 501(c)(6) organizations are NOT deductible as charitable contributions.

### May Be Business Deduction
May be deductible as trade or business expense if ordinary and necessary.

## Application Process

### Form 1024
File Form 1024 for recognition of exemption:
- Not required for exemption
- Required for IRS determination letter
- File within 27 months for retroactive recognition

## Filing Requirements

### Form 990
Must file annual information return:
- Form 990 for larger organizations
- Form 990-EZ for mid-sized organizations
- Form 990-N for small organizations

### Form 990-T
Required if unrelated business income exceeds $1,000.

### Form 1120-POL
May be required for political organization income.
