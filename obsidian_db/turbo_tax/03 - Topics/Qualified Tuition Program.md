# Qualified Tuition Program

## Overview

A Qualified Tuition Program (QTP), also known as a **529 plan**, is a tax-advantaged savings account for education expenses. Earnings grow tax-free and distributions are tax-free when used for qualified education expenses.

**Authority:** [[Pub 970 - Tax Benefits for Education#Chapter 7]]

## Types of QTPs

### Prepaid Tuition Plans
- Lock in current tuition rates
- Sponsored by states or eligible educational institutions
- Cover future tuition at today's prices

### Education Savings Plans
- Investment accounts for education savings
- Sponsored by states
- Can be used at any eligible institution nationwide
- More flexible than prepaid plans

## Qualified Education Expenses

### Higher Education Expenses
1. Tuition and fees (required for enrollment)
2. Books, supplies, and equipment
3. Room and board (if enrolled at least half-time)
4. Computer equipment, software, and internet access
5. Expenses for special needs services
6. Up to $10,000 in student loan principal and interest

### K-12 Education Expenses
- Up to $10,000 per year for tuition at elementary/secondary schools

### Postsecondary Credential Expenses
- Fees for recognized postsecondary credential programs
- Testing and continuing education fees

## Contribution Rules

| Rule | Details |
|------|---------|
| Contribution Limit | No federal limit; varies by state |
| Deductibility | Contributions NOT deductible federally |
| State Deductions | Many states offer deductions/credits |
| Gift Tax | Treated as gift to beneficiary |
| 5-Year Election | Can make 5 years of contributions at once |

## Rollover to Roth IRA (After 2023)

Subject to requirements:
- QTP must be open more than 15 years
- Beneficiary must have contributions in account more than 5 years
- Rollover limited to contribution limits
- Must be direct trustee-to-trustee transfer

## Designated Beneficiary

- Person for whom the QTP is established
- Can be changed to a family member without tax consequences
- Family members include: spouse, children, siblings, parents, in-laws, first cousins

## Tax Treatment of Distributions

| Distribution Type | Tax Treatment |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Used for qualified expenses | Tax-free |
| Earnings not used for qualified expenses | Taxable + 10% penalty |
| Death or disability of beneficiary | No penalty |
| Scholarship received | No penalty (up to scholarship amount) |

## Coordination with Education Credits

You can use QTP distributions in the same year as education credits, but:
- Cannot use same expenses for both
- Must reduce QTP qualified expenses by expenses used for credits
- Consider allocating expenses to maximize total benefits

## Common Questions

### Q: Can I contribute to both a QTP and Coverdell ESA?
**A:** Yes, you can contribute to both in the same year for the same beneficiary.

### Q: What if my child gets a scholarship?
**A:** You can take a distribution up to the scholarship amount without the 10% penalty (but earnings are still taxable).

### Q: Can I change the beneficiary?
**A:** Yes, to any family member of the current beneficiary without tax consequences.
