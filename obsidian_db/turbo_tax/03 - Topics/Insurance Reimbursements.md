# Insurance Reimbursements

## Overview

When figuring a casualty or theft loss, you must **subtract any insurance or other reimbursements** from the loss amount. You don't have a deductible loss to the extent you are reimbursed.

## Key Principle

> Loss = Smaller of (Adjusted Basis or Decrease in FMV) - Reimbursements

## Expected Reimbursements

If you expect to be reimbursed for part or all of your loss:
- **Subtract the expected reimbursement** when figuring your loss
- Reduce your loss even if you don't receive payment until a later tax year
- The loss isn't sustained until you know with reasonable certainty whether reimbursement will be received

## Failure to File Insurance Claim

If your property is covered by insurance and you **don't file a claim**:
- You cannot deduct the unrecovered amount
- Only the portion not covered by your policy (e.g., deductible) is deductible

**Example**: $1,000 deductible is still deductible (subject to $100/10% rules) even if no claim filed.

## Types of Reimbursements

### Insurance Payments
Most common type - for stolen or damaged property.

### Employer's Emergency Disaster Fund
Money received from employer's fund that **must be used** to rehabilitate or replace property reduces your casualty loss.

**Does not reduce loss** if:
- Received as excludable cash gifts with no limits on use
- Used to pay for repairs but given as unrestricted gift

### Insurance Payments for Living Expenses

Do **not** reduce casualty loss for payments received when:
- You lose use of your main home due to casualty
- Government authorities deny access to your main home

**Taxable income**: If payments exceed temporary increase in living expenses, include excess in income (Schedule 1, line 8z).

**Exception**: In federally declared disaster areas, none of these payments are taxable.

### Disaster Relief

Food, medical supplies, and other assistance **don't reduce your casualty loss** unless they replace lost or destroyed property.

### Loan Proceeds
Do **not** reduce casualty loss. Loans must be repaid.

### Cash Gifts
Excludable cash gifts with no restrictions on use **don't reduce** casualty loss, even if used for repairs.

## Reimbursement Received After Deducting Loss

### Less Than Expected
Include the difference as an additional loss on your return for the year you can reasonably expect no more reimbursement.

### More Than Expected
May have to include the extra reimbursement in income for the year received. See [[Pub 525 - Taxable and Nontaxable Income|Recoveries]] in Pub 525.

**Limitation**: Don't include any part that didn't reduce your tax in the earlier year.

### Gain on Reimbursement

If total reimbursements exceed adjusted basis, you have a **gain**. You may be able to postpone reporting the gain using [[Replacement Property]] rules.

## Calculating Temporary Increase in Living Expenses

| Step | Calculation |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Insurance payment for living expenses |
| 2 | Actual expenses during displacement |
| 3 | Normal living expenses for same period |
| 4 | Temporary increase (Line 2 - Line 3) |
| 5 | Taxable amount (Line 1 - Line 4) |

## Reporting Timing

| Situation | When to Report |
|-----------|---------------|
| Reimbursement in same year as loss | Reduce loss on current year return |
| Expected reimbursement | Reduce loss by expected amount |
| Received less than expected | Additional loss when certainty established |
| Received more than expected | Income in year received (if prior deduction reduced tax) |
