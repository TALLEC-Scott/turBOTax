# Excess IRA Contributions

An excess contribution occurs when you contribute more than the allowable limit to an IRA. This topic covers how excess contributions happen, the penalties, and correction methods.

## What Is an Excess Contribution?

An excess contribution is the amount contributed that exceeds the **smaller of:**

1. The contribution limit ($7,000 for 2025; $8,000 if 50+)
2. Your taxable compensation for the year

### Common Causes
- Contributing more than the annual limit
- Contributing more than your compensation
- Contributing to Roth IRA when income exceeds limits
- Improper rollover contributions
- Employer SEP contributions exceeding limits

## Penalty for Excess Contributions

### 6% Excise Tax

**Rate:** 6% of the excess contribution

**Applies:** Each year the excess remains in the IRA

**Maximum:** 6% of the combined value of all your IRAs as of December 31

### How Reported
- Form 5329, Part III (Traditional IRA)
- Form 5329, Part IV (Roth IRA)
- Reported on Schedule 2 (Form 1040), Line 8

### Example

You contribute $8,000 to your Traditional IRA but your limit is $7,000. You have a $1,000 excess.

- **Year 1:** If not corrected by due date, $60 penalty (6% × $1,000)
- **Year 2:** If still not corrected, another $60 penalty
- **Continues:** 6% each year until corrected

## Correction Methods

### Method 1: Withdraw by Due Date (Best Option)

**Deadline:** Due date of your tax return, including extensions (typically October 15 if you filed for extension)

**Requirements:**
1. Withdraw the excess contribution amount
2. Withdraw any earnings attributable to the excess
3. No deduction claimed for the excess

**Result:**
- No 6% penalty
- Excess contribution not included in income
- Earnings ARE included in income for the year of contribution
- 10% early distribution penalty may apply to earnings (unless exception)
- **Exception:** Beginning December 29, 2022, 10% penalty does NOT apply to earnings withdrawn with excess contribution by due date

**How to withdraw after filing:**
If you timely filed your return without withdrawing, you have 6 months from the due date (excluding extensions) to withdraw. File Form 1040-X with "Filed pursuant to section 301.9100-2" at the top.

### Method 2: Apply to Future Year

If you can't withdraw by the due date:

**How it works:**
1. The excess carries forward to next year
2. Counts as a contribution for that year
3. Still owe 6% penalty for current year
4. Future year's contribution limit is reduced

**Calculation (Worksheet 1-5):**
```
1. Maximum IRA deduction for current year       _______
2. IRA contributions for current year           _______
3. Subtract line 2 from line 1                  _______
4. Excess contributions at beginning of year    _______
5. Lesser of line 3 or line 4 = Amount that
   can be deducted this year                    _______
```

### Method 3: Distribute Without Income Inclusion

After the due date, you can withdraw excess contributions tax-free if:

1. You didn't deduct the excess contribution
2. Total contributions for that year didn't exceed $7,000/$8,000
3. You withdraw the amount of the excess

**Note:** This only works for nondeductible excess contributions.

## Excess Roth IRA Contributions

### Same Basic Rules Apply
- 6% penalty per year
- Same correction methods

### Additional Considerations
- Income-based limits may cause unexpected excess
- Recharacterization may be an option (if timely)
- Excess contributions reduce future year contribution limits

### Roth-Specific Calculation
When calculating excess after the due date:
- Distributions from Roth IRA reduce prior year excess
- Apply distributions to oldest excess first

## Historical Contribution Limits

When calculating excess from prior years, use these limits:

| Year(s) | Under 50 | Age 50+ |
|---------|----------|---------|
| 2025 | $7,000 | $8,000 |
| 2024 | $7,000 | $8,000 |
| 2023 | $6,500 | $7,500 |
| 2019-2022 | $6,000 | $7,000 |
| 2013-2018 | $5,500 | $6,500 |
| 2008-2012 | $5,000 | $6,000 |

## Form 5329 Details

### Part III: Traditional IRA Excess

| Line | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 9 | Prior year excess from Form 5329 |
| 10 | Current year contributions under limit |
| 11 | Distributions included in income |
| 12 | Distributions of prior year excess |
| 13-14 | Calculate remaining prior year excess |
| 15 | Current year excess contributions |
| 16 | Total excess contributions |
| 17 | 6% tax (smaller of line 16 or IRA value) |

### Part IV: Roth IRA Excess
Similar calculation for Roth IRAs.

## SEP and SIMPLE IRA Excess

### SEP-IRA
- Different contribution limits (see [[SEP Plans]])
- Employer contributions may cause excess
- See Publication 560 for rules

### SIMPLE IRA
- Different contribution limits (see [[SIMPLE Plans]])
- Excess subject to different penalty rates

## Planning to Avoid Excess Contributions

### Before Contributing
1. Verify your contribution limit
2. Confirm your compensation amount
3. Check income limits (for Roth)
4. Coordinate with spouse contributions

### During the Year
1. Track total contributions across all IRAs
2. Don't exceed the combined limit
3. Verify compensation hasn't changed

### Year-End Review
1. Confirm total contributions before April 15
2. Verify Roth IRA eligibility based on actual income
3. Consider recharacterization if needed
