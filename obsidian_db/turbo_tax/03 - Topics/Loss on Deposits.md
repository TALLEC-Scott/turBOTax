# Loss on Deposits

## Overview

A loss on deposits occurs when a bank, credit union, or other financial institution becomes **insolvent or bankrupt**. You may be able to deduct this type of loss.

## Deduction Options

You can choose ONE of the following methods:

### Option 1: Casualty Loss

Deduct as a casualty loss for any year in which you can reasonably estimate how much of your deposits you have lost.

**Limitation**: Because this is not attributable to a [[Federally Declared Disasters|federally declared disaster]], you may only deduct losses to the extent they don't exceed your personal casualty gains.

### Option 2: Nonbusiness Bad Debt

Wait until the year the actual loss is determined and deduct as a nonbusiness bad debt.

**Treatment**: Deducted as a short-term capital loss on Schedule D.

## Choosing the Method

The choice is generally made on the return you file for that year and applies to all your losses on deposits for the year in that particular financial institution.

**Important**: Once you make the choice, you cannot change it without IRS permission.

## Reporting Requirements

| Method | Form | Schedule |
|--------|------|----------|
| Casualty loss | Form 4684 | Schedule A (Form 1040) |
| Nonbusiness bad debt | Form 8949 | Schedule D (Form 1040) |

## Key Considerations

### Casualty Loss Method Advantages
- May deduct earlier (when loss can be reasonably estimated)
- Ordinary loss treatment (if offsetting personal casualty gains)

### Nonbusiness Bad Debt Advantages
- No requirement to have personal casualty gains
- Short-term capital loss treatment (can offset capital gains)

## IRS Deposit Insurance

Deposits in FDIC-insured banks and NCUA-insured credit unions are protected up to certain limits. Losses typically only occur for amounts exceeding insurance limits or at non-insured institutions.

## Recovered Amounts

If you recover an amount you deducted as a loss in an earlier year, you may have to include the recovery in income for the year received.

**Limitation**: Don't include any part that didn't reduce your tax in the earlier year.

## Example

You had $50,000 deposited in a bank that failed. FDIC insurance covered $40,000. You have a $10,000 loss on deposits.

**Option 1**: If you have $15,000 in personal casualty gains from a federally declared disaster, you can deduct $10,000 as a casualty loss (offsetting gains).

**Option 2**: If you have no personal casualty gains, deduct $10,000 as a nonbusiness bad debt (short-term capital loss) when loss is determined.

## Historical Note

The ordinary loss deduction for deposits in insolvent or bankrupt financial institutions (previously available as a miscellaneous itemized deduction) is **no longer available** for tax years beginning after 2017 due to suspension of miscellaneous itemized deductions.
