# Secured Debt

## Overview

A secured debt is a loan where you sign an instrument (mortgage, deed of trust, or land contract) that makes your ownership in a qualified home security for payment of the debt. You can only deduct home mortgage interest if your mortgage is a secured debt.

## Requirements for Secured Debt

A debt is secured by your home if the instrument:

1. **Makes your ownership security** - Puts your qualified home up as collateral
2. **Provides for satisfaction** - Your home can satisfy the debt in case of default
3. **Is recorded or perfected** - The security interest is recorded under applicable state or local law

In other words, your mortgage is a secured debt if you put your home up as collateral to protect the lender's interests.

## What Is NOT Secured Debt

A debt is NOT secured by your home if:

- It's secured **solely by a lien on your general assets** (not your home specifically)
- It's a security interest that attaches to the property **without your consent** (mechanic's lien, judgment lien)
- It **once was** secured by your home but **no longer is**

### Wraparound Mortgage Example

A wraparound mortgage is NOT a secured debt unless it is recorded or perfected under state law.

**Example**: Ari owns a home subject to a $40,000 mortgage. Ari sells the home for $100,000 to Palmer, who takes it subject to the $40,000 mortgage. Palmer pays $10,000 down and gives Ari a $90,000 note secured by a wraparound mortgage. Ari doesn't record the $90,000 mortgage. Therefore, the mortgage isn't a secured debt and Palmer can't deduct any interest paid on it as home mortgage interest.

## Choice to Treat Debt as Not Secured

You can choose to treat any debt secured by your qualified home as **not secured** by the home.

### Why Make This Choice?

You might want to treat a debt as not secured if the interest is fully deductible elsewhere (for example, as a business expense). This may allow you to deduct more interest on other debts that are deductible only as home mortgage interest.

### Rules for This Choice

- Begins with the tax year you make the choice
- Continues for all later tax years
- Can only be revoked with IRS consent
