# Capital Assets

## Overview

Almost everything you own and use for personal purposes, pleasure, or investment is a capital asset. The classification determines whether gain or loss is treated as capital or ordinary.

## Examples of Capital Assets

- Stocks and bonds
- Household furnishings
- Personal residence
- Car used for pleasure or commuting
- Gems and jewelry
- Coin or stamp collections
- Gold, silver, and other metals
- Timber grown on home or investment property

## Tax Treatment

### Personal-Use Property
- Gain: **Capital gain** (taxable)
- Loss: **Not deductible** (except casualty/theft)

### Investment Property
- Gain: **Capital gain**
- Loss: **Capital loss** (deductible)

## Holding Periods

| Holding Period | Classification |
|----------------|----------------|
| 1 year or less | Short-term capital gain/loss |
| More than 1 year | Long-term capital gain/loss |

## Exceptions - NOT Capital Assets

See [[Noncapital Assets]] for property that is NOT treated as capital assets, including:
- Inventory and stock in trade
- Accounts receivable
- Business property (depreciable, real)
- Self-created intellectual property

## Special Rules

### Release of Restriction on Land
- Treated as proceeds from sale of capital asset

### Self-Created Musical Compositions
- May elect to treat as capital assets
- See Pub. 550, Chapter 4
