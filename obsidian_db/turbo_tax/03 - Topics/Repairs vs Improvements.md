# Repairs vs Improvements

## Overview

Distinguishing between repairs and improvements is critical for tax purposes. Repairs are generally not deductible and not added to basis, while improvements are added to basis but not immediately deductible.

## The Key Distinction

| | Repair | Improvement |
|---|--------|-------------|
| **Purpose** | Keeps home in ordinary condition | Adds value, extends life, or adapts use |
| **Tax Treatment** | Not deductible, not added to basis | Added to [[Basis of Home]] |
| **Examples** | Fixing leaks, repainting | New roof, room addition |

## Repairs

A **repair** keeps your home in an ordinary, efficient operating condition. It does NOT:
- Add to the value of your home
- Prolong its useful life

### Examples of Repairs

- Repainting interior or exterior walls
- Fixing gutters or floors
- Patching leaks
- Plastering
- Replacing broken window panes
- Fixing a broken furnace
- Repairing a leaking roof

### Tax Treatment

- **Not deductible** as an expense
- **Not added to basis**
- Exception: Repairs for rental property may be deductible (see [[Rental Expenses]])

## Improvements

An **improvement** materially adds to your home's value, considerably prolongs its useful life, or adapts it to new uses.

### The Three Tests

An improvement must meet at least ONE of these criteria:

1. **Adds value**: Materially increases the home's worth
2. **Prolongs life**: Considerably extends the useful life
3. **Adapts use**: Allows the home to be used for new purposes

### Examples of Improvements

- Adding a room or bathroom
- Installing a new roof (full replacement)
- Paving the driveway
- Installing central air conditioning
- Rewiring the electrical system
- Adding a deck or porch
- Installing a fence
- New plumbing or wiring systems

### Tax Treatment

- **Added to basis**: Increases your [[Basis of Home]]
- **Not immediately deductible**: Benefit realized when you sell

## Gray Areas and Exceptions

### Extensive Remodeling

Repairs done as part of **extensive remodeling or restoration** are treated as improvements.

**Example**: If you repair water damage while adding a new bathroom, the repair costs are part of the improvement.

### Partial Replacements

Replacing a **component** of the home is often an improvement:
- New roof (improvement)
- New furnace (improvement)
- New carpet (improvement)

### Restoration After Casualty

Repairs to restore property after a casualty (fire, storm) may be improvements if they restore the property to a better condition than before.

## Recordkeeping

For improvements:
- Keep receipts and invoices
- Document the date and nature of work
- Track all costs (materials, labor, permits)

For repairs:
- Good to keep records, but not required for tax purposes

## Why It Matters

| Situation | Why Distinction Matters |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Selling home** | Improvements increase basis, reducing gain |
| **Rental property** | Repairs may be deductible now; improvements are depreciated |
| **Home office** | Improvements affect basis; repairs may be partially deductible |

## Summary Table

| Activity | Type | Tax Treatment |
|----------|------|---------------|
| Fixing a leak | Repair | Not deductible, not added to basis |
| New roof | Improvement | Added to basis |
| Repainting walls | Repair | Not deductible, not added to basis |
| Adding a bedroom | Improvement | Added to basis |
| Fixing broken window | Repair | Not deductible, not added to basis |
| New central A/C | Improvement | Added to basis |
| Replacing carpet | Improvement | Added to basis |
