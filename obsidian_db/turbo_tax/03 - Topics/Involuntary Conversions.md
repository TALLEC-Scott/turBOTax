# Involuntary Conversions

## Overview

An involuntary conversion occurs when property is destroyed, stolen, condemned, or disposed of under threat of condemnation, and you receive other property or money (insurance, condemnation award).

## Types of Involuntary Conversions

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Condemnation** | Legal taking for public use |
| **Threat of Condemnation** | Sale under threat of condemnation |
| **Casualty** | Destruction or damage |
| **Theft** | Criminal taking of property |

## Condemnations

### Condemnation Award
- Money or property received for condemned property
- Includes amounts paid to mortgage holders
- Includes interest on award (taxable as ordinary income)
- Excludes relocation payments (not included in income)

### Severance Damages
- Paid when part of property is condemned
- Value of retained property decreases
- Reduces basis of remaining property
- May result in gain if exceeds basis

### Threat of Condemnation
- Sale to third party qualifies if reasonable belief of condemnation
- Must have official notification or confirmed news report

## Gain or Loss Recognition

### General Rule
- Gain or loss is usually recognized
- Loss on personal-use property not deductible (except casualty/theft)

### Postponement of Gain
Gain may be postponed if:
1. Replacement property is similar or related in service or use, OR
2. Qualifying replacement property purchased within replacement period

### Replacement Period
- Begins on date of disposition
- Ends 2 years after close of first tax year with realized gain
- Extended to 3 years for condemned principal residence

## Main Home Condemned

- May exclude up to $250,000 ($500,000 MFJ) of gain
- Must meet ownership and use tests
- See [[Sale of Home Exclusion]] for details
- May postpone gain above exclusion if replacement property purchased
