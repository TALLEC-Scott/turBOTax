# SALT Deduction

## Overview

The State and Local Tax (SALT) deduction allows taxpayers who itemize to deduct certain state and local taxes on their federal return. For 2025, significant changes increased the deduction limit.

## What's New for 2025

The overall limit on the SALT deduction has increased:
- **$40,000** for most taxpayers
- **$20,000** if married filing separately

### Reduction for High Income

The limit is reduced if your **modified adjusted gross income** exceeds:
- $500,000 ($250,000 if married filing separately)

However, the limit cannot be reduced below:
- $10,000 ($5,000 if married filing separately)

## What Taxes Are Included

The SALT limit applies to the combined total of:

1. **State and local income taxes** OR **state and local sales taxes** (your choice)
2. **State and local real estate taxes**
3. **State and local personal property taxes**

### Income Tax vs. Sales Tax

You can choose to deduct:
- **Income taxes**: State and local income taxes withheld or paid
- **Sales taxes**: State and local sales taxes actually paid (or use IRS tables)

You cannot deduct both income taxes and sales taxes—you must choose one.

### Real Estate Taxes

See [[Real Estate Taxes]] for detailed rules on property tax deductions.

## Where to Deduct

Report SALT deductions on [[Schedule A (Form 1040)]]:
- Line 5a: Check box if electing sales tax deduction
- Line 5b: State and local real estate taxes
- Line 5c: State and local income or sales taxes
- Line 5d: Personal property taxes
- Line 5e: Total (subject to limit)

## Limitation Details

### Pre-2018

Before 2018, there was no federal limit on SALT deductions.

### 2018-2024

The TCJA limited SALT deductions to $10,000 ($5,000 MFS).

### 2025 and After

The limit increased to $40,000 ($20,000 MFS) with a phaseout for high-income taxpayers.

## Who Benefits Most

The SALT deduction benefits taxpayers who:
- Live in high-tax states
- Own expensive homes (high property taxes)
- Have high income (and thus high state income taxes)
- Itemize deductions (rather than taking the standard deduction)

## Marriage Penalty Consideration

Married couples filing separately each get half the limit of joint filers, which can create a marriage penalty in high-tax states.
