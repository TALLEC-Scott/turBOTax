# Roth IRA

A Roth IRA is an individual retirement arrangement where contributions are made with after-tax dollars, earnings grow tax-free, and qualified distributions are completely tax-free.

## What Is a Roth IRA?

Unlike a Traditional IRA:
- **Contributions are NOT deductible**
- **Earnings grow tax-free** (not just tax-deferred)
- **Qualified distributions are tax-free**
- **No RMDs during owner's lifetime** (beneficiaries have different rules)
- **No age limit for contributions** - can contribute at any age if you have compensation

## Eligibility to Contribute

To contribute to a Roth IRA, you must have:
1. **Taxable compensation** (wages, self-employment income, alimony from pre-2019 instruments)
2. **Income below the limits** (see below)

### Income Limits for 2025

| Filing Status | Full Contribution | Reduced Contribution | No Contribution |
|---------------|-------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| MFJ/QSS | MAGI < $236,000 | $236,000 - $246,000 | ≥ $246,000 |
| Single/HOH | MAGI < $150,000 | $150,000 - $165,000 | ≥ $165,000 |
| MFS (lived with spouse) | MAGI = $0 | $0 - $10,000 | ≥ $10,000 |
| MFS (didn't live with spouse) | MAGI < $150,000 | $150,000 - $165,000 | ≥ $165,000 |

### 2026 Income Limits (Increased)

| Filing Status | Phaseout Range |
|---------------|----------------|
| MFJ/QSS | $242,000 - $252,000 |
| Single/HOH | $153,000 - $168,000 |

## Contribution Limits (2025)

| Age | Maximum Contribution |
|-----|---------------------|
| Under 50 | $7,000 |
| 50 or older | $8,000 |

**Key rules:**
- Combined limit with Traditional IRA contributions
- Cannot exceed taxable compensation
- Reduced by income phaseouts (see Worksheet 2-2 in Pub 590-A)

### Calculating Reduced Contribution

When MAGI falls in the phaseout range:
1. Calculate the reduction percentage: (MAGI - Lower Limit) ÷ Phaseout Range
2. Multiply by the maximum contribution
3. Subtract from maximum contribution
4. Round up to nearest $10 (minimum $200 if above zero)

**Example (Single, age 45, MAGI $151,000):**
- Reduction = ($151,000 - $150,000) ÷ $15,000 = 0.067
- Reduced contribution = $7,000 - ($7,000 × 0.067) = $6,531 → $6,540

## Spousal Roth IRA

A spouse can contribute to a Roth IRA for the other spouse if:
- Filing joint return
- Combined MAGI is below the limit
- Each spouse can contribute up to the annual limit

See [[IRA Contributions]] for spousal IRA rules.

## Qualified Distributions

A **qualified distribution** from a Roth IRA is tax-free and penalty-free. Requirements:

### 5-Year Holding Period
Must be met by **one** of the following:
- 5 years from the first day of the tax year of the **first contribution** to ANY Roth IRA
- 5 years from the first day of the tax year of a **conversion**

### AND one of these conditions:
- Age 59½ or older
- Disability
- Death (distribution to beneficiary)
- First-time home purchase (up to $10,000 lifetime)

## Ordering Rules for Distributions

Roth IRA distributions are taken in this order:
1. **Regular contributions** - Tax-free, no penalty
2. **Conversion contributions** - Tax-free; may have 10% penalty if within 5 years and under 59½
3. **Earnings** - Taxable and subject to 10% penalty if not qualified

## Roth Conversions

You can convert a Traditional IRA (or employer plan) to a Roth IRA:

### Conversion Methods
1. **Rollover** - Receive distribution and contribute within 60 days
2. **Trustee-to-trustee transfer** - Direct transfer between institutions
3. **Same trustee transfer** - If both accounts at same institution

### Tax Implications
- Must include converted amount in income (except for basis)
- No 10% penalty on conversion amounts
- Cannot recharacterize conversions made after 2017

### Income Planning
Consider converting in years with:
- Lower income
- Available funds to pay tax
- Before large income events (RMDs, Social Security taxation)

See [[Roth Conversions]] for comprehensive rules.

## Rollovers to Roth IRA

### From Employer Plans
You can roll over from:
- 401(k) plans (including designated Roth accounts)
- 403(b) plans
- 457(b) plans
- Thrift Savings Plan

### Section 529 to Roth IRA Rollover (New for 2024+)
Beginning with distributions after December 31, 2023:
- Beneficiary can roll over 529 funds to their Roth IRA
- **Lifetime limit:** $35,000
- **Annual limit:** Subject to annual Roth IRA contribution limit
- **Requirements:** 529 account must be open 15+ years

### Military Death Gratuities
Can be contributed to a Roth IRA:
- Military death gratuity payments
- Servicemembers' Group Life Insurance (SGLI) payments
- Must be rolled over within 1 year of receipt

## Designated Roth Accounts vs. Roth IRAs

**Not the same thing:**

| Feature | Designated Roth Account | Roth IRA |
|---------|------------------------|----------|
| Where | 401(k), 403(b), 457(b) | Separate IRA |
| Contribution limits | $23,500 (2025) | $7,000 (2025) |
| RMDs | Required starting 2024 | None during owner's life |
| Income limits | None | Yes |

## Excess Contributions

Same rules as Traditional IRA:
- 6% excise tax per year until corrected
- Can withdraw by due date (with earnings) to avoid penalty
- Can apply to future year

See [[Excess IRA Contributions]].

## Advantages of Roth IRA

1. **Tax-free growth** - Earnings never taxed if qualified
2. **Tax-free distributions** - Qualified distributions not included in income
3. **No RMDs** - Leave money growing tax-free indefinitely
4. **Estate planning** - Beneficiaries receive tax-free income
5. **Flexibility** - Can withdraw contributions anytime tax/penalty-free
6. **No age limit** - Can contribute at any age

## Disadvantages of Roth IRA

1. **No deduction** - Contributions made with after-tax dollars
2. **Income limits** - High earners can't contribute directly
3. **Lower contribution ceiling** - Same $7,000 limit as Traditional IRA

## Backdoor Roth IRA Strategy

For high-income earners who can't contribute directly:
1. Contribute to Traditional IRA (nondeductible if above income limits)
2. Convert to Roth IRA
3. Pay tax only on any earnings in the account

**Caution:** This strategy is complicated if you have other Traditional IRAs with pre-tax money due to the pro-rata rule.
