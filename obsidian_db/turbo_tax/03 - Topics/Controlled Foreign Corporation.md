# Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC)

## Overview

A Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC) is a foreign corporation in which U.S. shareholders own more than 50% of the total combined voting power or value of all classes of stock. CFC status triggers various anti-deferral rules that require U.S. shareholders to include certain types of income currently, even if not distributed.

## CFC Definition

### Ownership Test
A foreign corporation is a CFC if **U.S. shareholders** own:
- More than 50% of the total combined **voting power** of all classes of stock entitled to vote, OR
- More than 50% of the total **value** of all classes of stock

### U.S. Shareholder Definition
A U.S. shareholder is a U.S. person who owns:
- 10% or more of the total voting power, OR
- 10% or more of the total value of all classes of stock (for tax years beginning after 2017)

### Constructive Ownership
Ownership may be attributed through:
- Family members
- Related entities
- Option rules

## Consequences of CFC Status

### Subpart F Income
U.S. shareholders must include their pro rata share of Subpart F income currently:
- Foreign base company sales income
- Foreign base company services income
- Foreign personal holding company income
- Insurance income
- International boycott income

### GILTI Inclusions
U.S. shareholders must include [[GILTI]] in gross income:
- Calculated on Form 8992
- Subject to Section 250 deduction for corporations
- Separate category for FTC purposes

### Reporting Requirements

#### Form 5471
U.S. persons in control of a foreign corporation must file **Form 5471** (Information Return of U.S. Persons With Respect to Certain Foreign Corporations).

**Penalty for non-filing:**
- $10,000 per annual accounting period
- Additional penalties if failure continues after IRS notice
- 10% reduction in foreign taxes available for FTC credit

#### Schedule K-3
Partners and S corporation shareholders receive foreign tax information on Schedule K-3.

## Foreign Tax Credit Implications

### CFC Shareholder Credit
U.S. shareholders may claim FTC for:
- Subpart F inclusions (treated as separate limit income)
- GILTI inclusions (Section 951A category)
- Dividends from CFC earnings and profits

### Section 962 Election
Individuals may elect to be taxed at corporate rates on:
- Subpart F inclusions
- Section 956 amounts
- GILTI inclusions

**Requirements:**
- File Forms 1118 as applicable
- Include statement required under Regulations section 1.962-2
- Statement must include:
  - Section 951(a) amount breakdown
  - GILTI inclusion
  - Section 250 deduction amount
  - FTC taken on each category
  - U.S. tax liability on amounts subject to section 962

### Income Categorization from CFCs

| Income Type | Category for FTC |
|-------------|------------------|
| Subpart F income | Same category as at CFC level |
| Dividends from E&P | Passive to extent of passive E&P |
| Interest from CFC | Passive if attributable to passive income |
| GILTI | Section 951A category |

## Section 956 Amounts

U.S. shareholders may have inclusion for:
- Investment in U.S. property by CFC
- Includes obligations to related U.S. persons
- Excludes certain liquid assets

## Information Return Penalties

### Form 5471 Failure
If you fail to file Form 5471 when due:
- **$10,000 penalty** per annual accounting period
- Additional penalties if failure continues more than 90 days after IRS notice
- **10% reduction** in foreign taxes available for FTC

### Maximum Reduction
The total FTC reduction cannot exceed the greater of:
- $10,000, OR
- The income of the foreign corporation for the accounting period

## Planning Considerations

1. **Entity Structure Review** - Consider ownership percentages to avoid CFC status
2. **Check-the-Box Elections** - May change entity classification
3. **Subpart F Planning** - Timing of income recognition
4. **High-Tax Exception** - May exclude high-taxed Subpart F income
5. **Section 962 Election** - Compare individual vs. corporate tax treatment
