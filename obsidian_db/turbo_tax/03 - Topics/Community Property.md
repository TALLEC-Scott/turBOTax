# Community Property

## Overview

Community property states have special rules for determining income between spouses. If your domicile (permanent legal home) is in a community property state, your income may be considered community income regardless of which spouse earned it.

## Community Property States

The following states are community property states:
- Arizona
- California
- Idaho
- Louisiana
- Nevada
- New Mexico
- Texas
- Washington
- Wisconsin

## Community vs. Separate Income

### Community Income
Generally, income earned by either spouse during marriage is community income, including:
- Wages and salaries
- Business income
- Interest and dividends from community property
- Rental income from community property

### Separate Income
Income from separate property generally belongs to the owning spouse:
- Income from property owned before marriage
- Income from property received by gift or inheritance
- Income defined as separate under state law

## Reporting on Separate Returns

If you and your spouse file separate returns in a community property state:
- Report **half** of community income
- Report **all** of your separate income
- Each spouse can claim **half** of income tax withheld from community income

## Relief from Community Income Liability

You may NOT be responsible for tax on an item of community income if ALL of the following apply:

1. You didn't file a joint return for the tax year
2. You didn't include the community income item in your gross income
3. The item is one of the following:
   - Wages, salaries, compensation your spouse received as an employee
   - Income from your spouse's sole proprietorship
   - Your spouse's distributive share of partnership income
   - Income from your spouse's separate property
   - Any other income belonging to your spouse under community property law
4. You didn't know of, and had no reason to know of, that community income
5. It wouldn't be fair to include the item in your gross income

## Spouses Living Apart All Year

Special rules apply if ALL of the following conditions are met:

1. You and your spouse lived apart all year
2. You didn't file a joint return
3. You and/or your spouse had earned income that is community income
4. You didn't transfer earned income between yourselves before year-end

**Treatment:**
- **Earned income:** Report as income of spouse who performed services
- **Trade/business income:** Report as income of spouse carrying on the business
- **Partnership income:** Report as income of spouse who is the partner
- **Separate property income:** Report as income of owning spouse
- **Social Security benefits:** Report as income of receiving spouse
- **Other community income:** Follow state community property law

## Ending the Marital Community

The marital community ends when:
- An absolute decree of divorce or annulment is issued
- A legal separation decree terminates the community (varies by state)
- A separation agreement divides community property
- Permanent separation occurs (in some states)

**Tax Treatment:**
- Report half the community income for the part of the year before the community ends
- Income received after community ends is separate income

## Alimony in Community Property States

Payments that may otherwise qualify as [[Alimony]] aren't deductible by the payer if they are the recipient spouse's part of community income.

Deductible only to the extent payments exceed the spouse's share of community income.

## Example

George and Sharon live in a community property state but file separate returns. George earned $20,000 in wages and $5,000 from a consulting business. Sharon earned $22,000 in wages and $10,000 from a partnership. They have $1,000 in community interest income.

**Without living apart exception:**
- Each reports half of total community income: $30,500 each

**If living apart all year:**
- George reports: $25,000 (his earnings) + $500 (half of interest)
- Sharon reports: $32,000 (her earnings) + $500 (half of interest)
