# Annuity Starting Date

## Overview

The **annuity starting date** is a critical date used to figure your [[Investment in the Contract]] and [[Expected Return]] under the [[General Rule]]. It determines the point at which your annuity calculations begin.

## Definition

The annuity starting date is the **later of**:

1. The first day of the first period for which you receive payment under the contract, OR
2. The date on which the obligation under the contract becomes fixed

## When the Obligation Becomes Fixed

The obligation becomes fixed when you:

- Complete all required payments under the contract
- Have a legally enforceable right to receive payments
- Cannot change the terms of the contract

## Examples

**Example 1**: On January 1, you completed all payments for an annuity contract providing monthly payments starting August 1, for the period beginning July 1. The annuity starting date is **July 1** (first day of first payment period).

**Example 2**: You buy an annuity on March 15 with payments beginning April 1. The annuity starting date is **April 1**.

**Example 3**: You buy an annuity on September 28 that will pay $125/month for life starting October 30. The annuity starting date is **October 1** (first day of the first payment period).

## Why It Matters

The annuity starting date is used to determine:

1. **Age for calculations** - Your age at the birthday nearest to this date
2. **Investment in the contract** - Your net cost as of this date
3. **Expected return multiples** - Which actuarial table values to use
4. **Applicable rules** - Whether post-1986 or pre-1986 rules apply

## Determining Age

For actuarial calculations, your age is the age at your **birthday nearest to the annuity starting date**.

**Example**: If the annuity starting date is July 15 and your birthday is September 1, use the age you turned (or will turn) on September 1.

## Key Date Thresholds

| Date | Significance |
|------|--------------|
| July 1, 1986 | Post-July 1986 rules for exclusion limits begin |
| November 18, 1996 | Simplified Method generally required for qualified plans after this date |
| August 20, 1996 | Death benefit exclusion no longer available for deaths after this date |

## Fractional First Payment

If your first payment covers only part of the first payment period:

- It's called a **fractional payment**
- The exclusion percentage still applies
- Multiply the exclusion percentage by the fractional amount

## Practical Example

You purchased an annuity for $22,050 on September 28, providing $125/month for life beginning October 30. Your nearest birthday was 61.

| Element | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Annuity starting date | October 1 |
| Age at nearest birthday | 61 |
| Multiple from Table V | 23.3 |
| Expected return | $34,950 ($125 × 12 × 23.3) |
| Investment in contract | $22,050 |
| Exclusion percentage | 63.1% |
| First-year payments | 3 ($375) |
| Tax-free portion | $236.63 |
| Taxable portion | $138.37 |
