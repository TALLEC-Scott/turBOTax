# Retirement Plans for Clergy

## Overview

Clergy members have access to various retirement savings options, including plans available to self-employed individuals and special church-specific plans. The tax treatment varies based on employment status.

## Types of Retirement Plans

### Self-Employed Retirement Plans

If you are self-employed, you may establish:
- **SEP (Simplified Employee Pension)**
- **SIMPLE (Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees)**
- **Qualified retirement plan** (Keogh/H.R. 10, including 401(k))

**Key requirement:** Must be truly self-employed under common-law rules.

### Tax-Sheltered Annuity Plans (403(b))

Church employees, members of religious orders, and ministers working as ministers or chaplains can generally participate in 403(b) plans.

**Eligible participants:**
- Church employees
- Members of religious orders
- Duly ordained, commissioned, or licensed ministers
- Chaplains

### Individual Retirement Arrangements (IRAs)

#### Traditional IRA
- 2025 contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+)
- May be deductible (subject to income limits if covered by employer plan)
- Distributions taxed as ordinary income

#### Roth IRA
- 2025 contribution limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+)
- Contributions not deductible
- Qualified distributions tax-free
- Income limits apply

## Determining Employment Status

### Common-Law Employee

If a congregation:
- Pays you a salary
- Controls what you do and how you do it

You are generally a **common-law employee**, even if your salary is subject to SE tax.

**Impact on retirement plans:**
- Cannot establish self-employed retirement plans (SEP, SIMPLE, Keogh)
- Can participate in employer's 403(b) or other church plan
- Can contribute to IRA

### Self-Employed

Income received directly from congregation members for:
- Weddings, baptisms, funerals
- Speaking honoraria
- Other personal services

This income is self-employment income for ALL tax purposes.

**Impact on retirement plans:**
- Can establish SEP, SIMPLE, or qualified retirement plan
- Can contribute to IRA

## IRA Contribution Limits for 2025

| Category | Under Age 50 | Age 50+ |
|----------|--------------|---------|
| Traditional/Roth IRA | $7,000 | $8,000 |

### Traditional IRA Deduction Limits (If Covered by Employer Plan)

| Married Filing Jointly | $156,000 - $166,000 |

## 403(b) Plan Contributions

### For Employees
- Employer may exclude contributions from income
- Contributions not included in W-2 wages
- Distributions taxed when received
- Roth contributions also available (after-tax, tax-free distributions)

### For Self-Employed Ministers
- Deduct contributions on [[Schedule 1]], line 16

### For Ministers Not Self-Employed (employer doesn't exclude contributions)
- Deduct contributions on [[Schedule 1]], line 24g

## Retirement Savings Contributions Credit

You may claim a credit of up to $1,000 ($2,000 if married filing jointly) for contributions to retirement plans.

### Credit Rates

Based on filing status and AGI:

| AGI Range | Credit Rate |
|-----------|-------------|
| Lowest income | 50% |
| | 20% |
| Highest eligible | 10% |

### 2025 Income Limits

| Filing Status | Maximum AGI |
|---------------|-------------|
| Married Filing Jointly | $79,000 |
| Head of Household | $59,250 |
| Single/MFS/QSS | $39,500 |

### Ineligibility

Cannot claim credit if:
1. Born after January 1, 2008
2. Full-time student for 5+ months
3. Claimed as dependent by another
4. AGI exceeds limits above

**Use [[Form 8880]] to calculate the credit.**

## Additional Medicare Tax Considerations

When calculating retirement contributions and AGI:
- Consider Additional Medicare Tax on earnings over thresholds
- [[Form 8959]] for calculation
