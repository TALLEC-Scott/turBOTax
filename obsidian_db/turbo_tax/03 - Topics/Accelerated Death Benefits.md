# Accelerated Death Benefits

## Overview

Accelerated death benefits are amounts received under a life insurance contract **before the death of the insured**. These benefits may be excluded from income if certain requirements are met, providing tax relief for terminally or chronically ill individuals.

## Types of Eligible Individuals

### Terminally Ill Individual
A person who has been certified by a physician as having an illness or physical condition that can reasonably be expected to result in death within **24 months** of the certification date.

### Chronically Ill Individual
A person who has been certified by a licensed health care practitioner as being unable to perform (without substantial assistance) at least **two of six activities of daily living** for at least 90 days due to loss of functional capacity, OR requiring substantial supervision for cognitive impairment.

**Activities of Daily Living (ADLs):**
1. Eating
2. Bathing
3. Dressing
4. Toileting
5. Transferring (moving from bed to chair)
6. Continence

## Tax Exclusion Rules

### Terminally Ill Individuals
- **Full exclusion** available for accelerated death benefits
- No dollar limit on exclusion
- No restrictions on use of funds

### Chronically Ill Individuals
- Exclusion limited to **per diem amount** or actual cost of care
- 2025 per diem limit: **$460 per day** (subject to annual inflation adjustment)
- Must use benefits for qualified long-term care services
- Exception: Per diem limit does not apply if benefits are paid on a per diem basis without regard to actual costs

## Viatical Settlements

A viatical settlement is the sale or assignment of a life insurance policy to a viatical settlement provider in exchange for a lump-sum payment. The exclusion rules apply to viatical settlements if:

- The insured is a terminally or chronically ill individual
- The viatical settlement provider is licensed in the state where the insured resides
- The settlement meets IRS requirements for qualified viatical settlements

## Reporting Requirements

### Form 1099-LTC
Payers must report accelerated death benefits on Form 1099-LTC, Long-Term Care and Accelerated Death Benefits. This form shows:
- Gross amount paid
- Amount excludable from income
- Whether the insured was terminally or chronically ill

### Your Tax Return
If benefits are fully excludable:
- Do not report on your tax return
- Keep Form 1099-LTC for your records

If benefits exceed excludable amount:
- Report excess as "Other income" on Form 1040, Schedule 1

## Limitations and Restrictions

### Per Diem Limit (Chronically Ill)
- Annual per diem limit = $460 × 365 = $167,900 (2025)
- Prorated for partial year of eligibility
- Does not apply to reimbursements for actual long-term care services

### Insurance Contract Requirements
- Must be a life insurance contract
- Accelerated death benefit rider or provision required
- Contract cannot be a qualified long-term care insurance contract (different rules apply)

## Comparison: Accelerated Death Benefits vs. Long-Term Care Insurance

| Feature | Accelerated Death Benefits | Long-Term Care Insurance |
|---------|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Source | Life insurance policy | Standalone LTC policy |
| Benefit trigger | Terminal/chronic illness | Chronic illness only |
| Effect on death benefit | Reduces death benefit | No effect |
| Per diem limit | $460/day (chronically ill) | $460/day |
| Tax treatment | Same exclusion rules | Same exclusion rules |
