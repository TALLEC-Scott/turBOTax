# Child Support

## Overview

Child support is a payment specifically designated for the support of a child under a divorce or separation instrument. Unlike [[Alimony]], child support has no tax consequences for either party.

## Tax Treatment

### For the Payer
- **Not deductible** - Child support payments are not deductible on your tax return
- No adjustment to income needed

### For the Recipient
- **Not taxable** - Child support received is not included in gross income
- Not reported on tax return

## Determining Child Support

A payment is treated as child support if it is specifically designated as such in the divorce or separation instrument, or if it meets either of these conditions:

### Contingency Relating to Your Child

A payment is treated as child support if it is reduced:
- On the happening of a contingency relating to your child (e.g., child reaching age of majority, leaving school, marrying, becoming employed)
- At a time clearly associated with such a contingency

### Presumed Child Support Timing

Payments are presumed to be child support if reduced:
1. Not more than 6 months before or after the child reaches 18, 21, or local age of majority
2. On two or more occasions within 1 year before or after a child reaches age 18-24

This presumption can be overcome by showing the reduction timing was independent of child-related contingencies.

## Underpayment Rule

If both [[Alimony]] and child support are required and you pay less than the total:

**Payments apply first to child support, then to alimony.**

### Example

Your divorce decree requires:
- $200/month ($2,400/year) child support
- $150/month ($1,800/year) alimony (pre-2019 instrument)

If you pay only $3,600 during the year:
- $2,400 is child support (nondeductible)
- $1,200 is alimony (deductible)

## Past-Due Child Support and Tax Refunds

If you owe past-due child support:
- Your federal tax refund may be offset to pay the debt
- State tax refunds may also be offset
- Your spouse may file as an [[Injured Spouse]] to recover their share of a joint refund

## Key Differences from Alimony

| Child Support | Alimony (Pre-2019) |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Not deductible by payer | Deductible by payer |
| Not taxable to recipient | Taxable to recipient |
| Paid for child's benefit | Paid for spouse's benefit |
| Continues after recipient's death | Must end at recipient's death |
| No SSN reporting required | Must report recipient's SSN |

## Example

Your 2018 divorce decree requires $500/month child support and $300/month alimony. Your child turns 18 on July 15, 2025. The decree states payments continue at $500/month after the child turns 18.

Since payments don't decrease when the child turns 18, the full $500/month was child support throughout the year. The $300/month alimony is deductible (pre-2019 instrument).
