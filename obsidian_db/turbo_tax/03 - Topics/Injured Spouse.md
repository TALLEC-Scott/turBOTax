# Injured Spouse

## Overview

An injured spouse is someone who filed a joint return and had their share of the refund applied to their spouse's past-due debts. You can recover your share of the overpayment by filing Form 8379.

## Who Is an Injured Spouse?

You are an injured spouse if:

1. You filed a joint return, AND
2. All or part of your share of the overpayment was (or is expected to be) applied against your spouse's past-due debts, AND
3. You meet the following conditions:
   - You made and reported tax payments (federal withholding, estimated tax payments), OR claimed a refundable tax credit (EIC, additional child tax credit) on the joint return
   - You are NOT legally obligated to pay the past-due amount

### Special Rule for Community Property States

If your permanent home is in a community property state, you only need to meet condition 3(b) - not being legally obligated to pay the past-due amount. See [[Community Property]] for details.

## Types of Past-Due Debts Covered

Your refund may be offset to pay your spouse's:
- Federal tax
- State income tax
- Child support payments
- Spousal support (alimony)
- Federal nontax debts (e.g., student loans)

## How to File

### With Your Original Return

If you haven't filed yet and know your refund will be offset:
- File [[Form 8379]] with your joint return
- You should receive your refund within 14 weeks (paper return) or 11 weeks (e-filed return)

### After Your Refund Is Offset

If your joint refund has already been offset:
- File [[Form 8379]] by itself (don't attach your tax return)
- Include copies of all Forms W-2, W-2G, and 1099 showing income tax withheld for both spouses
- Can take up to 8 weeks to receive your refund

## Difference from Innocent Spouse Relief

| Injured Spouse | [[Innocent Spouse Relief]] |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| Recover YOUR share of a refund | Relief from joint TAX liability |
| Use [[Form 8379]] | Use [[Form 8857]] |
| Refund offset by spouse's debts | Erroneous items on joint return |
| No knowledge requirement | Must prove lack of knowledge |

**Key Distinction:** An injured spouse wants their portion of a refund. An innocent spouse wants relief from owing tax.

## Allocation of Overpayment

The IRS allocates the overpayment based on:
- Each spouse's share of the total tax withheld
- Each spouse's share of estimated tax payments
- Each spouse's share of refundable credits claimed

## When NOT to File

Don't file Form 8379 if:
- You owe past-due federal tax, state tax, or child/spousal support yourself
- The entire refund belongs to your spouse (you had no income or withholding)

## Example

You and your spouse file a joint return showing a $5,000 refund. Your spouse owes $10,000 in past-due child support from a previous marriage. You earned $40,000 with $6,000 withheld; your spouse earned $30,000 with $4,000 withheld.

- Your share of withholding: 60% ($6,000 / $10,000)
- Your injured spouse refund: $3,000 (60% of $5,000)
- Amount applied to spouse's debt: $2,000

File Form 8379 to recover your $3,000 share.
