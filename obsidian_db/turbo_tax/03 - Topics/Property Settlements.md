# Property Settlements

## Overview

Property settlements are transfers of property between spouses or former spouses incident to divorce. Generally, these transfers are tax-free - no gain or loss is recognized.

## General Rule

**No gain or loss is recognized** on a transfer of property from you to:
- Your spouse, OR
- Your former spouse (if incident to divorce)

This applies even if the transfer is in exchange for cash, release of marital rights, assumption of liabilities, or other consideration.

## What Qualifies as "Incident to Divorce"

A transfer is incident to divorce if:
1. It is related to the end of your marriage, OR
2. It occurs within **1 year** after the date your marriage ends

### Related to End of Marriage
A transfer is related to the end of marriage if BOTH:
- The transfer occurs within **6 years** after the marriage ends
- The transfer is made under your original or modified divorce or separation instrument

## Exceptions to Nonrecognition

The nonrecognition rule does NOT apply to:
- Transfers to a nonresident alien spouse/former spouse
- Certain stock redemptions under IRC Section 1041 regulations
- Certain transfers in trust (see below)

## Types of Property Covered

The term "property" includes:
- Real property (homes, land)
- Personal property
- Tangible property
- Intangible property
- Separate property
- Community property
- Property acquired after the end of marriage

**Does NOT include:** Services

## Special Property Types

### Health Savings Accounts (HSAs)
Transfer to spouse/former spouse is not taxable. After transfer, treated as spouse's HSA.

### Archer Medical Savings Accounts (MSAs)
Same treatment as HSAs.

### [[IRAs]]
Transfer of IRA interest under divorce decree is not taxable. After transfer, treated as spouse's IRA.

### Investment Credit Property
If transferred with recapture potential:
- Transferor doesn't have to recapture
- Recipient may have to recapture if they dispose of property or change use

## Transfers in Trust

If you transfer property in trust:
- Generally no gain or loss recognized
- BUT you must recognize gain to the extent liabilities assumed by the trust plus liabilities on the property exceed your adjusted basis

### Example
You transfer property (FMV $12,000, basis $1,000) in trust for your spouse. The property is subject to a $5,000 liability. No other liabilities.

Your recognized gain: $4,000 ($5,000 liability - $1,000 basis).

## Transfers to Third Parties

If you transfer property to a third party on behalf of your spouse, it's treated as two transfers:
1. Transfer from spouse to third party (no gain/loss to you)
2. Transfer from you to spouse (spouse may have gain/loss)

**Requirements for this treatment:**
- Required by divorce/separation instrument, OR
- Requested in writing by spouse, OR
- Spouse consents in writing (must state intent to apply IRC Section 1041)

## Basis of Property Received

**Your basis = Transferor's adjusted basis**

This is carryover basis (same as gift treatment):
- For determining gain OR loss on later disposition
- Applies regardless of value at transfer time
- Applies even if property liabilities exceed basis

## Reporting Income from Transferred Property

| If you transfer... | You report... | Spouse reports... |
|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Income-producing property | Income until transfer date | Income after transfer |
| Passive activity with unused losses | Cannot deduct unused losses | Increases basis by unused losses |
| Investment credit property | No recapture required | May have recapture obligation |
| Nonstatutory stock options/deferred comp | No income on transfer | Income when exercised/paid |

## Sale of Jointly Owned Property

If you sell jointly owned property to split proceeds:
- Report your share of gain/loss based on state property law
- See [[Pub 544]] for reporting requirements

### Sale of Home
If you sold your main home:
- May exclude up to $250,000 gain ($500,000 if married filing jointly)
- Special rules apply for divorced/separated individuals
- See [[Pub 523]] for details

## Gift Tax on Property Settlements

### Generally No Gift Tax
Transfers to a U.S. citizen spouse are generally not subject to gift tax.

### Exceptions May Apply
Transfer to former spouse may be subject to gift tax unless it:
- Is made in settlement of marital support rights
- Qualifies for marital deduction (before final decree)
- Is made under divorce decree
- Is made under written agreement (divorce within 3 years)
- Qualifies for annual exclusion ($19,000 for 2025)

## Costs of Getting a Divorce

**Not deductible:**
- Legal fees for getting a divorce
- Legal fees for tax advice related to divorce
- Fees to get [[Alimony]]
- Appraiser, actuary, accountant fees for tax or alimony
- Costs of personal advice or counseling
- Legal fees for property settlement (but can add to basis of property received)

## Example

You and your spouse jointly owned your home. Under your divorce decree, you transfer your interest to your spouse.

- No gain or loss is recognized on the transfer
- Your spouse's basis in your share = your adjusted basis
- Your spouse's total basis = combined adjusted basis from both shares
