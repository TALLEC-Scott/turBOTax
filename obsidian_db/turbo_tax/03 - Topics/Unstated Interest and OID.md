# Unstated Interest and Original Issue Discount (OID)

## Overview

When an [[Installment Sales|installment sale]] contract provides for little or no interest, the IRS may recharacterize part of the stated principal as interest. This prevents taxpayers from disguising interest income as principal payments. Two code sections govern this: **Section 1274** and **Section 483**.

## When These Rules Apply

The unstated interest rules apply when:
1. An installment sale contract doesn't provide for adequate stated interest
2. Payments are due more than 6 months after the sale date (Section 1274) or more than 1 year (Section 483)

## Section 1274

Section 1274 applies when:
- Any payment is due more than 6 months after sale
- The debt instrument doesn't provide adequate stated interest
- Total consideration exceeds $250,000

### Exceptions to Section 1274

Section 1274 does **not** apply to:
- Sales with total payments of $250,000 or less
- Sale of an individual's main home
- Farm sales of $1 million or less by qualifying taxpayers
- Certain land transfers between related persons
- Cash method debt instruments (below)

### Cash Method Debt Instrument

A special election allows parties to treat qualifying debt instruments under the cash method if:
1. Lender doesn't use accrual method or is not a dealer in the property
2. Both parties jointly elect cash method treatment
3. Stated principal is $5,211,900 or less (adjusted annually for inflation)

## Section 483

Section 483 applies to installment contracts:
- Not covered by Section 1274
- With inadequate stated interest
- Where payments are due more than 1 year after sale

### Exceptions to Section 483

Section 483 does **not** apply to:
- Sales where no payment is due more than 1 year after sale
- Sales of $3,000 or less

## Determining Which Section Applies

| Factor | Section 1274 | Section 483 |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Payment timing | Due > 6 months | Due > 1 year |
| Sales price | > $250,000 | Any amount |
| Property type | Most property | Most property |
| Main home exception | Yes | No |

## Adequate Stated Interest

Interest is "adequate" if it equals or exceeds the **Applicable Federal Rate (AFR)**:
- Published monthly in the Internal Revenue Bulletin
- Available at IRS.gov/ApplicableFederalRates
- Test rate cannot exceed 9% (compounded semiannually) for seller-financed sales of $7,296,700 or less

## Related Land Transfers

For land transfers between related persons:
- Section 483 applies to amounts up to $500,000
- Section 1274 applies to amounts exceeding $500,000

Related persons include: individual, spouse, brothers/sisters, ancestors, lineal descendants.

## Exceptions to Both Sections

Neither Section 1274 nor Section 483 applies to:
- Assumption of existing debt instrument (unless modified)
- Publicly traded debt or property
- Sale of patent rights with contingent amounts
- Certain annuity contracts
- Transfers between spouses (Section 1041)
- Below-market demand loans (Section 7872)
- Personal-use property below-market loans

## Effect on Installment Sale Calculations

When unstated interest or OID applies:
1. **Selling price reduced** by the amount of unstated interest/OID
2. **Gross profit reduced** accordingly
3. **Interest income** reported separately (ordinary income)

### Example

Sale price stated as $100,000, no stated interest, 5 annual payments:
- AFR requires $12,000 imputed interest
- Adjusted selling price = $88,000
- Imputed interest reported as ordinary income over payment period

## Common Questions

### Q: How do I know if my contract has adequate stated interest?
**A:** Compare your stated rate to the AFR for the month of sale. If equal or higher, interest is adequate.

### Q: What if my sale was for $200,000?
**A:** Section 1274 doesn't apply to sales of $250,000 or less, but Section 483 may still apply if payments extend beyond 1 year.

### Q: Who reports the unstated interest?
**A:** The seller reports the imputed interest as ordinary income. The buyer may have interest expense and OID deductions.
