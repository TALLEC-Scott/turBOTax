# Estate Income Tax Return

## Overview

An estate is a taxable entity that must file Form 1041, U.S. Income Tax Return for Estates and Trusts, if it meets certain filing requirements. The estate is a separate taxpayer from the decedent.

## Filing Requirements

### When Form 1041 is Required
- Gross income of $600 or more for the tax year, OR
- Any beneficiary is a nonresident alien, OR
- Estate has taxable income after deductions

### Tax Year Options
- Calendar year (January 1 - December 31)
- Fiscal year (any 12-month period ending on last day of month)
- Estate's tax year is established on first return filed

### When to File
- **Calendar year**: Due April 15
- **Fiscal year**: Due 15th day of 4th month after year-end
- Automatic 5½-month extension available via Form 7004

## Income Reporting

### Income to Include
- Interest and dividends received by estate
- Income in respect of decedent (IRD)
- Rental income from estate property
- Business income if estate operates a business
- Capital gains from sale of estate assets

### Accounting Method
- Generally same method used by decedent
- Can change with IRS approval
- Cash or accrual method available

## Exemption and Deductions

### Estate Exemption
- Amount deducted from gross income
- Simplified deduction for estates
- See Form 1041 instructions for current amount

### Income Distribution Deduction
- Amounts paid, credited, or required to be distributed to beneficiaries
- Limited to distributable net income (DNI)
- Estate gets deduction; beneficiary reports income

### Other Deductions
- Administrative expenses
- Attorney fees
- Executor commissions
- Interest and taxes
- Charitable contributions (if allowed by will)

## Distributable Net Income (DNI)

DNI determines:
- Maximum income distribution deduction for estate
- Amount beneficiary must include in income
- Character of income distributed to beneficiaries

### DNI Calculation
```
Taxable Income
+ Tax-exempt income
+ Income distribution deduction
+ Charitable deduction
- Capital gains allocated to corpus
- Expenses allocable to tax-exempt income
= Distributable Net Income
```

## Separate Shares Rule

Applies when:
- Estate has more than one beneficiary, AND
- Economic interests are independent of each other

Each share treated as separate estate for determining:
- Distributable net income
- Income distribution deduction
- Beneficiary's includible amount

## Distributions to Beneficiaries

### Currently Distributable Income
- Income required to be distributed under will or local law
- Deductible by estate whether or not actually distributed
- Beneficiary must include in income

### Other Amounts Distributed
- Discretionary distributions
- Amounts actually paid, credited, or distributed
- 65-day rule: Can elect to treat distributions within 65 days after year-end as made in prior year

### Property Distributed in Kind
- Deduction = lesser of estate's basis or FMV
- If estate recognizes gain, deduction = FMV
- Beneficiary takes estate's basis

## Schedule K-1

Each beneficiary receives Schedule K-1 showing:
- Share of income by character (interest, dividends, capital gains)
- Deductions and credits passed through
- Beneficiary reports these amounts on their individual return

## Estimated Tax

### Requirements
- Estates with tax years ending 2+ years after death must pay estimated tax
- Same rules as individuals for payment thresholds
- Use Form 1041-ES

### Due Dates
- 15th day of 4th, 6th, 9th months of tax year
- 15th day of 1st month of following tax year

## Termination of Estate

### Final Return
- File final return for year of termination
- Check "Final return" box on Form 1041
- Allocate remaining income to beneficiaries

### Excess Deductions on Termination
- Deductions in excess of income
- Beneficiaries deduct on their returns
- Separate character preserved (AGI deduction, itemized deduction)
