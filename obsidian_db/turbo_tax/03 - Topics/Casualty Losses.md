# Casualty Losses

## Overview

A casualty is the damage, destruction, or loss of property resulting from an identifiable event that is **sudden, unexpected, or unusual**.

- **Sudden**: Swift, not gradual or progressive
- **Unexpected**: Ordinarily unanticipated and unintended
- **Unusual**: Not a day-to-day occurrence

## Types of Casualty Losses

### 1. Federal Casualty Loss
An individual's casualty loss of personal-use property attributable to a [[Federally Declared Disasters|federally declared disaster]]. Must occur in a state receiving a federal disaster declaration.

### 2. Disaster Loss
A loss attributable to a federally declared disaster occurring in an area eligible for public or individual assistance. Available for business property and by corporations, partnerships, and S corporations.

### 3. [[Qualified Disaster Loss]]
Enhanced benefits for specific major disasters declared between 2016-2025.

## Deductible Causes

- Car accidents (except willful negligence)
- Earthquakes
- Fires (except willfully set)
- Floods
- Government-ordered demolition (disaster-related)
- Mine cave-ins
- Sonic booms
- Shipwrecks
- Storms (hurricanes, tornadoes)
- Vandalism
- Terrorist attacks
- Volcanic eruptions

## Nondeductible Losses

Losses are **not deductible** if caused by:

- Accidentally breaking articles under normal conditions
- Family pet damage
- Car accident from willful negligence
- Willfully set fires
- Progressive deterioration (termite damage, gradual wear)

### Special Exception: Corrosive Drywall
Special procedure allows deduction for repairs due to corrosive drywall installed 2001-2009 (Revenue Procedure 2010-36).

## Figuring the Loss

The amount of loss is the **smaller of**:

1. Adjusted basis in the property, OR
2. Decrease in fair market value (FMV)

**Minus**: Any insurance or other reimbursements

### Business/Income-Producing Property

If completely destroyed or stolen:
> Loss = Adjusted Basis - Salvage Value - Reimbursements
