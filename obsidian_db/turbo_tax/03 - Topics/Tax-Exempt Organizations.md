# Tax-Exempt Organizations

## Overview

Tax-exempt organizations are entities that are exempt from federal income tax under Internal Revenue Code Section 501(a). These organizations must be organized and operated exclusively for purposes specified in the Code, and no part of their net earnings may inure to private shareholders or individuals.

## Types of Tax-Exempt Organizations

### Section 501(c)(3) - Charitable Organizations
Organizations organized and operated for religious, charitable, scientific, testing for public safety, literary, or educational purposes, or to foster national or international amateur sports competition, or for the prevention of cruelty to children or animals. Contributions to these organizations are generally deductible.

See [[Section 501(c)(3) Organizations]] for detailed information.

### Section 501(c)(4) - Social Welfare Organizations
Civic leagues and organizations operated exclusively for promoting social welfare. Includes [[Social Welfare Organizations]] like community associations and volunteer fire departments.

### Section 501(c)(5) - Labor, Agricultural, and Horticultural Organizations
Organizations that better the conditions of persons engaged in labor, agriculture, or horticulture, improve products, or develop more efficiency.

### Section 501(c)(6) - Business Leagues
Business leagues, chambers of commerce, real estate boards, boards of trade, and professional associations.

### Section 501(c)(7) - Social and Recreation Clubs
Clubs organized for pleasure, recreation, and other nonprofitable purposes.

### Section 501(c)(8)/(10) - Fraternal Organizations
Fraternal beneficiary societies and domestic fraternal societies operating under the lodge system.

## Key Requirements

### Organizational Test
The organization must be organized for exempt purposes:
- Articles of organization must limit purposes to exempt purposes
- Assets must be dedicated to exempt purposes upon dissolution
- No provisions for private inurement

### Operational Test
The organization must be operated for exempt purposes:
- Primary activities must further exempt purposes
- No more than insubstantial part of activities can be non-exempt
- Cannot serve private interests

### Private Inurement Prohibition
No part of net earnings may inure to the benefit of private shareholders or individuals. See [[Excess Benefit Transactions]] for penalties.

## Application Process

1. Obtain an [[Employer Identification Number]]
2. File the appropriate exemption application:
   - Form 1023 or 1023-EZ for 501(c)(3) organizations
   - Form 1024 for most other organizations
3. Submit required user fee
4. Await IRS determination

See [[Pub 557 - Tax-Exempt Status for Your Organization]] for complete application procedures.

## Annual Filing Requirements

Most tax-exempt organizations must file annual information returns:

| Organization Type | Form Required |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Large exempt organizations | Form 990 |
| Small organizations | Form 990-EZ or 990-N |
| Private foundations | Form 990-PF |

See [[Form 990 Filing Requirements]] for detailed filing information.

## Unrelated Business Income

Tax-exempt organizations may be subject to tax on [[Unrelated Business Income]] if they regularly carry on a trade or business that is not substantially related to their exempt purpose.
