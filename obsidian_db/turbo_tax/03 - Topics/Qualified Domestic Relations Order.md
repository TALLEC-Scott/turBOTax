# Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO)

## Overview

A Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO) is a judgment, decree, or court order that divides retirement plan benefits between spouses or former spouses during a divorce. It allows retirement assets to be split without triggering early withdrawal penalties.

## Requirements for a QDRO

A QDRO must:

1. **Be a valid court order** issued under a state's or tribe's domestic relations law
2. **Recognize the right of** a spouse, former spouse, child, or other dependent to receive benefits
3. **Relate to** payment of child support, alimony, or marital property rights
4. **Specify:**
   - The amount or percentage of benefits to be paid
   - The number of payments or period to which the order applies
   - All affected retirement plans
   - The names and last known mailing addresses of the participant and alternate payee

## Plans Covered by QDRO

- 401(k) plans
- 403(b) tax-sheltered annuities
- Profit-sharing plans
- Pension plans
- Thrift Savings Plan (TSP)
- Most other qualified employer retirement plans

**NOT covered:**
- [[IRAs]] (transferred under different divorce rules - see [[Property Settlements]])
- Social Security benefits
- Certain non-qualified deferred compensation plans

## Tax Treatment

### Benefits Paid to Spouse or Former Spouse

- Taxable to the **recipient spouse** (not the plan participant)
- The recipient spouse is responsible for reporting the income
- The plan participant has no tax liability on amounts paid to the alternate payee

### Benefits Paid to Child or Other Dependent

- Treated as paid to the **plan participant**
- The participant includes the distribution in their income
- The participant is responsible for any taxes

### Early Withdrawal Penalty Exception

Distributions made under a QDRO to a spouse or former spouse are **NOT subject to the 10% early withdrawal penalty**, regardless of the recipient's age.

## Rollover Options

If you receive an eligible rollover distribution under a QDRO, you can:
- Roll it over to an [[IRAs|IRA]] tax-free
- Roll it over to another qualified retirement plan
- Take a cash distribution (taxable but no 10% penalty)

**Note:** The 10% early withdrawal penalty does NOT apply to QDRO distributions, even if not rolled over.

## Lump-Sum Distributions

A spouse or former spouse receiving a lump-sum distribution under a QDRO may use the special rules for lump-sum distributions if:
- The distribution would have qualified as a lump-sum distribution had the participant received it
- Only the spouse's/former spouse's balance is considered in determining if it's a total distribution

## Types of QDRO Awards

### Separate Interest Approach
- Creates a separate account for the alternate payee
- The alternate payee can choose their own payment form
- Surviving spouse benefits may be preserved

### Shared Payment Approach
- The alternate payee receives payments when the participant begins receiving benefits
- Payments end when the participant dies (unless the order specifies otherwise)
- Less flexibility for the alternate payee

## Getting a QDRO

1. **Prepare the order** - Usually drafted by an attorney
2. **Submit to the plan administrator** - The plan must review the order for qualification
3. **Plan administrator determines qualification** - The plan must accept or reject the order
4. **Court approves the order** - Final court approval is required

## Important Considerations

- **Act quickly:** Some plans have time limits for submitting QDROs after divorce
- **Get plan-specific language:** Each plan may have different requirements
- **Consider loan offsets:** Outstanding loans may affect the division
- **Review survivor benefits:** Determine what happens if either party dies

## Example

You and your spouse divorce after 15 years of marriage. Your spouse's 401(k) has a balance of $300,000. Under the QDRO, you are entitled to 50% of the account balance accrued during marriage ($150,000).

**Tax Treatment:**
- The $150,000 transfer to your IRA is tax-free
- You can leave the money in the IRA or roll it to your own 401(k)
- If you take a cash distribution, it's taxable but not subject to the 10% penalty
- Your former spouse reports nothing from this transfer
