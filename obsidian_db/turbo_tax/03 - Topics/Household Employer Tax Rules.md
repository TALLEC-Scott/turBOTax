# Household Employer Tax Rules

Tax rules that apply when you pay someone to work in your home, such as a nanny, housekeeper, or caregiver. Covered in [[Pub 503 - Child and Dependent Care Expenses]] and [[Pub 926]].

## Are You a Household Employer?

You are a household employer if you pay someone to work in your home AND you control:
- What work is done
- How it is done

### Common Household Employees

| Typically Employees | Typically NOT Employees |
|---------------------|-------------------------|
| Nannies | Daycare center workers |
| Babysitters working in your home | Care providers working at their own home/business |
| Housekeepers | Workers from placement agencies that control the work |
| Caregivers for elderly/disabled | Self-employed individuals |

### Placement Agency Rules

| Agency Role | Worker is Your Employee? |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| Agency controls what and how work is done | No (agency's employee) |
| Agency just provides a list of workers | Yes (if you hire and pay directly) |

## Employer Identification Number (EIN)

If you are a household employer, you need an **EIN** (Employer Identification Number). Apply online at IRS.gov/EIN.

## Employment Taxes

If you have a household employee, you may be subject to:

### 1. Social Security and Medicare Taxes

**Requirement:** Withhold from employee's pay and match as employer.

| Tax | Employee Rate | Employer Rate |
|-----|---------------|---------------|
| Social Security | 6.2% | 6.2% |
| Medicare | 1.45% | 1.45% |

**Wage Base:** Social Security tax applies up to the annual wage base limit.

### 2. Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA)

**Requirement:** Paid by employer only (not withheld from employee).

**Purpose:** Provides unemployment compensation to workers who lost jobs.

**Rate:** 6.0% on first $7,000 of wages (credit reduces effective rate).

### 3. Federal Income Tax Withholding

**Requirement:** Withhold from employee's pay IF the employee asks you to AND you agree.

**Note:** You are NOT required to withhold federal income tax for household employees unless they request it.

## Filing Requirements

### Schedule H (Form 1040)

File [[Schedule H (Form 1040)]] with your tax return if you paid:
- $2,800 or more (2025) in cash wages to a household employee
- Total wages of $1,000 or more in any calendar quarter to household employees

### Form W-2

Provide Form W-2 to each household employee by January 31 if you withheld Social Security and Medicare taxes.

### Form W-3

File Form W-3 with the Social Security Administration by January 31.

## State Requirements

You may also owe:
- State unemployment tax
- State employment taxes
- Workers' compensation insurance

Contact your state unemployment tax office for requirements.

## Exceptions

### Workers Who Are Not Employees

- Self-employed individuals in business for themselves
- Your spouse
- Your child under age 21
- Your parent (with exceptions for Social Security)
- Workers under age 18 (with exceptions)

### Non-Cash Payments

Social Security and Medicare taxes don't apply to non-cash wages like room and board.

## Impact on Child and Dependent Care Credit

If you pay someone to work in your home caring for your dependent or spouse:

1. You must indicate on [[Form 2441]] whether the provider was your household employee
2. The taxes you pay on their wages count as [[Work-Related Expenses]] for the credit
3. You must follow all household employer rules if they qualify as your employee

## Recordkeeping

Keep records of:
- Employee's name, address, SSN
- Dates of employment
- Wages paid (cash and non-cash)
- Taxes withheld
- Forms provided (W-4, W-2, I-9)
