# Resident Alien Status

## Overview

A resident alien is a non-U.S. citizen who meets either the **Green Card Test** or the **Substantial Presence Test** for the calendar year. Resident aliens are generally taxed on their worldwide income the same as U.S. citizens.

## Green Card Test

You are a resident alien if you are a lawful permanent resident of the United States at any time during the calendar year.

**Key Points:**
- Possess a valid Form I-551 (Permanent Resident Card/Green Card)
- Status continues until officially revoked or abandoned
- Filing Form I-407 or abandonment letter with USCIS terminates status
- Long-term residents who cease status may face expatriation tax

**Caution:** Even with a green card, you may be treated as a nonresident under a tax treaty tiebreaker rule (section 7701(b)(6)(B)).

## Substantial Presence Test

You meet this test if you are physically present in the U.S. for at least:

1. **31 days** during the current year, AND
2. **183 days** during the 3-year period counting:
   - All days in current year
   - 1/3 of days in prior year
   - 1/6 of days in second prior year

**Example:** Present 120 days each year (2023-2025):
- 2025: 120 days
- 2024: 40 days (1/3 × 120)
- 2023: 20 days (1/6 × 120)
- Total: 180 days → Not a resident under SPT

## Days Not Counted

The following days are excluded from the substantial presence test:

- **Regular commuters** from Canada or Mexico
- **Transit days** (less than 24 hours in U.S.)
- **Crew members** of foreign vessels
- **Medical condition** days (if condition arose while in U.S.)
- **Exempt individuals** (students, teachers, foreign government officials)

## Closer Connection Exception

Even if you meet the substantial presence test, you may be treated as a nonresident if you:

1. Are present in the U.S. for fewer than 183 days during the year
2. Maintain a tax home in a foreign country
3. Have a closer connection to that foreign country than to the U.S.

**Filing Requirement:** File [[Form 8840]] to claim this exception.

## Residency Starting Date

For those meeting the substantial presence test, residency generally begins on the first day of presence in the U.S. during the year.

**Exception:** Up to 10 days may be excluded if you had a closer connection to a foreign country and your tax home was there.
