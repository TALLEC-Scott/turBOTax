# IRA Recharacterizations

Recharacterization allows you to treat a contribution made to one type of IRA as if it had been made to a different type of IRA. This topic covers when and how to recharacterize contributions.

## What Is Recharacterization?

Recharacterization lets you "change your mind" about the type of IRA contribution you made:
- Traditional IRA contribution → Treat as Roth IRA contribution
- Roth IRA contribution → Treat as Traditional IRA contribution

**Key benefit:** Correct contribution mistakes or respond to changed circumstances.

## Important Limitation (Post-2017)

**Conversions cannot be recharacterized.**

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 eliminated recharacterization for Roth conversions made after December 31, 2017. You cannot:
- Convert Traditional IRA to Roth, then change your mind
- Recharacterize a rollover to a Roth IRA

**What you CAN still recharacterize:**
- Regular Traditional IRA contributions → Roth IRA
- Regular Roth IRA contributions → Traditional IRA

## Deadline for Recharacterization

**Due date of your tax return** for the year of the contribution:
- April 15 of the following year (or October 15 if you filed for extension)
- The contribution is treated as if made to the second IRA on the original contribution date

## How to Recharacterize

### Step 1: Notify Both Trustees
You must notify:
1. The trustee of the first IRA (original contribution)
2. The trustee of the second IRA (where contribution is moving)

**If same trustee:** Only one notification needed.

### Step 2: Required Information in Notification
Your notification must include:
1. Type and amount of contribution being recharacterized
2. Date the contribution was made to the first IRA
3. Year for which it was made
4. Direction to transfer the contribution plus net income
5. Name of both trustees
6. Any additional information needed for the transfer

### Step 3: Complete the Transfer
The trustee must transfer:
- The contribution amount, AND
- Any net income attributable to the contribution

**If there's a loss:** The net income transferred may be negative.

## Calculating Net Income

### Worksheet 1-3 (Pub 590-A)

```
1. Amount of IRA contribution to recharacterize        _______

2. Fair market value of IRA immediately before
   recharacterization (include any distributions,
   transfers, or recharacterizations)                 _______

3. Fair market value of IRA immediately before the
   contribution was made (include the contribution
   and any other contributions/transfers)             _______

4. Subtract line 3 from line 2                        _______

5. Divide line 4 by line 3 (decimal to 3 places)      _______

6. Multiply line 1 by line 5 = Net income             _______

7. Add lines 1 and 6 = Total amount to transfer       _______
```

### Simplified Method
Most IRA trustees/custodians calculate the net income for you automatically.

## Tax Reporting

### On Your Tax Return
Report the recharacterization on your return for the year the original contribution was made:
- Enter the contribution as if made to the second IRA
- Include a statement explaining the recharacterization
- Attach to your return

### Statement Must Include
1. IRA type (Traditional or Roth)
2. Dollar amount recharacterized
3. Date of original contribution
4. Date of transfer
5. Amount transferred (contribution + earnings/loss)
6. Amount transferred to the new IRA

## Examples of Recharacterization

### Example 1: Income Too High for Roth
**Situation:** You contributed $7,000 to a Roth IRA, but your income ended up exceeding the Roth IRA limits.

**Solution:** Recharacterize as a Traditional IRA contribution:
- Transfer $7,000 plus any earnings to Traditional IRA
- May or may not be deductible (see [[IRA Deductions and Phaseouts]])

### Example 2: Deduction Better Than Expected
**Situation:** You contributed to a Roth IRA, but your income was lower than expected, making a Traditional IRA deduction valuable.

**Solution:** Recharacterize Roth contribution as Traditional:
- Transfer to Traditional IRA
- Claim the deduction if eligible

### Example 3: Change of Mind
**Situation:** You contributed to Traditional IRA, but now prefer tax-free Roth distributions.

**Solution:** Recharacterize as Roth contribution:
- Transfer to Roth IRA
- Not taxable (was already taxed)
- Earnings go to Roth IRA

## Special Situations

### Multiple Contributions
If you made multiple contributions, you can recharacterize any portion. Specify which contribution(s) are being recharacterized.

### Loss on Contribution
If the contribution lost value:
- Net income calculation results in negative amount
- You transfer LESS than the original contribution
- Example: $7,000 contribution now worth $6,500; transfer $6,500 to second IRA

### Traditional IRA Mistakenly Moved to SIMPLE IRA
You CAN recharacterize an amount mistakenly rolled over from a Traditional IRA to a SIMPLE IRA.

### Excess Contributions
You can only recharacterize **actual contributions** made:
- Cannot recharacterize "applied" excess contributions from prior years
- Must be timely with respect to the year the contribution was made

### Employer Contributions
You CANNOT recharacterize employer contributions under SEP or SIMPLE plans.

## What You Cannot Recharacterize

1. **Roth Conversions (post-2017)** - Cannot undo a conversion
2. **Rollovers from employer plans to Roth IRA** - Cannot recharacterize
3. **Employer SEP/SIMPLE contributions** - Cannot recharacterize
4. **Applied excess contributions** - Cannot recharacterize after applying to future year

## Recharacterization vs. Rollover

| Aspect | Recharacterization | Rollover |
|--------|-------------------|----------|
| Effect on contribution limit | None - treated as original contribution | Counts toward limit |
| Once-per-year rule | Does not count as rollover | Subject to once-per-year rule |
| Timing | By due date of return | 60 days from distribution |
| Availability | Regular contributions only | Any IRA funds |

## Summary

Recharacterization is a valuable tool for:
- Correcting contributions made to wrong IRA type
- Responding to unexpected income changes
- Optimizing your tax situation

**Key reminders:**
- Deadline is your tax return due date (including extensions)
- Works for regular contributions only, not conversions
- Must include net earnings (or losses) in the transfer
- Report on your tax return with explanatory statement
