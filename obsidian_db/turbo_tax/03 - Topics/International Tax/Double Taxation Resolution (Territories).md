# Double Taxation Resolution (Territories)

## Overview

A mutual agreement procedure exists to settle cases of double taxation between the United States and each U.S. territory. This process helps resolve situations where the same income may be taxed by both jurisdictions.

## What Is Double Taxation?

Double taxation occurs when:
1. Both the United States and a territory claim the right to tax the same income
2. The taxpayer has paid or may owe tax to both jurisdictions on the same income

## When Double Taxation May Occur

### Common Situations

| Situation | Potential Issue |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Joint returns with spouses in different jurisdictions | Unclear which jurisdiction has primary taxing right |
| Income sourced to both jurisdictions | Allocation disputes |
| Residency status disputes | Both claim individual as resident |
| Year of move timing | Both claim income in transition year |
| Non-bona fide residents | Territory tax plus U.S. tax on same income |

### Territory-Specific Issues

**CNMI, Guam, USVI**: Generally avoid double taxation through the mirror tax system and filing Form 5074 or Form 8689 to allocate income.

**American Samoa, Puerto Rico**: May have overlapping claims when:
- Income is partially territory-sourced and partially U.S.-sourced
- Taxpayer has both territory and U.S. source income
- Determining proper allocation of deductions

## Mutual Agreement Procedure

### How It Works

1. **Request**: Taxpayer submits request for assistance
2. **Review**: IRS and territory tax authority jointly review the case
3. **Agreement**: Authorities attempt to reach mutual agreement
4. **Resolution**: Taxpayer notified of resolution

## Prevention Strategies

### Proper Filing

- Determine residency status correctly using the [[Bona Fide Residence Test]]
- File with the correct jurisdiction based on status
- Use allocation forms when required (Form 5074, Form 8689)

### Foreign Tax Credit

If taxed by both jurisdictions on the same income:
- Claim credit for territory taxes paid on U.S. return (Form 1116)
- Reduce credit for taxes attributable to excluded income

### Documentation

Maintain records showing:
- Days of presence in each jurisdiction
- Source of income
- Tax payments to each jurisdiction
- Residency-related actions (voter registration, driver's license, etc.)
