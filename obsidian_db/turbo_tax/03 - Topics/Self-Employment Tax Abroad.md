# Self-Employment Tax Abroad

## Overview

Self-employment tax is a separate tax from income tax that funds Social Security and Medicare. For U.S. citizens and resident aliens abroad, self-employment tax obligations continue regardless of where you live or work.

**Critical Point:** The [[Foreign Earned Income Exclusion]] does NOT reduce self-employment tax liability. You must pay SE tax on your net self-employment earnings even if you exclude all income from income tax.

## Who Must Pay

You must pay self-employment tax if:
- You are a self-employed U.S. citizen or resident alien
- Your net earnings from self-employment are **$400 or more**

This applies whether you live in the United States or abroad.

## Self-Employment Tax Rate

The self-employment tax rate is:

| Component | Rate | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Social Security | 12.4% | On net earnings up to wage base limit |
| Medicare | 2.9% | On all net earnings |
| Additional Medicare | 0.9% | On earnings above threshold |

**Wage Base Limit:** Check IRS.gov for current year limit (adjusted annually for inflation).

### Additional Medicare Tax
If your net earnings from self-employment exceed:
- $200,000 (Single, Head of Household)
- $250,000 (Married Filing Jointly)
- $125,000 (Married Filing Separately)

You must pay an additional 0.9% Medicare tax (file Form 8959).

## FEIE Does NOT Apply to SE Tax

**Important:** Income excluded under the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion is STILL subject to self-employment tax.

### Example
You are self-employed abroad and earn $150,000 in net profit. You qualify for and claim the FEIE, excluding $120,000 from income tax.

**Income Tax:** Only on $30,000 (after exclusion)
**SE Tax:** On FULL $150,000 (exclusion doesn't apply)

## Effect on Other Tax Benefits

If you claim the FEIE, you CANNOT claim:
- [[Earned Income Credit]]
- Additional Child Tax Credit (refundable portion)
- Foreign tax credit on excluded income

However, you CAN claim:
- Foreign tax credit on non-excluded self-employment income
- Deductions related to self-employment

## Social Security Agreements (Totalization Agreements)

The U.S. has bilateral social security agreements (totalization agreements) with many countries to eliminate dual coverage and dual contributions.

### How They Work

| Your Situation | Coverage |
|----------------|----------|
| Self-employed, resident in U.S. | U.S. Social Security only |
| Self-employed, resident in treaty country | Usually foreign system only |
| Self-employed, resident in non-treaty country | U.S. SE tax applies |

### Benefits of Totalization Agreements
- Avoid paying into two systems
- Coverage under one system
- Benefit eligibility can combine credits from both countries

### Certificate of Coverage
If your self-employment earnings should be exempt from foreign social security tax, request a certificate of coverage from the U.S. Social Security Administration.

**Request online:** SSA.gov/international/CoC_link.html

### Countries with Totalization Agreements
Include: Australia, Canada, France, Germany, Japan, South Korea, Mexico, United Kingdom, and many others.

## Clergy Members

Members of the clergy are treated as self-employed for SE tax purposes. However, you may be eligible for exemption:

- File Form 4361 for exemption based on religious opposition to public insurance
- Exemption applies to ministerial duties only

See Pub 517 for detailed guidance.

## Church Employees

If employed by a U.S. church or qualified church-controlled organization that is exempt from Social Security:
- You must pay self-employment tax if wages are $108.28 or more
- You may qualify for exemption as a member of a recognized religious sect

## U.S. Territory Business Income

If you own and operate a business in a U.S. territory (Puerto Rico, Guam, CNMI, American Samoa, or USVI):
- You must pay SE tax on net earnings of $400 or more
- This applies even if the income is exempt from U.S. income tax

If you don't have to file Form 1040 with the U.S., use **Form 1040-SS** to report SE tax.

## Calculating and Paying SE Tax

### Form Required
File **Schedule SE (Form 1040)** with your tax return.

### Estimated Tax Payments
If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in taxes (including SE tax), you must make estimated tax payments.

**Payment Options:**
- Form 1040-ES (by mail)
- Electronic payments via IRS.gov/Payments

### Foreign Electronic Payments
For electronic payment options available abroad, see IRS.gov/Individuals/International-Taxpayers/Foreign-Electronic-Payments-Tax-Type-Codes

## Deductible Portion

You can deduct half of your self-employment tax when calculating:
- Adjusted gross income (AGI)
- Net earnings from self-employment

This deduction is taken on Form 1040, Schedule 1.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Thinking FEIE eliminates SE tax** - It doesn't
2. **Not making estimated tax payments**
3. **Ignoring totalization agreement benefits**
4. **Failing to file Schedule SE**
5. **Not deducting the employer-equivalent portion**
