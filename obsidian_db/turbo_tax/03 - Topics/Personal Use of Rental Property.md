# Personal Use of Rental Property

## Overview

Personal use of rental property triggers special rules that can limit your deductions. Understanding what counts as personal use and how it affects your taxes is essential for property owners who use their rentals themselves or let family members use them.

## What Counts as Personal Use

A day of personal use includes any day the dwelling unit is used by:

### 1. The Owner
- You or any co-owner
- Exception: Rented to another owner as their main home under shared equity financing agreement

### 2. Family Members
Includes anyone related to you OR any co-owner:
- Spouse
- Siblings and half-siblings
- Parents and grandparents
- Children and grandchildren
- Exception: If family member uses it as their **main home** and pays **fair rental price**

### 3. Reciprocal Arrangements
Anyone using the property under an arrangement that lets you use their dwelling unit.

### 4. Below-Market Rentals
Anyone using the property at **less than fair rental price**.

## Shared Equity Financing Agreement

An agreement where two or more persons acquire undivided interests (50+ years) in a dwelling unit, and one or more co-owners occupy the unit as their main home upon paying rent to other co-owner(s).

**Special rule**: When a co-owner lives in the property under this arrangement and pays fair rent, it's NOT personal use by the other owners.

## Personal Use While Renting

If you rent the property at fair rental price but also use it personally that same day:

- **For expense allocation**: It's a **rental day**
- **For home determination**: It's a **personal day**

This distinction matters when applying the vacation home rules.

## Main Home Definition

When determining if a family member uses the property as their main home:
- If they have more than one home, their main home is where they live most of the time
- They must pay a fair rental price to avoid counting as personal use

## Donation of Use to Charity

If you donate use of your property to a charitable organization and the organization sells the use at a fundraiser, the days the "purchaser" uses the property count as **your personal use**.

## Implications of Personal Use

### Less Than 15 Rental Days
If you rent for fewer than 15 days:
- All rental income is **tax-free**
- Property is considered a personal residence
- Deduct mortgage interest and taxes on Schedule A

### More Than 15 Rental Days + Limited Personal Use
If personal use is ≤ 14 days OR ≤ 10% of rental days:
- Property is NOT a home
- Report all rental income and expenses on Schedule E
- Losses can offset other income (subject to passive rules)

### More Than 15 Rental Days + Excessive Personal Use
If personal use exceeds both 14 days AND 10% of rental days:
- Property IS a home
- Deductions limited to rental income
- Expenses allocated in specific order
- Carry forward excess deductions

## Examples

### Example 1: Co-owner Use
You and a neighbor co-own a beach condo. The neighbor uses it for 2 weeks; you don't use it at all.
- **Result**: Both of you have 14 days of personal use (neighbor's use counts for both owners)

### Example 2: Family Member
You rent your rental house to your son at fair market rent. He lives there as his main home.
- **Result**: NOT personal use (he's paying fair rent and it's his main home)

### Example 3: Below-Market Rent
You rent to your sister for $500/month when fair market rent is $1,500/month.
- **Result**: All days she uses the property are personal use (less than fair rental price)

## Records to Keep

Track and document:
- Dates of all personal use
- Who used the property
- Their relationship to you
- Rent paid (if any)
- Fair market rental value
