# Independent Contractor

## Overview

An independent contractor is a person who performs services for others but is not an employee. Per [[Pub 334 - Tax Guide for Small Business]], independent contractors are self-employed and report income on [[Schedule C (Form 1040)]].

## Definition

An individual is an independent contractor if the payer has:
- **Right to control only the result** of the work
- **NOT the method** of how the work is done

This contrasts with employees, where the employer controls both what is done and how.

## Common Independent Contractors

Per [[Pub 334 - Tax Guide for Small Business]]:
- Doctors
- Dentists
- Veterinarians
- Lawyers
- Accountants
- Contractors and subcontractors
- Public stenographers
- Auctioneers

**Gig Economy Workers:**
- Ride-share drivers (Uber, Lyft)
- Delivery drivers (DoorDash, Instacart)
- Freelancers on platforms (Upwork, Fiverr)

## Determining Worker Status

The IRS examines three categories per [[Pub 15-A - Employer's Supplemental Tax Guide]]:

### 1. Behavioral Control
Does the company control how the worker performs tasks?
- Instructions on when/where/how to work
- Training provided
- Evaluation methods
- More control = more likely employee

### 2. Financial Control
Does the worker have business-like characteristics?
- Unreimbursed expenses (independent contractor)
- Investment in equipment/facilities
- Services available to other customers
- Profit/loss opportunity
- More independence = more likely contractor

### 3. Type of Relationship
What do both parties expect?
- Written contracts
- Employee benefits provided?
- Permanency of relationship
- Services as key business activity
- More business-like = more likely contractor

**No single factor is determinative.** Consider the entire relationship.

## Tax Implications

### For Independent Contractors

**Income:**
- Report on Schedule C
- May receive [[Form 1099-NEC]] from payers (if paid $600+)
- Subject to [[Self-Employment Tax]]

**Deductions:**
- Claim business expenses on Schedule C
- No unreimbursed employee expense limitation

**Estimated Tax:**
- Pay quarterly via [[Form 1040-ES]]
- Must pay both income and SE tax

### For Employers (Hiring Independent Contractors)

**No Employment Taxes:**
- No Social Security/Medicare withholding
- No FUTA tax
- No income tax withholding

**Reporting:**
- File [[Form 1099-NEC]] for payments of $600+
- No W-2 required

**Risk of Misclassification:**
- If IRS determines worker is actually an employee
- Employer owes back employment taxes + penalties
- Interest on unpaid amounts

## Statutory Employees

Some workers classified by statute as employees despite being contractors-like:
- Corporate officers
- Agent/commission drivers
- Full-time life insurance agents
- Certain home workers

**Characteristics:**
- Box 13 checked on Form W-2
- Report on Schedule C (wages and expenses)
- Wages NOT subject to SE tax (FICA already withheld)

## Form 1099-NEC Reporting

### When Required
Payors must file [[Form 1099-NEC]] if paying $600+ for:
- Nonemployee compensation (Box 1)
- Cash payments for fish
- Payments to attorneys

### Due Dates
- **Recipient copy:** January 31
- **IRS filing:** January 31 (paper) or March 31 (electronic)

### Backup Withholding
If payee fails to provide TIN:
- Withhold 24% of payments
- Report on Form 1099-NEC, Box 4

## Self-Employment Tax

Independent contractors pay the full 15.3% SE tax:
- 12.4% Social Security (up to wage base)
- 2.9% Medicare (no limit)
- Additional 0.9% Medicare on high earnings

**Deduction:** Claim 50% of SE tax as adjustment to income on Schedule 1.

## Employee vs. Independent Contractor Comparison

| Factor | Employee | Independent Contractor |
|--------|----------|------------------------|
| Tax withholding | Employer withholds | Pays estimated tax |
| FICA tax | Split 50/50 | Pays 100% (deducts half) |
| Expense deduction | Limited (unreimbursed) | Full Schedule C deduction |
| Job benefits | May receive benefits | No benefits |
| Job security | More protections | Less protection |
| Schedule C | No (unless statutory) | Yes |
| Form W-2 | Yes | No |
| Form 1099 | No | Yes (if $600+) |

## Determining Your Status

If unsure whether you are an employee or independent contractor:
1. Review your work arrangement
2. Consider the three-factor test
3. Request determination using [[Form SS-8]]

**Form SS-8:** IRS reviews facts and issues determination letter (may take 6+ months).

## Common Questions

### Q: What if my employer says I'm a contractor but treats me like an employee?
**A:** File Form SS-8 for IRS determination. If reclassified as employee, employer owes back taxes. You may still owe income tax regardless of classification.

### Q: Do I need to make estimated tax payments?
**A:** Yes, if you expect to owe $1,000+ in taxes when you file. Pay quarterly to avoid underpayment penalties.

### Q: Can I be both an employee and independent contractor?
**A:** Yes. You can have W-2 employment and self-employment income simultaneously. File both W-2 income and Schedule C.
