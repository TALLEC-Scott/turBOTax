# Farm Recordkeeping

## Overview

Good recordkeeping is essential for preparing accurate tax returns, monitoring business progress, and supporting items reported to the IRS. Farmers must keep records to verify income, expenses, and credits claimed on their tax returns.

## Benefits of Good Records

### Business Management
- Monitor farm profitability
- Identify profitable crop and livestock enterprises
- Make informed management decisions
- Prepare accurate financial statements

### Tax Compliance
- Prepare accurate tax returns
- Support income and deductions claimed
- Facilitate IRS examination if needed
- Calculate depreciation and basis

## Types of Records to Keep

### Income Records
- Sales receipts and invoices
- Crop production records
- Livestock purchase and sales records
- Government payment documentation
- Form 1099s received

### Expense Records
- Canceled checks and bank statements
- Credit card statements
- Receipts and invoices
- Vendor statements
- Payroll records

### Asset Records
- Purchase documents
- Depreciation schedules
- Improvement costs
- Disposition records
- Trade-in documentation

## How Long to Keep Records

### General Rule
Keep records for **3 years** from the due date of the return or the date filed, whichever is later.

### Special Situations
- **Employment tax records**: 4 years after tax due or paid, whichever is later
- **Asset records**: Until period of limitations expires for year of disposition
- **Basis records**: As long as you own the property plus limitation period
- **Nontaxable exchanges**: Keep records for both properties

## Electronic Records

All requirements for hard copy records apply to electronic storage systems:
- Must be accessible to the IRS
- Must reproduce records legibly
- Must be retained as long as material
- Must meet Revenue Procedure 97-22 requirements

## Specific Recordkeeping Rules

### Travel Expenses
- Written records of time, place, and business purpose
- Receipts for lodging and transportation
- Standard meal allowance option available

### Vehicle Expenses
- Mileage log showing business use
- Total miles driven for year
- Date, destination, and purpose of trips

### Asset Records
- Cost basis
- Date placed in service
- Depreciation method used
- Business use percentage
- Disposition date and terms
