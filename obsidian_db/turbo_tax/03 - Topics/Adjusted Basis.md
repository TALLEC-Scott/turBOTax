# Adjusted Basis

## Overview

Adjusted basis is the result of increasing or decreasing your original cost basis over time. Before figuring gain or loss on a sale, exchange, or other disposition of property, or figuring allowable depreciation, you must usually make certain adjustments to the basis.

## Increases to Basis

Increase the basis of property by all items properly added to a capital account:

### Capital Improvements
- Putting an addition on your home
- Replacing an entire roof
- Paving your driveway
- Installing central air conditioning
- Rewiring your home

### Assessments for Local Improvements
- Water connections
- Sidewalks
- Roads

### Other Increases
- Legal fees for defending and perfecting title
- Zoning costs
- The cost of extending utility service lines
- Impact fees
- Capitalized value of redeemable ground rent
- Restoration of damaged property (casualty restoration)

## Decreases to Basis

The following items reduce the basis of property:

### Deductions
- Section 179 deduction
- Section 179D deduction for energy efficient commercial building property
- Depreciation previously allowed or allowable
- Amortization and depletion deductions

### Credits
- Certain vehicle credits
- Residential energy credits
- Investment credit taken
- Credit for employer-provided child care
- Disabled access credit (reduces amount capitalized)

### Other Decreases
- Casualty and theft losses (minus insurance reimbursement)
- Nontaxable corporate distributions
- Exclusion of subsidies for energy conservation measures
- Postponed gain from sale of home
- Easements
- Canceled debt excluded from income
- Gas-guzzler tax
- Adoption tax benefits

## Depreciation Rules

Decrease basis by the depreciation you deducted OR could have deducted under the method chosen. If you took less depreciation than allowed, decrease basis by the amount you could have taken.

### Special Depreciation Allowance
Unless you elect not to deduct it, decrease basis by the special depreciation allowance for property placed in service after September 10, 2001.

### Excess Depreciation
If you deducted more depreciation than you should have, decrease basis by:
- The depreciation you should have deducted, PLUS
- The part of excess depreciation that actually reduced your tax liability

## Example Calculation

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Original cost of building | $72,275 |
| Add: Improvements | $20,000 |
| Add: Repair of damages | $5,500 |
| **Subtotal** | **$97,775** |
| Less: Depreciation | ($14,526) |
| Less: Deducted casualty loss | ($5,000) |
| **Adjusted Basis** | **$78,249** |

## Partial Disposition of MACRS Property

When you sell or abandon a portion of a MACRS asset:
- Reduce adjusted basis by the adjusted basis of the portion sold/abandoned
- Make partial disposition election to recognize loss
- Report ordinary loss on Form 4797, Part II
