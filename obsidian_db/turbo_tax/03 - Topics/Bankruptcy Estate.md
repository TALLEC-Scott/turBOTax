# Bankruptcy Estate

## Overview

A bankruptcy estate is created when a bankruptcy petition is filed. For individuals under [[Chapter 7 Bankruptcy Tax|Chapter 7]] or [[Chapter 11 Bankruptcy Tax|Chapter 11]], the estate is treated as a **separate taxable entity** from the debtor.

The estate generally includes all of the debtor's legal and equitable interests in property as of the petition date. Exempt property and abandoned property are initially part of the estate but are subsequently removed. Excluded property is never included.

## Separate Taxable Entity Rules

When an individual files under Chapter 7 or 11:

| Filer | Form | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| Debtor | Form 1040 or 1040-SR | Individual income tax return |
| Estate (Trustee/DIP) | Form 1041 | Bankruptcy estate income tax return |

For [[Chapter 13 Bankruptcy Tax|Chapter 12]] and Chapter 13 cases, **no separate taxable entity** is created. The debtor files Form 1040 as usual.

## Income of the Bankruptcy Estate

### Chapter 7 Cases

Gross income includes:
- Income the debtor is entitled to under the Bankruptcy Code
- Income generated from estate property after commencement

**Not included:**
- Amounts received by debtor before petition date
- Income debtor earns after petition date

### Chapter 11 Cases

Gross income includes:
- Income from estate property
- **Post-petition earnings from debtor's services** (under IRC Section 1398(e)(1) and 11 U.S.C. Section 1115)

## Tax Attributes

The bankruptcy estate assumes the following tax attributes from the debtor (determined as of the first day of the debtor's tax year in which bankruptcy begins):

1. NOL carryovers
2. Carryovers of excess charitable contributions
3. Recovery of tax benefit items
4. Credit carryovers
5. Capital loss carryovers
6. Basis, holding period, and character of assets
7. Method of accounting
8. Passive activity loss and credit carryovers
9. Unused at-risk deductions
10. Other tax attributes per regulations

## Employer Identification Number (EIN)

The trustee or debtor-in-possession must obtain a separate EIN for the bankruptcy estate. **The debtor's SSN cannot be used** as the estate's EIN.

Apply for EIN:
- Online at IRS.gov/EIN (immediate issuance)
- By mail or fax using Form SS-4

## Transfer of Assets

| Transfer Type | Tax Consequence |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Debtor → Estate | Nontaxable disposition |
| Estate → Debtor (at termination) | Nontaxable disposition |
| Abandoned property to debtor | Nontaxable disposition |

The estate assumes the same basis, holding period, and character of transferred assets.

## Administrative Expenses

The estate can deduct administrative expenses including:
- Attorney fees
- Accounting fees
- Court costs

**Report on:** Schedule 1 (Form 1040), Part II, line 24z

These expenses are deductible in arriving at AGI and can only be claimed by the estate, never by the debtor.

## Administrative Expense Loss (AEL)

If administrative expenses exceed gross income, the excess is an AEL:
- May be carried back 3 years and forward 7 years
- Can only be carried to estate tax years (never debtor's years)
- Must be carried back to earliest year possible first
- NOL carrybacks are applied before AEL carrybacks

## Estate Termination

When the estate terminates, the debtor assumes any remaining tax attributes. However, the debtor does **not** assume the estate's AELs.
