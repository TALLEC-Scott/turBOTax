# Sale to Related Person

## Overview

Special rules apply to [[Installment Sales]] between related persons. These rules prevent tax avoidance through related-party transactions and may accelerate gain recognition.

## Sale of Depreciable Property

If you sell depreciable property to a related person, you generally **cannot use the installment method**. All payments are considered received in the year of sale.

### Definition: Depreciable Property

Any property the purchaser can depreciate, including:
- Equipment and machinery
- Buildings
- Rental property

### Who is "Related" for This Rule?

- A person and all controlled entities
- A taxpayer and any trust where they (or spouse) is a beneficiary
- An executor and estate beneficiary (except for pecuniary bequests)
- Partnerships with more than 50% common ownership

### Exception: No Tax Avoidance Purpose

You may use the installment method if you can show:
- No significant tax deferral benefit will be derived
- Tax avoidance was not a principal purpose of the sale

## Second Disposition Rule

If you sell property to a related person who then disposes of it, you may have to report remaining gain immediately.

### When It Applies

The second disposition rule applies when:
1. You sold property on installment method to a related person
2. The related person disposes of the property before paying in full
3. The disposition occurs within 2 years of your sale (marketable securities exempt)

### Amount to Report

Report the **lesser of**:
1. Amount realized on second disposition, or
2. Contract price on first disposition

**Minus:** Payments already received and reported

### Related Persons for Second Disposition Rule

Includes broader categories:
- Family members (spouse, siblings, ancestors, descendants)
- Partnerships and their partners
- Trusts and their beneficiaries/grantors
- Corporations with > 50% common ownership
- S corporations with > 50% common ownership
- Fiduciaries and beneficiaries of different trusts with same grantor
- Tax-exempt organizations and controlling individuals

## Example: Second Disposition

In 2024, you sold farmland to your child Adrian for $500,000:
- Your basis: $250,000
- Gross profit percentage: 50%
- 5 annual payments of $100,000

**2024:** Received $100,000, reported $50,000 gain

**2025:** Adrian sells property to unrelated party for $600,000
- Amount realized on second disposition: $600,000
- Lesser of (1) $600,000 or (2) $500,000 = $500,000
- Payments received: $200,000 (2024 + 2025)
- Remaining to report: $300,000

## Reporting Requirements

For sales to related persons, you must:
1. Complete [[Form 6252]] Part III for year of sale and 2 years after
2. Continue filing Form 6252 each year until obligation is paid
3. Report gain acceleration if second disposition occurs

## Exceptions to Second Disposition Rule

The rule does **not** apply if:
- The second disposition is a like-kind exchange or involuntary conversion
- Both dispositions are subject to the rule (chain of related party sales)
- The second disposition is to the U.S. government
- Marketable securities are involved (2-year limit doesn't apply)

## Common Questions

### Q: Does selling to my brother qualify as a related party sale?
**A:** Yes, for the second disposition rule. Brothers and sisters are related persons. However, the depreciable property restriction uses a narrower definition.

### Q: What if the related person holds the property for more than 2 years?
**A:** The second disposition rule only applies to dispositions within 2 years (except for marketable securities). After 2 years, normal installment rules apply.

### Q: Can I sell rental property to my child on installment?
**A:** Yes, but the second disposition rule applies. If your child sells the property within 2 years, you must report all remaining gain immediately.
