# Section 457 Plans

## Overview

Section 457 plans are deferred compensation plans for employees of state and local governments and certain tax-exempt organizations. These plans allow you to defer current income and the associated taxes until you receive the funds in retirement or separation from service.

## Types of Section 457 Plans

### Eligible Plans
- For state and local government employees
- For employees of tax-exempt organizations (501(c) organizations)
- No current taxation on deferred amounts
- Subject to contribution limits
- Protected from creditors

### Ineligible Plans
Plans that don't qualify as eligible include:
- Bona fide vacation, sick leave, compensatory time plans
- Severance pay plans
- Disability pay plans
- Death benefit plans
- Nonelective plans for independent contractors
- Plans maintained by churches
- Length of service award plans exceeding $7,500/year for volunteer firefighters

## Contribution Limits (2025)

| Limit Type | Amount |
|------------|--------|
| Elective deferral limit | $23,500 |
| Catch-up contribution (age 50+) | $7,500 |
| Special 3-year catch-up | Up to $47,000 |

### Special 3-Year Catch-Up
If permitted by the plan, in the 3 years before normal retirement age, you may contribute up to twice the normal limit to "catch up" on unused prior years' contributions.

## Taxation of Distributions

### Government Plans
- Taxed only when distributed
- Report on Form 1040, lines 5a and 5b
- Eligible for rollover to IRA or other qualified plans

### Tax-Exempt Organization Plans
- Taxed when distributed OR made available
- "Made available" means you have an unrestricted right to the funds
- May be subject to different timing rules

### Designated Roth Accounts
457(b) plans may offer designated Roth accounts:
- Contributions are included in income
- Qualified distributions are tax-free
- Same 5-year and age 59½ rules as other Roth accounts
- See [[Roth Contributions]] for details

## Rollovers

Amounts in 457 plans can be rolled over to:
- Traditional IRA
- Roth IRA (taxable conversion)
- 401(k) plan
- 403(b) plan
- Other governmental 457(b) plans

### Restrictions
- Only eligible plans can receive rollovers
- Non-governmental 457 plans have limited rollover options

## Loans

Loans from 457 plans may be available:
- Must meet plan requirements
- Subject to loan limits ($50,000 or 50% of vested balance)
- Must be repaid within 5 years (except for home purchase)
- Treated as distribution if not repaid properly

## Required Minimum Distributions

RMDs apply to governmental 457(b) plans:
- Must begin by April 1 after year you turn 73
- Failure to take RMD results in 25% excise tax
- See [[Required Minimum Distributions]] for details

## Key Differences from Other Plans

| Feature | 457(b) Plan | 401(k) Plan | 403(b) Plan |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Catch-up before retirement | Yes (3-year) | No | No |
| Early withdrawal penalty | No penalty | 10% before 59½ | 10% before 59½ |
| Loan availability | Plan dependent | Plan dependent | Plan dependent |
| Rollover flexibility | Broad | Broad | Broad |

## No Early Distribution Penalty

Unlike 401(k) and 403(b) plans, governmental 457(b) plans have **no 10% early distribution penalty**. You can take distributions at any age after separation from service without penalty.

## Key Rules

1. **Available to government and tax-exempt employees only**
2. **No 10% early distribution penalty for governmental plans**
3. **Special 3-year catch-up before normal retirement age**
4. **Tax-exempt plans taxed when made available**
5. **Rollovers to and from other qualified plans permitted**
