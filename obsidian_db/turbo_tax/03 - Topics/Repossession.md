# Repossession

## Overview

If you repossess property after making an [[Installment Sales|installment sale]], you must figure your gain or loss on the repossession and your basis in the repossessed property. The rules differ for **personal property** and **real property**.

## Requirements for Repossession Treatment

For repossession rules to apply:
1. Repossession must protect your security rights in the property
2. The installment obligation satisfied must be from the original sale
3. No additional consideration paid to buyer (unless contract provided or buyer defaulted)

## Personal Property Repossession

### Figuring Gain or Loss

**Gain/Loss = FMV of Repossessed Property − (Basis in Obligation + Repossession Costs)**

### Basis in Installment Obligation

If you used installment method for original sale:
**Basis = Unpaid Balance × (1 − Gross Profit Percentage)**

If you did NOT use installment method:
- Basis = Issue price increased by OID included in income, decreased by payments
- Or, amount realized increased by unstated interest, decreased by payments

### Basis in Repossessed Property

**Basis = FMV at Time of Repossession**

### Character of Gain or Loss

- Same character as gain/loss on the original sale
- If installment method used: same capital/ordinary character
- If not: gain is ordinary income; loss may be bad debt

## Real Property Repossession

Real property rules allow you to recover your original adjusted basis, essentially canceling the original sale's tax treatment.

### Figuring Taxable Gain

**Taxable Gain = Lesser of:**
1. (Payments received − Gain already reported), or
2. (Gross profit − Gain already reported − Repossession costs)

### Worksheet for Real Property

1. Total payments received before repossession
2. Total gain already reported as income
3. Gain on repossession (Line 1 − Line 2)
4. Gross profit on original sale
5. Repossession costs
6. Line 2 + Line 5
7. Line 4 − Line 6
8. Taxable gain = Lesser of Line 3 or Line 7

### Basis in Repossessed Real Property

**Basis = Adjusted Basis in Obligation + Taxable Gain + Repossession Costs**

Where:
**Adjusted Basis in Obligation = Unpaid Balance − (Unpaid Balance × Gross Profit %)**

## Example: Personal Property

You sold a piano for $1,500:
- Down payment: $300, monthly payments of $100
- Gross profit percentage: 40%
- After 4 payments, buyer defaults
- Unpaid balance: $800
- FMV of piano: $1,400
- Repossession costs: $75

**Calculation:**
1. Basis in obligation: $800 × (1 − 0.40) = $480
2. Total: $480 + $75 = $555
3. Gain: $1,400 − $555 = $845

## Example: Real Property

You sold land for $25,000:
- Your basis: $19,000
- Selling expenses: $1,000
- Gross profit: $5,000
- Gross profit percentage: 20%

After receiving $9,000 in payments ($1,800 gain reported), you repossess:
- Repossession costs: $500
- Unpaid balance: $16,000

**Taxable Gain:**
- Gain on repossession: $9,000 − $1,800 = $7,200
- Lesser of $7,200 or ($5,000 − $1,800 − $500) = $2,700

**New Basis:**
- Adjusted basis in obligation: $16,000 × (1 − 0.20) = $12,800
- New basis: $12,800 + $2,700 + $500 = $16,000

## Special Rules

### Not a Repossession

The repossession rules don't apply if:
- Buyer puts property up for sale and you repurchase it
- You receive property in satisfaction of debt (not as foreclosure)

### Main Home

Special rules apply if you repossess property that was your main home before the sale. See Regulations Section 1.1038-2.

## Common Questions

### Q: Can I deduct repossession costs?
**A:** Repossession costs are added to your basis in the repossessed property or included in the gain calculation—they are not separately deductible expenses.

### Q: What if the FMV of repossessed property is less than my basis in the obligation?
**A:** You have a loss. For installment sales, this is a bad debt (business or nonbusiness depending on original property use).

### Q: Does it matter how I repossess (foreclosure vs. voluntary surrender)?
**A:** No. The repossession rules apply regardless of how you reclaim the property, as long as the obligation is satisfied.
