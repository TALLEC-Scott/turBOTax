# Employee Classification

## Overview

Correctly classifying workers as employees or independent contractors is critical for tax compliance. Misclassification can result in penalties, back taxes, and legal liability.

## Common Law Employee Test

Under common law rules, a worker is an employee if the business has the **right to control**:
1. **What work** will be done
2. **How** the work will be done

### Key Factors (No Single Factor Controls)

#### Behavioral Control
- Instructions on when/where to work
- Training provided by business
- Detailed instructions on how to perform tasks
- Evaluation systems measuring details of performance

#### Financial Control
- Worker's investment in equipment
- Unreimbursed expenses
- Services available to other businesses
- How worker is paid (hourly vs. project-based)
- Opportunity for profit or loss

#### Type of Relationship
- Written contracts
- Employee benefits provided
- Permanency of relationship
- Services as key business activity

## Statutory Employees

Certain workers are treated as employees by statute, regardless of common law status:

| Category | Conditions |
|----------|------------|
| Driver | Distributes food/beverages, laundry, or dry cleaning for you |
| Life insurance agent | Sells life insurance or annuity contracts |
| Home worker | Works on goods/materials you provide |
| Traveling salesperson | Sells merchandise for resale or use in business |

**Tax treatment:** FICA taxes required; income tax withholding optional; FUTA required for some.

## Statutory Nonemployees

Treated as independent contractors by statute:

### Direct Sellers
- Sell consumer products to buyers for resale or use in homes
- Paid based on sales commissions

### Licensed Real Estate Agents
- Licensed real estate brokers/agents
- Paid based on sales output

### Qualified Real Estate Agents
- Meet certain qualification requirements
- Treated as nonemployees for FICA and FUTA

## Independent Contractors (Form 1099-NEC)

Workers who are in business for themselves:
- Control their own work methods
- May work for multiple clients
- Responsible for their own taxes
- Receive Form 1099-NEC for payments $600+ ($2,000+ starting 2026)

## Family Employees

### Children Under 18
Working for parent's trade or business:
- **Exempt from FICA** (social security and Medicare)
- **Exempt from FUTA**
- Subject to income tax withholding if wages are sufficient

### Children 18-20
Working for parent's trade or business:
- **Exempt from FUTA**
- Subject to FICA and income tax withholding

### Spouse
Working for spouse's business:
- Subject to FICA
- **Exempt from FUTA**
- Subject to income tax withholding

### Parent Working for Child
- Subject to FICA, FUTA, and income tax withholding

**Exception:** Family employment rules don't apply to corporations or partnerships (unless family members are partners).

## Consequences of Misclassification

### Employer Penalties
- Back taxes (FICA, FUTA, income tax withholding)
- Penalties for failure to withhold
- Interest on unpaid taxes
- Possible fraud penalties if intentional

### Worker Impact
- Self-employment tax liability
- Missed employment benefits
- No unemployment insurance coverage

## Safe Harbor (Section 530)

Employers may claim relief from employment tax liability if they:
1. Had reasonable basis for treating workers as contractors
2. Consistently treated similarly situated workers as contractors
3. Filed required Forms 1099 for workers
