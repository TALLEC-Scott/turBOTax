# Like-Kind Exchange

## Overview

A **like-kind exchange** under IRC Section 1031 allows taxpayers to defer recognition of gain on the exchange of property held for productive use in a trade or business or for investment. Beginning January 1, 2018, this treatment applies only to **real property**.

## Key Changes (Post-2017)

After December 31, 2017, Section 1031 only applies to:
- Real property held for use in a trade or business
- Real property held for investment
- NOT real property held primarily for sale (dealer property)
- NOT personal property (equipment, vehicles, artwork, etc.)

## Interaction with Installment Sales

Like-kind exchanges can involve [[Installment Sales]] in several ways:

### Boot Received in Exchange

If you receive "boot" (non-like-kind property or cash) in an exchange:
- The exchange portion is tax-deferred under Section 1031
- Boot is taxable and may qualify for installment treatment if:
  - It's an installment obligation
  - Payments are received after the tax year

### Installment Sale Followed by Exchange

If you sell property on installment and later exchange the installment obligation:
- The exchange of the obligation may qualify under Section 1031
- Special rules for what constitutes like-kind for obligations

## Requirements for Like-Kind Exchange

### Property Requirements

| Requirement | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Like-kind** | Both properties must be real property of like nature |
| **Held for use/investment** | Not personal-use, not held for sale |
| **Same taxpayer** | The same taxpayer must give and receive property |

### Timing Requirements

| Requirement | Time Limit |
|-------------|------------|
| **Identification period** | 45 days from transfer of relinquished property |
| **Exchange period** | 180 days from transfer (or tax return due date) |

## Boot in Like-Kind Exchanges

Boot includes:
- Cash received
- Net liability relief (liabilities assumed minus liabilities taken)
- Non-like-kind property received

### Installment Obligation as Boot

If you receive an installment obligation as boot:
- The obligation's FMV is boot
- If seller takes back financing, installment sale rules may apply to the boot portion

## Deferred Gain in Exchange

In a like-kind exchange:
1. **Realized gain** = FMV of property received + Boot received − Adjusted basis
2. **Recognized gain** = Lesser of realized gain or boot received
3. **Deferred gain** = Realized gain − Recognized gain

The deferred gain reduces the basis in the new property.

## Basis of Replacement Property

**Basis = FMV − Deferred Gain + Additional Cash Paid**

This "carryover basis" ensures the deferred gain is recognized when the replacement property is later sold.

## Common Questions

### Q: Can I exchange my rental property for another rental property?
**A:** Yes, if both are held for investment or productive use in a trade or business, they qualify for like-kind exchange treatment.

### Q: Can I still do a like-kind exchange of equipment?
**A:** No. After December 31, 2017, personal property no longer qualifies for Section 1031 treatment.

### Q: What if I sell my property and then buy another?
**A:** This is not a like-kind exchange. However, you may be able to use the [[Installment Sales]] method if you receive payments after the year of sale.
