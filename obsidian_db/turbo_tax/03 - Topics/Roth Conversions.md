# Roth Conversions

A Roth conversion transfers funds from a Traditional IRA (or other eligible retirement plan) to a Roth IRA, converting tax-deferred money to tax-free money.

## What Is a Roth Conversion?

A Roth conversion is a taxable event where you:
1. Withdraw money from a Traditional IRA or employer plan
2. Contribute it to a Roth IRA within 60 days (or via direct transfer)
3. Include the taxable amount in your gross income for the year

**Key benefit:** Future growth and qualified distributions are tax-free.

## Eligibility

### No Income Limits
Unlike Roth IRA contributions, **anyone can convert** regardless of income level.

### No Age Limits
No minimum or maximum age for conversions.

### Account Types You Can Convert From
- Traditional IRA (including SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA)
- 401(k) plans
- 403(b) plans
- 457(b) governmental plans
- Other qualified retirement plans

## Conversion Methods

### 1. Rollover (60-Day Rule)
- Receive distribution from Traditional IRA/plan
- Contribute to Roth IRA within 60 days
- **Risk:** If you miss the deadline, taxable distribution + possible penalties

### 2. Trustee-to-Trustee Transfer (Recommended)
- Direct transfer between financial institutions
- No 60-day risk
- No 20% withholding
- Preferred method for most situations

### 3. Same Trustee Transfer
- Both accounts at same institution
- Can simply "redesignate" the Traditional IRA as a Roth IRA
- Simplest administrative method

## Tax Consequences

### Income Inclusion
You must include in gross income:
- Pre-tax contributions
- All earnings

**Not included:** Nondeductible contributions (your basis)

### Tax Rates
- Ordinary income tax rates (not capital gains rates)
- Consider impact on tax bracket

### Timing
- Taxed in the year of conversion
- No deferral options

### Withholding Considerations
- Withholding from conversion may apply if distributed to you first
- Consider increasing withholding or making estimated payments

## When to Convert

### Ideal Situations
1. **Low-income year** - In lower tax bracket
2. **Before large income events** - RMDs, Social Security taxation
3. **Before tax rate increases** - Lock in current rates
4. **Have cash for taxes** - Can pay conversion tax from outside funds

### Avoid Converting When
1. **High-income year** - Would push into higher bracket
2. **Near-term distribution needed** - Won't benefit from tax-free growth
3. **Can't pay taxes from outside funds** - Reduces conversion benefit
4. **Will need the money within 5 years** - Distribution penalties may apply

## Pro-Rata Rule

**Critical for those with existing Traditional IRAs:**

When you have both deductible and nondeductible contributions in Traditional IRAs, the pro-rata rule applies:

### Formula
```
Nondeductible contributions
--------------------------- × Amount converted = Tax-free portion
Total Traditional IRA balance
```

### Example
- Traditional IRA balance: $100,000
- Nondeductible contributions (basis): $20,000
- Conversion amount: $40,000

Tax-free portion: $20,000 ÷ $100,000 × $40,000 = $8,000
Taxable portion: $32,000

### Avoiding Pro-Rata Issues
1. Roll pre-tax Traditional IRA to 401(k) (if plan allows)
2. Convert only from accounts with no pre-tax money
3. Consider doing conversions before building up basis

## Recharacterization No Longer Available

**Important:** Conversions completed after 2017 **cannot be recharacterized**.

Before 2018, you could "undo" a conversion if the market dropped or your tax situation changed. This is no longer allowed.

## Five-Year Rule for Conversions

Each conversion has its own 5-year clock for the 10% early distribution penalty:

- **Purpose:** Determines if conversion amount is subject to 10% penalty
- **Starts:** January 1 of the year of conversion
- **Applies if:** Under age 59½ when taking distribution from Roth IRA
- **Per conversion:** Each conversion has its own 5-year period

### Order of Distribution from Roth IRA
1. Regular contributions (always tax/penalty-free)
2. Conversion contributions (5-year rule applies per conversion)
3. Earnings (qualified distribution rules apply)

## Conversion Planning Strategies

### Multi-Year Conversions
- Convert over several years to stay in lower tax brackets
- Avoid large one-time income spike

### Tax Bracket Management
- Fill up current bracket before moving to next
- Consider state income tax implications

### Year-End Planning
- Project full-year income before year-end
- Convert enough to reach top of current bracket

### Roth Ladder (Early Retirement)
- Convert during low-income years before Social Security/RMDs
- Create tax-free income for later years

## Special Conversion Rules

### From Employer Plans
- Use direct rollover to avoid 20% withholding
- Can specify which funds to convert (if plan allows)

### Required Minimum Distributions
- Cannot convert RMD amounts
- Must take RMD first, then convert remaining

### SIMPLE IRAs
- 2-year rule applies before conversion eligible
- After 2 years, same rules as Traditional IRA

## Backdoor Roth IRA

For high-income earners who can't contribute directly to Roth IRA:

### Strategy
1. Make nondeductible Traditional IRA contribution
2. Convert to Roth IRA

### Requirements for Success
1. No other Traditional IRA balance (pro-rata rule)
2. Convert soon after contribution (minimal earnings)
3. Report correctly on Form 8606

### Caution
The pro-rata rule makes this ineffective if you have pre-tax Traditional IRA balances.

## Reporting Requirements

### Form 8606
Required to report:
- Nondeductible contributions (Part I)
- Conversions (Part II)
- Distributions from Roth IRA (Part III)

### Form 1099-R
- Received from financial institution
- Reports distribution from Traditional IRA
- Code 2 or 7 in Box 7 indicates conversion

### Form 5498
- Received the following May
- Reports conversion contribution to Roth IRA
