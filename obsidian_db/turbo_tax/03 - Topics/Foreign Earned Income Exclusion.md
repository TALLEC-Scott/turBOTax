# Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE)

## Overview

The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion allows U.S. citizens and resident aliens living and working abroad to exclude a portion of their foreign earned income from U.S. taxation. This is one of the most significant tax benefits for Americans abroad.

**Maximum Exclusion:** For 2025, up to the annual inflation-adjusted amount per taxpayer (check IRS.gov/InflationAdjustment for current limits).

## Requirements

To claim the FEIE, you must meet ALL of the following:

### 1. Tax Home in a Foreign Country
Your [[Tax Home]] must be in a foreign country (or countries). Your tax home is generally your principal place of business, employment, or post of duty—not necessarily where your family home is located.

### 2. Foreign Earned Income
You must have earned income from personal services performed in a foreign country. This includes:
- Wages and salaries
- Self-employment income
- Bonuses
- Professional fees

**NOT eligible for FEIE:**
- Investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains)
- Pension income
- Gambling winnings
- Amounts paid by the U.S. Government to its employees

### 3. Qualification Test
You must meet EITHER the [[Bona Fide Residence Test]] OR the [[Physical Presence Test]].

## Bona Fide Residence Test

You qualify if you are:
1. A U.S. citizen, OR
2. A U.S. resident alien who is a citizen or national of a country with which the U.S. has an income tax treaty

AND you are a bona fide resident of a foreign country for an **uninterrupted period that includes an entire tax year** (January 1 - December 31 for calendar year taxpayers).

### Factors Determining Bona Fide Residence
- Intention to stay (vs. temporary assignment)
- Establishment of home
- Economic ties to the foreign country
- Social and family connections
- Duration of stay

**Note:** Living in a foreign country for one year does not automatically make you a bona fide resident.

## Physical Presence Test

You qualify if you are physically present in a foreign country (or countries) for **330 full days** during any 12 consecutive months.

### Key Rules
- The 330 days need NOT be consecutive
- Days of arrival and departure generally don't count as full days
- The test is purely mathematical—intentions and residence type don't matter
- Travel days over international waters don't count

### Example
You arrive in Germany on March 1, 2025, and leave on February 28, 2026. During this period, you were present in foreign countries for 340 full days. You qualify under the physical presence test.

## Claiming the Exclusion

### Form Required
File **Form 2555** (Foreign Earned Income) with your Form 1040.

### Election Requirements
- Must make a valid election by attaching Form 2555 to your return
- Can be filed with original return or amended return (Form 1040-X)
- Once elected, remains in effect for future years unless revoked

### Married Couples
If both spouses work abroad and meet the tests, each can claim the full exclusion amount—potentially doubling the benefit.

## Limitations and Interactions

### What FEIE Does NOT Affect
- [[Self-Employment Tax Abroad|Self-employment tax]]—still due on excluded income
- State income taxes (varies by state)
- Your obligation to file a tax return

### Ineligibility for Other Benefits
If you claim FEIE, you CANNOT claim in the same year:
- [[Earned Income Credit]]
- Additional Child Tax Credit (refundable portion)
- Foreign tax credit on excluded income

### Tax Calculation
You must figure tax on non-excluded income using the tax rates that would have applied had you NOT claimed the exclusion. Complete the Foreign Earned Income Tax Worksheet in Form 1040 instructions.

## Revoking the Exclusion

You may revoke your FEIE election by:
1. Attaching a statement to your return for the first year you don't wish to claim it
2. Claiming the foreign tax credit, earned income credit, or additional child tax credit (considered automatic revocation)

**Caution:** If you revoke and later wish to re-elect FEIE within 5 years, you must request IRS approval.

## Exceptions for Adverse Conditions

If you must leave a foreign country due to war, civil unrest, or similar adverse conditions, the minimum time requirements may be waived. The IRS publishes an annual list of qualifying countries in the Internal Revenue Bulletin.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not meeting the full tax year requirement** for bona fide residence test
2. **Counting partial days** for physical presence test
3. **Excluding income from U.S. Government employment** (not allowed)
4. **Forgetting self-employment tax** is still due on excluded income
5. **Missing the filing deadline** for claiming the exclusion
