# Basis of Home

## Overview

Basis is your starting point for figuring gain or loss when you sell your home. It's also used to figure depreciation if you use part of your home for business or rental purposes. Understanding basis is critical for tax planning.

## Why Basis Matters

- **Selling your home**: Gain = Amount realized - Adjusted basis
- **Business/rental use**: Basis determines depreciation deductions
- **Casualty losses**: Basis affects deductible losses

## Figuring Your Basis

### Cost as Basis (Purchase)

The basis of a home you bought is generally its **cost**, including:
- Down payment
- Any debt you assumed
- Most settlement and closing costs

**Items you CAN add to basis:**
- Abstract fees
- Legal fees (title search, sales contract, deed preparation)
- Recording fees
- Transfer or stamp taxes
- Surveys
- Owner's title insurance
- Amounts seller owes that you agree to pay (back taxes, recording fees, improvements)

**Items NOT added to basis:**
- Fire insurance premiums
- Charges for utilities before closing
- Rent before closing
- Mortgage-related fees (appraisal, credit report, loan assumption fees)
- Mortgage insurance premiums

### Construction

If you built your home, basis includes:
- Cost of land
- Cost of labor and materials
- Contractor fees
- Architect fees
- Building permits
- Utility connection charges
- Direct legal fees

**Note**: You cannot include the value of your own labor.

### Gift

If you received your home as a gift:
- **If donor's adjusted basis > FMV at gift**: Your basis = Donor's adjusted basis
- **If donor's adjusted basis ≤ FMV**: Your basis = Donor's adjusted basis + part of gift tax paid on net increase in value

Special rules apply when determining gain vs. loss on disposition.

See [[Pub 551 - Basis of Assets]] for detailed examples.

### Inheritance

Your basis in an inherited home is generally its **fair market value** on the date of the decedent's death (or alternate valuation date if elected).

If an estate tax return was filed, your basis is generally the value listed on the return.

### Property Transferred from Spouse

Basis is the same as your spouse's (or former spouse's) adjusted basis just before the transfer. See [[Pub 504 - Divorced or Separated Individuals]].

## Adjusted Basis

While you own your home, events can increase or decrease your original basis. The result is **adjusted basis**.

### Increases to Basis

| Item | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Improvements** | Additions, new roof, central A/C, paved driveway, fencing, rewiring |
| **Local assessments** | Streets, sidewalks, sewer systems (add to basis, not deductible) |
| **Restoration costs** | Costs to restore damaged property |

### Decreases to Basis

| Item | Notes |
|------|-------|
| **Insurance reimbursements** | For casualty losses |
| **Deductible casualty losses** | Uninsured losses |
| **Easement payments** | Payments received for rights-of-way |
| **Depreciation** | Allowed or allowable for business/rental use |
| **Energy conservation subsidies** | Utility subsidies excluded from income |
| **Adoption tax benefits** | Credits claimed for improvements |
| **Residential energy credits** | Credit reduces basis for qualifying improvements |

## Real Estate Taxes and Basis

### Taxes Paid by Seller for You

If the seller paid your share of real estate taxes (from date of sale forward) and you didn't reimburse them:
- **Reduce basis** by the amount paid for you
- You can still deduct those taxes

### Taxes You Paid for Seller

If you paid the seller's share (up to date of sale) and weren't reimbursed:
- **Add to basis** (can't deduct as taxes)

## Improvements vs. Repairs

### Improvements (Add to Basis)

An improvement:
- Materially adds to the home's value, OR
- Considerably prolongs its useful life, OR
- Adapts it to new uses

**Examples**: Room additions, new roof, central A/C, fence, driveway paving, plumbing/wiring upgrades

### Repairs (Not Added to Basis)

A repair keeps the home in ordinary, efficient operating condition without:
- Adding value, OR
- Prolonging life

**Examples**: Repainting, fixing gutters/floors, patching leaks, replacing broken windows

**Exception**: Repairs done as part of extensive remodeling/restoration are considered improvements.

## Recordkeeping

Keep records of:
- Purchase contract and settlement papers
- Receipts for improvements
- Form 1098 statements
- Property tax bills
- Any events affecting basis

Keep records for as long as you own the home plus the period of limitations after disposition.
