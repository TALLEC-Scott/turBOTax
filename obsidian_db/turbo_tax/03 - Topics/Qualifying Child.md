# Qualifying Child

A qualifying child is a dependent who meets five specific tests. Claiming a qualifying child can provide tax benefits including the [[Child Tax Credit]], [[Earned Income Credit]], and Head of Household filing status.

## Five Tests for a Qualifying Child

### 1. Relationship Test

The child must be your:
- Son or daughter (including adopted, foster, or stepchild)
- Brother, sister, half-brother, half-sister, stepbrother, or stepsister
- Descendant of any of the above (e.g., grandchild, niece, nephew)

**Adopted Child:** Always treated as your own child. Includes a child lawfully placed with you for legal adoption.

**Foster Child:** An individual placed with you by an authorized placement agency or court order.

### 2. Age Test

The child must be:

| Condition | Age Requirement |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Under 19 | Younger than you (or your spouse if filing jointly) |
| Student under 24 | Younger than you (or your spouse if filing jointly) |
| Permanently and totally disabled | Any age |

**Age 65 Rule:** For 2025, you are considered age 65 on the day before your 65th birthday (born before January 2, 1961).

**Student Definition:** Must be enrolled as a full-time student at a school during any 5 calendar months of the year. The 5 months don't need to be consecutive.

### 3. Residency Test

The child must have lived with you for **more than half the year**.

**Exceptions:**
- **Temporary absences** count as time lived with you (school, vacation, illness, business, military service, detention in juvenile facility)
- **Birth or death** during the year - treated as living with you the entire time they were alive
- **Adopted or foster children** - lived with you since placement
- **Kidnapped children** - treated as meeting residency test under certain conditions
- **Children of divorced/separated parents** - special rules apply

### 4. Support Test

The child must **NOT** have provided more than half of their own support for the year.

**What counts as support:**
- Food, shelter, clothing
- Education expenses
- Medical care
- Recreation and other necessities

**What doesn't count as support provided by the child:**
- Scholarships (for full-time students)
- Social Security benefits used for support (these count as third-party support)

### 5. Joint Return Test

The child must **NOT** file a joint return for the year.

**Exception:** If the child files a joint return only to claim a refund of withheld income tax or estimated tax paid, this test is still met.

## Citizen or Resident Test

The child must be:
- A U.S. citizen
- A U.S. resident alien
- A U.S. national, or
- A resident of Canada or Mexico

**Exception for Adopted Children:** If you are a U.S. citizen or national who adopted a child who isn't a U.S. citizen, resident alien, or national, the child meets this test if they lived with you as a member of your household all year.

## Qualifying Child of More Than One Person

If a child meets the tests to be a qualifying child of more than one person, **only one person** can claim the child.

### Tiebreaker Rules

| Situation | Who Can Claim the Child |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Only one person is the parent | The parent |
| Parents live together and file separately | The parent with higher AGI |
| Child lives with one parent more than the other | The parent with whom the child lived longer |
| Parents don't agree on who claims | The parent with higher AGI |
| Parent vs. nonparent | The parent (if higher AGI than nonparent) |
| Two nonparents | The person with higher AGI |

### Special Rule for Divorced or Separated Parents

The noncustodial parent can claim the child as a qualifying child if:
1. The parents are divorced, legally separated, or lived apart for the last 6 months
2. The child received over half their support from the parents
3. The child was in the custody of one or both parents for more than half the year
4. The custodial parent signs Form 8332 releasing the claim

**Important:** The noncustodial parent can claim the child for [[Child Tax Credit]], but NOT for [[Earned Income Credit]], Head of Household filing status, or dependent care benefits.

## Tax Benefits from a Qualifying Child

1. **[[Child Tax Credit]]** - Up to $2,000 per child under 17
2. **[[Earned Income Credit]]** - Increased credit amount
3. **Head of Household** filing status - Lower tax rates and higher standard deduction
4. **[[Child and Dependent Care Credit]]** - For childcare expenses
5. **Exclusion for [[Dependent Care Benefits]]** - Employer-provided childcare

## EIC-Specific Qualifying Child Rules

For the [[Earned Income Credit|Earned Income Credit (EIC)]], qualifying children have additional requirements:

### SSN Requirement
The child must have a **valid Social Security Number** issued on or before the due date of the return (including extensions). The SSN cannot be marked "Not valid for employment" unless immigration status has changed.

### Residency Must Be in U.S.
The child must have lived with you in the United States (50 states and D.C.) for more than half the year. This does NOT include Puerto Rico or U.S. territories.

### Self-Only EIC Option
If your qualifying child doesn't have a valid SSN, you may still qualify for a "self-only" EIC using the rules for taxpayers without a qualifying child.

See [[EIC Tiebreaker Rules]] for rules when multiple persons can claim the same child for EIC.
