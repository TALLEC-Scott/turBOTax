# Foreign Tax Credit

## Overview

The Foreign Tax Credit prevents double taxation by allowing U.S. taxpayers to claim a credit for income taxes paid to foreign countries. This credit directly reduces your U.S. tax liability dollar-for-dollar.

**Alternative:** You may also deduct foreign taxes as an itemized deduction instead of taking the credit, though the credit is usually more beneficial.

## Eligibility

You can claim the foreign tax credit if you:
1. Are a U.S. citizen or resident alien
2. Paid or accrued foreign income taxes
3. Have foreign source income
4. Choose to claim the credit

## What Taxes Qualify

### Qualifying Taxes
- Foreign income taxes
- Taxes on wages, salaries, and self-employment income
- Taxes on interest, dividends, and royalties
- Foreign taxes on business income

### NOT Qualifying
- Taxes paid on income excluded under [[Foreign Earned Income Exclusion]]
- Taxes paid on income excluded under [[Foreign Housing Exclusion and Deduction]]
- Taxes on income that is exempt under U.S. law
- Value-added taxes (VAT)
- Sales taxes
- Real estate taxes (unless on rental income)
- Luxury taxes
- Customs duties
- Taxes paid to countries on the State Sponsors of Terrorism list

## Credit vs. Deduction

| Approach | Benefit | Best When |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Credit** | Dollar-for-dollar reduction of U.S. tax | Most cases |
| **Deduction** | Reduces taxable income | Low tax bracket, limited foreign taxes |

**Note:** You cannot claim both a credit and deduction for the same taxes.

## Foreign Earned Income Exclusion Interaction

### Critical Rule
You **cannot** claim a foreign tax credit for taxes paid on income that you excluded under the [[Foreign Earned Income Exclusion]] or [[Foreign Housing Exclusion and Deduction]].

### Planning Consideration
For high earners whose foreign income exceeds the FEIE limit:
1. Use FEIE for the maximum excluded amount
2. Use foreign tax credit for remaining foreign income
3. This can be the optimal strategy in high-tax countries

## Form Required

File **Form 1116** (Foreign Tax Credit) to claim the credit.

### Exceptions (No Form 1116 Needed)
- Foreign taxes paid are $300 or less ($600 if married filing jointly)
- All foreign income is from interest/dividends reported on 1099s
- No carryback or carryforward is claimed

## Credit Limitation

Your foreign tax credit is limited to:

```
Foreign Tax Credit Limit = U.S. Tax × (Foreign Taxable Income ÷ Worldwide Taxable Income)
```

### Separate Category Limitations

Foreign income is divided into categories (baskets), and the limitation is calculated separately for each:

1. **General Category Income** - Wages, business income, interest
2. **Passive Category Income** - Dividends, interest, royalties, annuities
3. **Other Categories** - Section 901(j) income, certain oil-related income

### Example Calculation

- U.S. tax on worldwide income: $20,000
- Foreign source taxable income: $50,000
- Worldwide taxable income: $100,000
- Foreign taxes paid: $12,000

**Credit Limit:** $20,000 × ($50,000 ÷ $100,000) = **$10,000**

**Result:** You can claim a credit of $10,000. The remaining $2,000 can be carried back 1 year or forward 10 years.

## Carrybacks and Carryforwards

If your foreign taxes exceed the credit limit:
- **Carry back** 1 year (to the extent of limitation in that year)
- **Carry forward** 10 years

This ensures you don't lose the benefit of foreign taxes paid in high-tax years.

## Treaty Benefits

[[Tax Treaties]] may provide:
- Higher credit allowances
- Exemptions from foreign tax
- Reduced withholding rates
- Special provisions for certain types of income

## Choosing Between FEIE and FTC

| Factor | Favor FEIE | Favor FTC |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Foreign tax rate** | Lower than U.S. rate | Higher than U.S. rate |
| **Income level** | Below exclusion limit | Above exclusion limit |
| **Future planning** | Expect to return to U.S. | Long-term foreign residence |
| **Complexity** | Simpler filing | More complex |

**Important:** Once you choose the FEIE, you cannot claim FTC on excluded income. You must choose wisely.

## Self-Employment Tax

Foreign taxes paid on self-employment income may be credited against income tax, but NOT against [[Self-Employment Tax Abroad|self-employment tax]].

Social security totalization agreements may exempt you from dual coverage.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Claiming FTC on excluded income** - Not allowed
2. **Not filing Form 1116** when required
3. **Missing carryforward opportunities**
4. **Not separating income into proper categories**
5. **Claiming credit for non-income taxes** (VAT, sales tax)
