# Excess Benefit Transactions

## Overview

An excess benefit transaction is a transaction in which an economic benefit is provided by an applicable tax-exempt organization directly or indirectly to or for the use of a disqualified person if the value of the economic benefit exceeds the value of the consideration received. Section 4958 imposes excise taxes on these transactions.

## Applicable Tax-Exempt Organizations

Section 4958 applies to:
- Section 501(c) organizations exempt under 501(a)
- Section 527 political organizations
- Section 501(d) apostolic organizations

Does NOT apply to:
- Private foundations (covered by Section 4941 self-dealing rules)
- Certain governmental entities

## Disqualified Persons

A disqualified person is anyone who was, at any time during the 5-year period ending on the date of the transaction, in a position to exercise substantial influence over the organization:

### Per Se Substantial Influence
- Voting members of governing body
- Presidents, CEOs, executive directors
- Treasurers and chief financial officers

### Facts and Circumstances Test
May have substantial influence if:
- Family members of substantial influencers
- Substantial contributors
- Persons with managerial authority
- Corporations/partnerships controlled by above

## What Constitutes an Excess Benefit

### Compensation Arrangements
An excess benefit exists if compensation exceeds reasonable compensation for services rendered.

**Reasonable compensation factors**:
- All forms of cash and noncash compensation
- Taxable and nontaxable fringe benefits
- Deferred compensation
- Bonuses and severance payments

### Property Transactions
An excess benefit exists if property is sold or exchanged at other than fair market value.

### Revenue-Sharing Arrangements
May be excess benefit if organization shares revenue with disqualified person without adequate consideration.

## Excise Taxes (Section 4958)

### First-Level Taxes
| Tax Base | Tax Rate | Who Pays |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Excess benefit | 25% | Disqualified person |
| Excess benefit | 10% | Organization manager (if knowing) |

### Second-Level Taxes
| Tax Base | Tax Rate | Who Pays |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Remaining excess benefit | 200% | Disqualified person |
| N/A | Up to $10,000 | Organization manager |

### Maximum Penalty
- First-level tax on disqualified person: 25% of excess benefit
- Second-level tax (if not corrected): 200% of remaining excess benefit
- Manager penalty: Up to $10,000 per transaction

## Rebuttable Presumption of Reasonableness

Compensation is presumed reasonable if:
1. Approved by authorized body without conflict of interest
2. Authorized body relied on appropriate comparability data
3. Authorized body adequately documented its determination

### Safe Harbor for Small Organizations
Organizations with gross receipts under $1 million may use data from three comparable organizations in the same or similar communities.

## Correction of Excess Benefit Transactions

### Steps to Correct
1. Identify the excess benefit amount
2. Return benefit to organization
3. Make organization whole

### Timing
Must be corrected within taxable period:
- Before IRS notices of deficiency are mailed, or
- Before IRS assessment, whichever is earlier

## Disregarded Benefits

The following are NOT considered excess benefits:
- Nontaxable fringe benefits under Section 132
- Benefits to volunteers for payments of $75 or less
- Benefits provided as part of charitable class
- Transfers to governmental units for public purposes

## Initial Contract Exception

Section 4958 does not apply to fixed payments under an initial contract with a person who was not a disqualified person immediately before the contract.

## Reporting Requirements

Excess benefit transactions must be reported on:
- Form 990, Part VII and Schedule L
- Form 4720 for excise taxes

See [[Form 990 Filing Requirements]] for reporting details.
