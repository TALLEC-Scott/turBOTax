# Dependent Care Benefits

Benefits provided by an employer for the care of a [[Qualifying Person]] that may be excluded from income. Covered in [[Pub 503 - Child and Dependent Care Expenses]].

## What Are Dependent Care Benefits?

Dependent care benefits include:

1. Amounts employer paid directly to you or your care provider for care while you work
2. Fair market value of care in a daycare facility provided or sponsored by your employer
3. Pre-tax contributions you made under a dependent care flexible spending arrangement (FSA)

**Where to find on W-2:** Benefits are shown in Box 10 of Form W-2.

**Self-employed individuals:** Benefits from a qualified dependent care plan are deducted on Schedule C, E, or F rather than excluded from wages.

## Exclusion from Income

If your employer provides dependent care benefits under a **qualified plan**, you may be able to exclude these benefits from your income.

### Maximum Exclusion

For 2025, the maximum amount that can be excluded from income is:

| Filing Status | Maximum |
|---------------|---------|
| Single, Head of Household, MFJ | $5,000 |
| Married Filing Separately | $2,500 |

### Limitations on Exclusion

The amount you can exclude is limited to the **smallest of**:

1. Total dependent care benefits received during the year
2. Total qualified expenses incurred during the year
3. Your earned income
4. Your spouse's earned income
5. Maximum allowed under your plan ($5,000 / $2,500)

### Earned Income Definition

For the exclusion, earned income does NOT include dependent care benefits you receive.

## Claiming the Exclusion

To claim the exclusion:

1. Complete **Part III of Form 2441** (Child and Dependent Care Expenses)
2. The excluded amount reduces your taxable income

## Effect on the Child and Dependent Care Credit

If you exclude dependent care benefits from your income:

1. The excluded amount is NOT included in your [[Work-Related Expenses]]
2. Your dollar limit for the [[Child and Dependent Care Credit]] is **reduced** by the excluded amount

### Example: Reduced Dollar Limit

George has one child and pays $2,900 for childcare. His employer pays $1,000 directly to the provider under a qualified plan.

| Step | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Maximum for one qualifying person | $3,000 |
| Minus benefits excluded from income | -$1,000 |
| **Reduced dollar limit** | **$2,000** |

George can only use $2,000 of his $2,900 expenses for the credit.

### Example: Maximum Exclusion

Randall has two children and pays $6,000 for daycare. He elected $5,000 pre-tax through his employer's cafeteria plan.

| Step | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Maximum for two qualifying persons | $6,000 |
| Minus benefits excluded from income | -$5,000 |
| **Reduced dollar limit** | **$1,000** |

Randall can only use $1,000 of his $6,000 expenses for the credit.

## Combat Pay Election

You can elect to include nontaxable combat pay in earned income when figuring your exclusion or deduction, even if you elect NOT to include it for the [[Earned Income Credit]] or the [[Child and Dependent Care Credit]].

## Coordination with Other Benefits

### Medical Expense Deduction
Amounts excluded from income under a dependent care benefits plan CANNOT be used to claim a [[Medical and Dental Expenses|medical expense deduction]].

### Expenses That Don't Qualify for Credit
If you have expenses that exceed your dollar limit (after reduction), you cannot use the excess for the credit. However, expenses for a disabled qualifying person might qualify as medical expenses.

## Key Points

- **Complete Part III of Form 2441** to claim the exclusion
- The exclusion **reduces** your dollar limit for the credit
- Maximum exclusion: **$5,000** ($2,500 if MFS)
- Must have earned income to claim exclusion
- Self-employed taxpayers get a **deduction** instead of exclusion
