# Tax Treaty Benefits

## Overview

The United States has income tax treaties with over 60 countries that may provide:
- Reduced tax rates on specific income types
- Exemptions from U.S. taxation
- Special rules for students, teachers, and researchers

## Treaty Eligibility

To claim treaty benefits, you generally must be a **resident of the treaty country** under the treaty's residency article (not necessarily under U.S. tax law).

### Residency Tiebreaker
If you are a dual resident (resident of both U.S. and treaty country), the treaty's tiebreaker rules determine residency:
1. Permanent home
2. Center of vital interests
3. Habitual abode
4. Citizenship

## Common Treaty Benefits

### Personal Services Income

#### Dependent Personal Services (Employment)
Most treaties exempt employment income if:
- Present in U.S. ≤ 183 days in 12-month period
- Paid by foreign employer
- Income not borne by U.S. permanent establishment

#### Independent Personal Services
Some treaties exempt income if:
- No fixed base regularly available in U.S.
- Present in U.S. for limited period

**Note:** Many newer treaties use "business profits" article instead.

### Teachers and Researchers

Many treaties exempt teaching/research income for 2-3 years after arrival.

**Requirements:**
- Visiting primarily to teach or research
- At accredited educational institution
- Must be resident of treaty country immediately before arrival

**Time Limits:** Usually 2-3 years; varies by treaty

### Students, Apprentices, and Trainees

Treaty benefits may include:
- Exemption for remittances from abroad
- Exemption for scholarships/grants
- Limited exemption for compensation

**Time Limits:** Usually 4-5 years; varies by treaty

### Dividends, Interest, Royalties

| Income Type | Typical Treaty Rate |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Dividends | 5% - 15% (often 0% for direct investment) |
| Interest | 0% (often exempt) |
| Royalties | 0% - 10% |

### Capital Gains

Most treaties exempt gains from personal property sales. Real property gains generally remain taxable.

## Claiming Treaty Benefits

### For Withholding
File appropriate form with withholding agent:

| Situation | Form Required |
|-----------|---------------|
| Non-service income (dividends, interest) | [[Form W-8BEN]] |
| Personal services income | [[Form 8233]] |
| Students/teachers | Form 8233 + Appendix statement |

### On Tax Return
File [[Form 8833]] to disclose treaty-based return positions.

**When Form 8833 is Required:**
- Claiming residency under treaty tiebreaker with >$100,000 income
- Claiming treaty benefit that modifies IRC treatment of U.S. real property
- Claiming foreign tax credit not allowed under IRC

**When Form 8833 is NOT Required:**
- Reduced withholding rate on FDAP income
- Treaty benefits for personal services, pensions, students, teachers
- Income ≤ $10,000

## Resident Aliens and Treaties

Resident aliens may claim treaty benefits if the treaty has an exception to the "saving clause."

**Saving Clause:** Preserves U.S. right to tax citizens and residents as if treaty didn't exist.

**Common Exceptions:**
- Students and teachers (many treaties)
- Social security benefits (some treaties)
- Specific income types (varies by treaty)

### Example: China Treaty
Article 20 exception allows Chinese students to claim benefits even after becoming resident aliens.

## Treaty Income Calculation

1. Calculate tax on treaty income at reduced treaty rate
2. Calculate tax on non-treaty income at regular rate
3. Total cannot exceed tax calculated without treaty benefit

**Example:**
- Treaty dividends: $1,400 × 15% = $210
- Regular ECI tax: $2,705
- Total with treaty: $2,915 (vs. $3,125 without treaty)
