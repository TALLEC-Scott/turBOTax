# Accounting Methods

## Overview

An accounting method is a set of rules determining when and how income and expenses are reported. Per [[Pub 334 - Tax Guide for Small Business]] and [[Pub 538 - Accounting Periods and Methods]], you must use a consistent method that clearly reflects income.

Your accounting method includes:
- Overall method (cash, accrual, combination)
- Accounting treatment for specific items

## Types of Accounting Methods

### Cash Method

Most individuals and small businesses use the cash method.

**Income Recognition:**
- Report income when actually or constructively received
- Cash, checks, property, or services count as receipts

**Expense Deduction:**
- Deduct expenses when actually paid
- Payment by credit card = paid when charged

**Advantages:**
- Simple to maintain
- Income not taxed until received
- Control timing of deductions

**Limitations:**
- Must use accrual for inventory if applicable (unless small business taxpayer)
- Certain corporations and partnerships cannot use cash method

### Accrual Method

Required for businesses with inventory (unless small business taxpayer).

**Income Recognition (All Events Test):**
- Report income when all events have occurred fixing the right to receive it
- Amount must be determinable with reasonable accuracy
- Economic performance must have occurred

**Expense Deduction:**
- Deduct expenses when liability is fixed
- Amount must be determinable
- Economic performance must have occurred

### Combination Method

Use different methods for different aspects:
- Cash for some items, accrual for others
- Must clearly reflect income for each item
- Must use same method for similar items

## Special Methods of Accounting

### Installment Sales
- Report gain as payments are received
- Use [[Form 6252]]
- Interest portion taxed as ordinary income

### Long-Term Contracts
- Percentage of completion method
- Completed contract method (limited situations)

### Cash vs. Accrual Comparison

| Event | Cash Method | Accrual Method |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Income received | Report when received | Report when earned |
| Expenses paid | Deduct when paid | Deduct when incurred |
| Accounts receivable | Not tracked | Reported as income |
| Accounts payable | Not tracked | Deducted as expense |

## Who Must Use Accrual Method

Required if:
1. You have inventory (unless small business taxpayer)
2. C corporation (other than qualified personal service corporation)
3. Partnership with C corporation partner
4. Tax shelter

## Small Business Taxpayer Exception

A small business taxpayer may use the cash method even with inventory if:
- Average annual gross receipts ≤ $31 million (for 2025) for prior 3 years
- Not a tax shelter

**Small business taxpayer may also:**
- Not account for inventory (treat as non-incidental materials/supplies)
- Use simpler accounting for certain contracts

## Cash Method: Constructive Receipt

You have constructive receipt of income when:
- Amount is credited to your account
- Made available without restriction
- You could have received it but chose not to

**Example:** Interest credited to your account in December 2025, but you withdraw it in January 2026 → Report as 2025 income.

**Cannot defer income by:**
- Holding checks until next year
- Refusing to accept payment

## Accrual Method: Economic Performance

For expenses, economic performance occurs when:
- Services are provided
- Property is used or provided
- Work is done

**Example:** 2025 services billed but performed in 2026 → Deduct in 2026

## Change in Accounting Method

Generally requires IRS approval via [[Form 3115]].

### Automatic Change Procedures
Many changes have simplified automatic consent procedures:
- No user fee
- Specific filing requirements
- See Revenue Procedures for details

### Section 481(a) Adjustment
When changing methods:
- Calculate cumulative effect on income
- Report adjustment over multiple years
- Prevents double-counting or omission of items

## Inventories and Accounting Methods

Per [[Pub 334 - Tax Guide for Small Business]], if inventory is an income-producing factor:

**General Rule:**
- Use accrual method for purchases and sales
- May use different method for other items

**Small Business Taxpayer Exception:**
- May use cash method
- May treat inventory as non-incidental materials/supplies
- May use simplified inventory methods

## Accounting Periods

### Calendar Tax Year
- January 1 through December 31
- Required if no books, no fiscal year qualification, or Code requirement

### Fiscal Tax Year
- 12 consecutive months ending on last day of any month except December
- 52-53 week tax year permitted

**Change in Tax Year:**
- Requires [[Form 1128]]
- Automatic approval available for certain changes

## Two or More Businesses

- May use different accounting methods for separate and distinct businesses
- Must maintain complete separate books for each
- Methods must clearly reflect income for each business

## Common Questions

### Q: Which method should I choose?
**A:** Most small businesses prefer the cash method for simplicity. However, if inventory is significant, accrual may be required unless you qualify as a small business taxpayer.

### Q: Can I change methods?
**A:** Yes, but generally requires filing Form 3115. Many changes qualify for automatic consent procedures without a user fee.

### Q: What if I use the wrong method?
**A:** An incorrect method may result in penalties and adjustments. File Form 3115 to correct accounting method errors.
