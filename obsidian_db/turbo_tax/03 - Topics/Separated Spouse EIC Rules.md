# Separated Spouse EIC Rules

Married taxpayers who are separated from their spouse may be able to claim the [[Earned Income Credit|Earned Income Credit (EIC)]] even without filing a joint return. Special rules apply under [[Pub 596 - Earned Income Credit|Publication 596, Rule 3]].

## General Rule

Married taxpayers must typically file a joint return to claim the EIC. However, there is an exception for certain separated spouses.

## Qualifying Under the Special Rule

You may claim the EIC as a separated spouse if you meet ALL of the following:

1. You are married
2. You are not filing a joint return
3. You have a [[Qualifying Child|qualifying child]] who lived with you for more than half of 2025
4. AND either:
   - You lived apart from your spouse for the **last 6 months of 2025**, OR
   - You are legally separated under a written separation agreement or decree of separate maintenance, AND did not live in the same household as your spouse at the end of 2025

## Requirements for the Child

The qualifying child must:
- Meet all four qualifying child tests (relationship, age, residency, joint return)
- Have lived with you for more than half the year
- Have a valid SSN (for increased EIC amount)

## Documentation

When filing, check the box in the Dependents section on page 1 of Form 1040 or 1040-SR that indicates you meet the special rule for separated spouses.

Attach [[Schedule EIC]] to list your qualifying child.

## What Disqualifies You

You CANNOT claim EIC as a separated spouse if:
- You lived together at any time during the last 6 months of 2025 (unless legally separated)
- You are not legally separated AND lived together at the end of 2025
- You do not have a qualifying child

## Examples

### Example 1: Qualifies Under Special Rule
You and your spouse separated in June 2025. Your child lived with you from June through December. You did not live with your spouse during the last 6 months. **You qualify** for EIC under the special rule.

### Example 2: Does Not Qualify
You and your spouse had an argument in September and lived apart for October-December, but you were not legally separated and reconciled briefly in November. **You do not qualify** because you did not live apart for the entire last 6 months.

### Example 3: Legally Separated Qualifies
You have a written separation agreement dated July 1, 2025. Your child lives with you. Even though your spouse came to your house to visit the children in December, you did not live together as spouses. **You qualify** under the legal separation provision.

## Community Property States

If you live in a community property state (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin), special rules apply:

- Your earned income for EIC does NOT include amounts earned by your spouse that are treated as yours under community property laws
- Your earned income includes the entire amount YOU earned, even if part is treated as belonging to your spouse

See [[Community Property]] for more details.

## Related Filing Status

Separated spouses who qualify for EIC may also be able to file as [[Head of Household]] if they meet additional requirements:
- Paid more than half the cost of keeping up the home
- Home was main home of qualifying child for more than half the year
- Spouse lived apart for last 6 months of the year

## Impact on Other Credits

If you qualify under the separated spouse rules for EIC, you may also qualify for:
- [[Child and Dependent Care Credit]] - If you meet the work-related expense tests
- [[Child Tax Credit]] - For qualifying children
