# Basis of Rental Property

## Overview

Basis is the amount of your investment in property for tax purposes. It's used to figure depreciation, gain or loss on sale, and various other tax calculations. Understanding basis is fundamental to properly reporting rental property.

## Cost Basis

Basis is usually what you paid for the property, including:

### Included in Basis
- Purchase price (cash, debt, property, or services)
- Sales tax charged on purchase
- Freight and delivery charges
- Installation and testing charges

### Real Property Settlement Fees
**Included in basis**:
- Abstract fees
- Legal fees
- Utility installation charges
- Recording fees
- Transfer taxes
- Surveys
- Title insurance
- Amounts owed by seller that you agree to pay (back taxes, recording fees, commissions)

**NOT included in basis**:
- Fire insurance premiums
- Rent/occupancy charges before closing
- Loan-related fees (points, appraisal, credit report, loan assumption fees)
- Escrow amounts for future taxes/insurance

## Real Estate Taxes at Purchase

- If you pay seller's owed taxes and seller doesn't reimburse → Add to basis (can't deduct)
- If you reimburse seller for taxes they paid for you → Deduct as taxes (don't add to basis)

## Assumption of Mortgage

If you assume an existing mortgage:
**Basis = Amount paid + Remaining mortgage balance**

**Example**: Buy building for $60,000 cash, assume $240,000 mortgage → Basis = $300,000

## Separating Land and Building

When cost includes both land and building, allocate based on FMV ratios:

**Example**:
- Purchase price: $200,000
- Assessment: $136,000 house, $24,000 land (total $160,000)
- House FMV ratio: 85% → Basis: $170,000
- Land FMV ratio: 15% → Basis: $30,000

Land is not depreciable; only the building basis is depreciated.

## Adjusted Basis

Adjust basis for events between acquisition and placement in service:

### Increases to Basis
- Additions and improvements with useful life > 1 year
- Post-casualty restoration costs
- Utility line extension costs
- Legal fees for title defense or zoning
- Local improvement assessments (streets, sidewalks)

### Decreases to Basis
- Insurance or casualty payments received
- Previously deducted casualty losses
- Easement payments received
- Energy credits claimed (if added to basis)
- Section 179 or bonus depreciation claimed
- Depreciation deducted or deductible

### Special Decreases (Former Main Home)
If rental property was previously your main home, also decrease basis by:
- Postponed gain from pre-May 7, 1997 home sale
- DC first-time homebuyer credit
- Discharged qualified principal residence indebtedness (2007+)

## Basis Other Than Cost

Different basis rules apply if property was received:
- In return for services performed
- As a gift
- In an exchange for other property
- From spouse or former spouse (divorce)
- As an inheritance

See [[Pub 551 - Basis of Assets]] for these situations.

## Property Changed to Rental Use

When converting personal property to rental use, basis for depreciation is the **lesser of**:

1. **FMV on date of conversion**, OR
2. **Adjusted basis on date of conversion**

**Example**:
- Adjusted basis: $164,000
- FMV at conversion: $147,000 (house), $21,000 (land)
- Depreciation basis for house: $147,000 (FMV is less)

This rule prevents claiming depreciation on personal losses.
