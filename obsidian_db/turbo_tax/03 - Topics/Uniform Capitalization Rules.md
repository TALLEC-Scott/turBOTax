# Uniform Capitalization Rules

## Overview

The uniform capitalization rules under Section 263A specify costs that must be added to the basis of property produced or acquired for resale, rather than deducted as current expenses.

## Activities Subject to the Rules

You must use uniform capitalization rules if you:

1. **Produce real or tangible personal property for use** in your business or profit-seeking activity
2. **Produce real or tangible personal property for sale** to customers
3. **Acquire property for resale**

### What "Produce" Means

Produce includes: construct, build, install, manufacture, develop, improve, create, raise, or grow property.

Treat property produced for you under a contract as produced by you (up to amounts you pay).

### Tangible Personal Property

Includes films, sound recordings, video tapes, books, and similar property.

## What Must Be Capitalized

Under the rules, you must capitalize:

- **All direct costs** of production or resale
- **An allocable part of most indirect costs** related to production or resale activities

**Capitalize** = Include in basis of property or inventory costs rather than deduct as current expense. Recover through depreciation, amortization, or cost of goods sold.

### Example: Business Meals
If a business meal deduction is limited to 50% of cost:
- The 50% deductible portion is subject to uniform capitalization rules
- The nondeductible 50% is NOT subject to the rules

## Exceptions

### Small Business Exception (2025)

You're NOT subject to uniform capitalization rules if:

| Requirement | Threshold |
|-------------|-----------|
| Average annual gross receipts | $31 million or less |
| Period | 3 preceding tax years |
| Business type | Not a tax shelter |

**Note:** The threshold may be adjusted for inflation. Check IRS.gov/Newsroom/Inflation-AdjustedTax-Items-by-Tax-Year for current amounts.

### Other Exceptions

The following are NOT subject to uniform capitalization rules:

1. **Property you produce** that you don't use in your trade, business, or profit-seeking activity
2. **Research and experimental expenses** deductible under Section 174 or 174A
3. **Qualified creative expenses** of freelance writers, photographers, or artists
4. **Property produced under long-term contracts** (except certain home construction contracts)
5. **Personal property acquired for resale** (before 2018, if average gross receipts ≤ $10 million)

## Deducting vs. Capitalizing Costs

Some costs you can choose to either deduct or capitalize:

| Cost Type | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| Carrying charges (interest, taxes) | Except when required to capitalize |
| Research and experimental costs | Can elect to deduct or capitalize |
| Intangible drilling costs | Oil, gas, geothermal wells |
| Exploration costs | New mineral deposits |
| Circulation costs | Newspapers and periodicals |
| Mining development costs | New mineral deposits |
| Barrier removal costs | Disability/elderly access (reduce by credit) |

**Rule:** If you capitalize, include in basis. If you deduct, don't include in basis.

## Impact on Basis

Costs capitalized under these rules:

- Increase the basis of the property produced
- Are recovered through depreciation or amortization
- Or through cost of goods sold when property is sold
