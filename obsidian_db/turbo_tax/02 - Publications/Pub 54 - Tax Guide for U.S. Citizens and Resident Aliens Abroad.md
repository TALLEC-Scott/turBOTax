# Pub 54 - Tax Guide for U.S. Citizens and Resident Aliens Abroad

## Summary

Publication 54 provides comprehensive tax guidance for U.S. citizens and resident aliens living and working abroad. It covers filing requirements, exclusions, deductions, and credits specific to international taxpayers.

**Key Principle:** U.S. citizens and resident aliens are taxed on their **worldwide income** regardless of where they live. However, special provisions may exclude or reduce U.S. tax on foreign-earned income.

## Who Must File

If you are a U.S. citizen or resident alien living abroad, you must file a U.S. income tax return if your worldwide gross income meets the filing requirements for your filing status.

### Gross Income Includes
- All income from U.S. and foreign sources
- Income you exclude under the foreign earned income exclusion
- Income you exclude as a foreign housing amount

### Self-Employed Individuals
Must file if net earnings from self-employment are **$400 or more**.

## Key Tax Benefits

### Foreign Earned Income Exclusion
U.S. citizens and resident aliens working abroad may exclude a portion of foreign earned income from U.S. taxation. See [[Foreign Earned Income Exclusion]] for detailed requirements.

**Requirements:**
1. [[Tax Home]] in a foreign country
2. [[Foreign Earned Income Exclusion#Bona Fide Residence Test|Bona Fide Residence Test]] OR [[Physical Presence Test]]
3. Valid election on Form 2555

### Foreign Housing Exclusion/Deduction
In addition to the foreign earned income exclusion, you may exclude or deduct certain housing costs. See [[Foreign Housing Exclusion and Deduction]].

### Foreign Tax Credit
You may claim a credit or deduction for income taxes paid to foreign countries. See [[Foreign Tax Credit]] for rules on claiming this benefit.

**Important:** You cannot claim a foreign tax credit for taxes paid on income excluded under FEIE.

## Filing Deadlines and Extensions

### Automatic 2-Month Extension
Available if on the regular due date (April 15) you are:
- Living outside the U.S. and Puerto Rico with your main place of business/post of duty outside the U.S.
- In military or naval service on duty outside the U.S.

**Note:** Interest still accrues from April 15 on any unpaid tax.

### Additional Extensions
- **Form 4868:** Additional 4-month extension (total 6 months to October 15)
- **Form 2350:** Extension for meeting bona fide residence or physical presence tests

See [[Filing Requirements for Expats]] for detailed filing information.

## Self-Employment Tax

Self-employed individuals abroad must pay self-employment tax on net earnings of $400 or more, even if income is excluded under FEIE. See [[Self-Employment Tax Abroad]].

**Key Points:**
- FEIE does not reduce self-employment tax liability
- Social security agreements (totalization agreements) may exempt you from dual coverage
- Use Schedule SE (Form 1040) to calculate

## Tax Treaties

The U.S. has income tax treaties with many countries providing benefits such as:
- Reduced withholding rates
- Exemption from foreign taxation
- Non-discrimination protections

See [[Tax Treaties]] for treaty benefits and claiming procedures.

## Special Situations

### Nonresident Alien Spouse
You may elect to treat a nonresident alien spouse as a U.S. resident for tax purposes by filing a joint return with an attached statement.

### Blocked Income
Income that cannot be converted to U.S. dollars due to foreign restrictions may be deferred until it becomes unblockable.

### U.S. Territories
Special rules apply to residents of:
- U.S. Virgin Islands
- Guam
- Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands
- Puerto Rico
- American Samoa
