# Publication 908 - Bankruptcy Tax Guide

## Summary

Publication 908 explains the federal income tax aspects of bankruptcy proceedings. It covers the tax treatment of bankruptcy estates, filing requirements for debtors and estates, debt cancellation rules, and the discharge of tax debts in bankruptcy.

**Key Purpose:** Provides guidance on how bankruptcy affects tax obligations for individuals, partnerships, and corporations under Chapters 7, 11, 12, and 13 of the Bankruptcy Code.

## Core Concepts

### Bankruptcy Estate Tax Treatment

When an individual files under [[Chapter 7 Bankruptcy Tax|Chapter 7]] or [[Chapter 11 Bankruptcy Tax|Chapter 11]], the [[Bankruptcy Estate]] is treated as a separate taxable entity. The estate files Form 1041 while the debtor continues to file Form 1040.

For [[Chapter 13 Bankruptcy Tax|Chapter 12]] and Chapter 13 cases, no separate taxable entity is created—the debtor files Form 1040 as usual.

### Filing Threshold (2025)

For 2025, a bankruptcy estate must file Form 1041 only if gross income is at least **$15,750** (equal to the standard deduction for married filing separately).

## Short Tax Year Election

Individual debtors in Chapter 7 or 11 may elect under IRC Section 1398 to close their tax year on the day before filing bankruptcy. This creates two short tax years:

1. **First short year:** Ends day before bankruptcy filing
2. **Second short year:** Begins on bankruptcy filing date

The election must be made by filing Form 1040 for the first short year by the 15th day of the 4th month after that year ends.

## Tax Rates for Bankruptcy Estates

The bankruptcy estate uses the **tax rate schedule for Married Individuals Filing Separately**—not the estate and trust tax rates.

The estate may itemize deductions or take the basic standard deduction for married filing separately ($15,750 for 2025).

## Administrative Expenses

Bankruptcy administrative expenses (attorney fees, accounting fees, court costs) are deductible on Schedule 1 (Form 1040), line 24z as an adjustment to income. These can only be deducted by the estate, never by the debtor.
