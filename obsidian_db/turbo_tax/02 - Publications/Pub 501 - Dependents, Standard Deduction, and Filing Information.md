# Publication 501 - Dependents, Standard Deduction, and Filing Information

## Summary

Publication 501 covers the fundamental rules for filing federal income tax returns, including who must file, which filing status to use, how to claim dependents, and how to calculate the standard deduction.

## What's New for 2025

### Filing Thresholds Increased
The gross income thresholds for filing requirements have increased. See [[Filing Requirements]] for the new amounts.

### Standard Deduction Increased
Standard deduction amounts are higher for 2025:
- Single: $15,750
- Married Filing Jointly: $31,500
- Head of Household: $23,625

### Enhanced Deduction for Seniors (New)
Taxpayers age 65 or older may claim an enhanced deduction up to $6,000 ($12,000 if married filing jointly and both spouses eligible). The deduction phases out above $75,000 MAGI ($150,000 MFJ).

### Trump Accounts and Form 4547 (New)
Parents, guardians, and authorized individuals may establish a new type of IRA called a "Trump account" for children born after 2024 and before 2029. A $1,000 pilot program contribution may be available. See Form 4547.

## Key Topics Covered

### Filing Requirements

You must file a tax return if your gross income exceeds the threshold for your filing status and age. Even if not required, you should file to claim refunds or credits.

See [[Filing Requirements]] for detailed thresholds and special situations.

### Filing Status

Your filing status determines your tax rates, standard deduction, and eligibility for credits.

**Five Filing Statuses:**
1. [[Filing Status#Single|Single]]
2. [[Filing Status#Married Filing Jointly|Married Filing Jointly]]
3. [[Filing Status#Married Filing Separately|Married Filing Separately]]
4. [[Head of Household]]
5. [[Qualifying Surviving Spouse]]

### Dependents

A dependent is either a [[Qualifying Child]] or a [[Qualifying Relative]]. Claiming a dependent can provide tax benefits including the [[Child Tax Credit]] and [[Credit for Other Dependents]].

**Key Tests for Dependents:**
- Dependent taxpayer test
- Joint return test
- Citizen or resident test

### Standard Deduction

The standard deduction reduces your taxable income. The amount depends on your filing status, age, and whether you're blind.

See [[Standard Deduction]] for complete tables and rules.

## 2025 Filing Requirements Table

| Filing Status | Age at End of 2025 | File if Gross Income At Least |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Single | Under 65 | $15,750 |
| Single | 65 or older | $17,750 |
| Head of Household | Under 65 | $23,625 |
| Head of Household | 65 or older | $25,625 |
| Married Filing Jointly | Both under 65 | $31,500 |
| Married Filing Jointly | One spouse 65+ | $33,100 |
| Married Filing Jointly | Both 65+ | $34,700 |
| Married Filing Separately | Any age | $5 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse | Under 65 | $31,500 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse | 65 or older | $33,100 |

**Note:** Gross income includes all income not exempt from tax, including income from outside the U.S. Do not include Social Security benefits unless married filing separately and lived with spouse, or if half of benefits plus other income exceeds $25,000 ($32,000 if MFJ).

## Other Situations Requiring You to File

You must file a return even with income below the thresholds if:

1. You owe special taxes (AMT, additional tax on IRAs, household employment taxes, recapture taxes)
2. You received Archer MSA, Medicare Advantage MSA, or HSA distributions
3. You had net self-employment earnings of at least $400
4. You had wages of $108.28+ from a church exempt from employer Social Security/Medicare taxes
5. You received advance premium tax credit payments
6. You have section 965 income inclusion or net tax liability
7. You transferred a clean vehicle credit to a dealer
