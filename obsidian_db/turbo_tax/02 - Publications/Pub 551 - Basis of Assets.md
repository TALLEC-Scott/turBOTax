# Publication 551 - Basis of Assets

## Summary

Publication 551 explains how to determine the basis of property for tax purposes. Basis is the amount of your investment in property and is used to figure depreciation, amortization, depletion, casualty losses, and gain or loss on sale or disposition. The publication covers cost basis, adjusted basis, and basis other than cost (such as property received as a gift or inheritance).

## What's New for 2025

### Uniform Capitalization Rules Threshold
For tax years beginning in 2025, small businesses are not subject to the uniform capitalization rules if average annual gross receipts are $31 million or less for the 3 preceding tax years and the business isn't a tax shelter.

### Inherited Property Basis Consistency
Section 1014(f) requires that basis of certain property acquired from a decedent be consistent with the value of the property as finally determined for estate tax purposes. See T.D. 9991 for final regulations.

## Main Sections

### Cost Basis
The basis of property you buy is usually its cost. Cost includes:
- Cash paid
- Debt obligations assumed
- Sales tax and excise taxes
- Installation and testing fees
- Freight charges
- Legal and accounting fees (when capitalized)
- Recording fees and revenue stamps
- Real estate taxes assumed from seller

### Adjusted Basis
Original basis is adjusted by:
- **Increases**: Capital improvements, assessments for local improvements, legal fees for defending title
- **Decreases**: Depreciation, Section 179 deduction, casualty losses, certain credits

### Basis Other Than Cost
Special rules apply when property is:
- Received as a gift
- Inherited from a decedent
- Received in a nontaxable exchange
- Converted from personal to business use
