# Publication 537 - Installment Sales

## Summary

IRS Publication 537 explains the tax rules for **installment sales**—sales of property where at least one payment is received after the tax year of the sale. The installment method allows taxpayers to spread gain recognition over the period payments are received, providing potential tax deferral benefits.

## Key Concepts

- **[[Installment Sales]]** - Definition and qualification requirements
- **[[Gross Profit Percentage]]** - How to calculate and apply to payments
- **[[Installment Obligation]]** - The buyer's debt instrument securing future payments
- **[[Depreciation Recapture]]** - Ordinary income treatment on depreciable property
- **[[Unstated Interest and OID]]** - Interest imputation rules for below-market financing

## What Qualifies as an Installment Sale

An installment sale is a sale of property where you receive at least one payment after the tax year of the sale. Key requirements:

1. **Gain on sale** - Must realize a gain (cannot use installment method for losses)
2. **Payment after year-end** - At least one payment due after the tax year of sale
3. **Eligible property** - Not inventory, dealer property, or publicly traded securities

### Ineligible Transactions

- Sale of inventory (personal property held for sale in ordinary course of business)
- Dealer sales of personal property
- Stock or securities traded on an established securities market
- Sales where you elect out of the installment method

## Figuring Installment Sale Income

### Three Components of Each Payment

Each payment consists of:
1. **Return of adjusted basis** (tax-free)
2. **Interest income** (ordinary income)
3. **Gain on sale** (installment sale income)

### Gross Profit Percentage Formula

```
Gross Profit Percentage = Gross Profit ÷ Contract Price
```

**Gross Profit** = Selling Price − Adjusted Basis − Selling Expenses − Depreciation Recapture

**Contract Price** = Selling Price − Mortgages and Liabilities Assumed + Excess of Liabilities Over Basis

## Special Rules

### Electing Out of the Installment Method

You may elect to report the entire gain in the year of sale by not filing Form 6252 and instead reporting on Form 8949, Form 4797, or both. Election must be made by the due date (including extensions) for the tax return for the year of sale.

### [[Sale to Related Person]]

Special rules apply when selling to related parties:
- **Depreciable property** - Generally cannot use installment method
- **Second disposition rule** - May have to report remaining gain if related person disposes of property within 2 years

### [[Pledge Rule]]

If you use an installment obligation to secure debt (and selling price exceeds $150,000), the net debt proceeds may be treated as a payment on the obligation. Exceptions apply for:
- Sales of property used or produced in farming
- Qualifying sales of timeshares and residential lots
- Sales of personal-use property

### [[Repossession]]

Rules for figuring gain/loss when repossessing property:
- **Personal property** - Gain/loss = FMV − Basis in obligation − Repossession costs
- **Real property** - Special rules allow recovery of original adjusted basis

### [[Like-Kind Exchange]]

Section 1031 like-kind exchange treatment applies only to exchanges of real property held for use in a trade or business or for investment (after December 31, 2017). Installment sale rules may apply to "boot" received in a like-kind exchange.

## Interest on Deferred Tax

For large installment obligations (over $5 million), interest may be charged on deferred tax liability under Section 453A. Applies if:
- Total nondealer installment obligations exceed $5 million at year-end
- Property sales price exceeded $150,000

## Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) Investments

Capital gains invested in a QOF may be deferred until the earlier of:
- Sale or exchange of the QOF investment
- December 31, 2026

Permanent exclusion may apply if QOF investment held for at least 10 years. Report on [[Form 8997]].

## Common Questions

### Q: Can I use the installment method for a loss?
**A:** No. The installment method can only be used to report gains. Losses must be deducted in the tax year of sale.

### Q: What if the buyer assumes my mortgage?
**A:** If the mortgage is not more than your basis, it's treated as recovery of basis (not a payment). If it exceeds your basis, the excess is treated as a payment in the year of sale.

### Q: How do I report the sale on my tax return?
**A:** Use [[Form 6252]] for each year of the installment agreement. Also report on [[Schedule D (Form 1040)]] or [[Form 4797]] as appropriate.
