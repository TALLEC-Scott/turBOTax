# Publication 502 - Medical and Dental Expenses

## Summary

Publication 502 explains the itemized deduction for medical and dental expenses claimed on [[Schedule A (Form 1040)|Schedule A]]. It covers what expenses qualify, whose expenses you can include, how to treat reimbursements, and how to figure and report the deduction.

## Key Rules

### AGI Limitation

You can deduct on [[Schedule A (Form 1040)|Schedule A]] only the part of your medical and dental expenses that exceeds **7.5% of your adjusted gross income (AGI)**.

### What Are Medical Expenses?

Medical expenses are costs of:
- Diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease
- Affecting any structure or function of the body
- Payments for legal medical services by physicians, surgeons, dentists, and other practitioners
- Equipment, supplies, and diagnostic devices needed for these purposes

Expenses must be primarily to alleviate or prevent a physical or mental disability or illness. They don't include expenses merely beneficial to general health.

## Whose Expenses Can You Include?

You can include medical expenses paid for:

1. **Yourself** - Any medical expenses you pay
2. **Spouse** - If married when services were provided or when you paid
3. **Dependents** - If the person was your dependent when services were provided or when paid

See [[Qualifying Child]] and [[Qualifying Relative]] for dependent rules.

### Special Rules

- **Decedent**: Expenses paid by estate within 1 year of death can be treated as paid by decedent
- **Divorced Parents**: Each parent can include expenses they pay for child
- **Multiple Support Agreement**: You can include expenses paid for person you claim under agreement

## Deductible Medical Expenses

### Medical Care
- Physician and surgeon fees
- Hospital services (meals and lodging included if principal reason is medical care)
- Dental treatment (X-rays, fillings, braces, extractions, dentures)
- Psychiatric care and psychoanalysis
- Eye exams, eyeglasses, contact lenses, eye surgery
- Hearing aids and batteries
- Artificial limbs and teeth
- Crutches and wheelchairs

### Insurance
- Health insurance premiums (medical, dental, vision)
- Medicare Part A premiums (if voluntarily enrolled)
- Medicare Part B and Part D premiums
- Long-term care insurance (subject to age-based limits)

### Long-Term Care
- Qualified long-term care services
- Long-term care insurance premiums (limits based on age):
  - Age 40 or under: $480
  - Age 41-50: $900
  - Age 51-60: $1,800
  - Age 61-70: $4,810
  - Age 71 or over: $6,020

### Home Modifications
See [[Capital Medical Expenses]] for special rules on home improvements for medical care.

### Transportation
- Ambulance service
- Bus, taxi, train, or plane fares for medical care
- Car expenses: 21 cents per mile (2025) or actual out-of-pocket costs
- Parking fees and tolls
- Transportation of nurse or parent accompanying patient

### Other Deductible Expenses
- Abortion (legal)
- Acupuncture
- Alcoholism and drug addiction treatment
- Birth control pills (prescribed)
- Breast pumps and supplies
- Fertility enhancement (in vitro fertilization)
- Guide dog or service animal (purchase, training, maintenance)
- Laboratory fees
- Lead-based paint removal (for lead-poisoned child)
- Legal fees to authorize treatment for mental illness
- Lodging (up to $50/night/person) while away for medical care
- Medical conferences (admission and transportation)
- Medicines (prescribed) and insulin
- Nursing home (if principal reason is medical care)
- Nursing services
- Oxygen and oxygen equipment
- Physical examinations
- Pregnancy test kits
- Special education (for learning disabilities)
- Stop-smoking programs
- Transplants (donor expenses)
- Weight-loss program (if for specific disease like obesity)

## Nondeductible Expenses

- Baby sitting and childcare for healthy children
- Controlled substances not legal under federal law
- Cosmetic surgery (unless for deformity from disease/injury)
- Dancing or swimming lessons for general health
- Diaper services (unless for specific disease)
- Electrolysis or hair removal
- Funeral expenses
- Health club dues
- Health Savings Account contributions
- Household help (non-medical)
- Illegal operations and treatments
- Imported medicines (unless legally imported)
- Life insurance premiums
- Maternity clothes
- Nonprescription drugs (except insulin)
- Nutritional supplements (unless prescribed for specific condition)
- Personal use items
- Premiums paid with pre-tax dollars (employer plans)
- Teeth whitening
- Veterinary fees (except service animals)
- Weight-loss program for general health

## Reimbursements

Reduce medical expenses by any reimbursements received from insurance or other sources. See [[Health Insurance Premiums]] for special rules.

### Excess Reimbursement

If reimbursement exceeds medical expenses:
- Premiums paid by you: Generally not taxable
- Premiums paid by employer: Taxable to extent of employer contribution
- Premiums split: Partially taxable (use worksheets in publication)

## Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction

If self-employed, you may deduct health insurance premiums as an adjustment to income (not subject to 7.5% AGI limit). See [[Self-Employment Tax]] and Form 7206.

## How to Claim

1. Total all medical expenses paid during the year
2. Subtract 7.5% of AGI
3. Report the remainder on [[Schedule A (Form 1040)|Schedule A]], line 1
