# Publication 596 - Earned Income Credit

**Comprehensive guide to the Earned Income Credit (EIC) for tax year 2025.**

## Overview

The Earned Income Credit (EIC) is a refundable tax credit for certain people who work and have earned income under $68,675. A tax credit reduces the amount of tax owed and may result in a refund. The EIC is designed to provide financial assistance to low-to-moderate income working individuals and families.

## What's New for 2025

### Trump Accounts and Form 4547
Recent legislation allows parents, guardians, and authorized individuals to establish "Trump accounts" (a new type of IRA) for children born after 2024 and before 2029. A $1,000 pilot program contribution may be available. Elections are made on Form 4547.

### Form 1040 Changes
- **Dependents section redesigned** with numbered rows and additional information requests
- **New checkbox** on Form 1040 to indicate if main home was in the U.S. for over half of 2025
- **New checkbox** in Dependents section for special rule for separated spouses
- **New checkboxes** for clergy filing Schedule SE and EIC eligibility

### Income Limits for 2025

| Qualifying Children | Single/Head of Household | Married Filing Jointly |
|---------------------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| 0 | $19,104 | $26,214 |
| 1 | $50,434 | $57,554 |
| 2 | $57,310 | $64,430 |
| 3 or more | $61,555 | $68,675 |

### Investment Income Limit
Maximum investment income to qualify: **$11,950**

## EIC Eligibility Rules

### Chapter 1: Rules for Everyone (Rules 1-7)

#### Rule 1: AGI Limits
Your adjusted gross income must be less than the applicable limit based on filing status and number of qualifying children with valid SSNs.

#### Rule 2: Valid SSN Required
You (and your spouse if filing jointly) must have a valid SSN by the due date of your 2025 return (including extensions). Qualifying children must have valid SSNs to claim a higher EIC amount.

#### Rule 3: Separated Spouse Rules
If married but not filing jointly, you may qualify under special rules if:
- You have a qualifying child who lived with you for more than half of 2025
- You lived apart from spouse for last 6 months of 2025, OR
- You are legally separated under written separation agreement or decree of separate maintenance

#### Rule 4: U.S. Citizen or Resident Alien
Must be a U.S. citizen or resident alien all year. Nonresident aliens can qualify only if filing married filing jointly with a U.S. citizen/resident alien spouse.

#### Rule 5: Cannot File Form 2555
Cannot claim EIC if you file Form 2555 (Foreign Earned Income exclusion).

#### Rule 6: Investment Income Limit
Investment income must be $11,950 or less.

#### Rule 7: Must Have Earned Income
Must have earned income from work. If married filing jointly, at least one spouse must have earned income.

### Chapter 2: Rules if You Have a Qualifying Child (Rules 8-10)

#### Rule 8: Qualifying Child Tests
Child must meet all four tests:
1. **Relationship**: Son, daughter, stepchild, foster child, sibling, or descendant
2. **Age**: Under 19 (under 24 if full-time student), or any age if permanently and totally disabled
3. **Residency**: Lived with you in the U.S. for more than half the year
4. **Joint Return**: Cannot file joint return (except to claim refund only)

#### Rule 9: Tiebreaker Rules
If child qualifies for more than one person, tiebreaker rules determine who can claim the EIC.

#### Rule 10: Cannot Be Qualifying Child of Another
You cannot be the qualifying child of another taxpayer.

### Chapter 3: Rules if You Do Not Have a Qualifying Child (Rules 11-14)

#### Rule 11: Age Requirements
Must be at least age 25 but under age 65 at end of 2025.

#### Rule 12: Cannot Be Dependent of Another
Cannot be claimed as a dependent on another person's return.

#### Rule 13: Cannot Be Qualifying Child of Another
Cannot be the qualifying child of another taxpayer.

#### Rule 14: U.S. Residency
Must have lived in the United States more than half of the year.

## Earned Income Definition

### What Counts as Earned Income
- Wages, salaries, tips (Form W-2, box 1)
- Net earnings from self-employment
- Gross income as statutory employee
- Disability benefits (if under minimum retirement age)
- Strike and lockout benefits
- Nontaxable combat pay (optional election)

### What Does NOT Count as Earned Income
- Interest and dividends
- Pensions and annuities
- Social Security benefits
- Alimony and child support
- Welfare benefits
- Workers' compensation
- Unemployment compensation
- Nontaxable foster care payments
- Veterans' benefits

## Special Situations

### Members of the Clergy
Housing allowances and rental values for ministers have special EIC rules. See Form 4361 and Form 4029.

### Members of the Military
- Nontaxable combat pay can be included in earned income (may increase EIC)
- Extended active duty outside U.S. counts as U.S. residency
- See [[Pub 3 - Armed Forces Tax Guide]]

### Self-Employed Individuals
Calculate earned income on EIC Worksheet B in Form 1040 instructions.

### Disability Benefits
Taxable disability benefits count as earned income until minimum retirement age is reached.

## Claiming the EIC

### Required Forms
- **Form 1040 or 1040-SR**: Lines 27a-27c
- **Schedule EIC**: Required if claiming with qualifying child
- **Form 8862**: Required if EIC was previously disallowed

### IRS Can Figure the EIC
The IRS will calculate your EIC if you qualify and prefer not to calculate it yourself.

## Disallowance of EIC

EIC may be disallowed if:
- You claimed EIC fraudulently (10-year ban)
- You claimed EIC recklessly (2-year ban)
- You did not meet eligibility requirements

Form 8862 is required to claim EIC after disallowance.

## Impact on Welfare Benefits

EIC refunds cannot be counted as income for determining eligibility for:
- Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF)
- Supplemental Security Income (SSI)
- Medicaid
- Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP)
- Low-income housing
