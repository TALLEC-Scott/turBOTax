# Pub 915 - Social Security and Equivalent Railroad Retirement Benefits

## Summary

Publication 915 explains the federal income tax rules for Social Security benefits and the Social Security Equivalent Benefit (SSEB) portion of Tier 1 railroad retirement benefits. It covers how to determine if benefits are taxable, how much is taxable, and special rules for lump-sum payments and repayments.

**Prepared jointly by:** IRS, Social Security Administration (SSA), and U.S. Railroad Retirement Board (RRB)

## What's Covered

- Whether any of your benefits are taxable
- How to report taxable benefits
- How to treat lump-sum benefit payments
- How much of your benefits is taxable
- Deductions related to your benefits
- Worksheets for calculating taxable benefits

## What's NOT Covered

This publication does NOT cover:
- Non-social security equivalent benefit (NSSEB) portion of Tier 1 benefits
- Tier 2 railroad retirement benefits
- Supplemental annuity benefits
- Vested dual benefits
- Foreign social security benefits

For these, see [[Pub 575 - Pension and Annuity Income]].

## Types of Benefits

### Social Security Benefits (Taxable May Apply)
- Monthly retirement benefits
- Survivor benefits
- Disability benefits

### NOT Taxable
- Supplemental Security Income (SSI) payments
- Lump-sum death payments
- Benefits for months before December 1983

### Railroad Retirement Benefits Covered
- **SSEB portion of Tier 1** - Taxed like Social Security
- **Special guaranty benefits** - Taxed like Social Security

## Determining Taxability

### Base Amounts (2025)

| Filing Status | Base Amount |
|---------------|-------------|
| Single, Head of Household, Qualifying Surviving Spouse | $25,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $32,000 |
| Married Filing Separately (lived apart all year) | $25,000 |
| Married Filing Separately (lived with spouse at any time) | $0 |

### Quick Check (Worksheet A)

Compare to base amount:
1. One-half of your benefits (Box 5 of SSA-1099/RRB-1099)
2. Plus all other income (including tax-exempt interest)
3. Don't reduce by exclusions for qualified savings bonds, education loans, adoption benefits, foreign earned income, or Puerto Rico/American Samoa income

If total exceeds base amount, some benefits may be taxable.

## Maximum Taxable Percentage

| Situation | Maximum Taxable |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Combined income ≤ base amount | 0% |
| Combined income between base and $34,000 ($44,000 MFJ) | Up to 50% |
| Combined income over $34,000 ($44,000 MFJ) | Up to 85% |
| Married Filing Separately (lived with spouse) | 85% |
| Nonresident aliens | 85% (withheld at 30%) |

## Reporting on Tax Return

### Form 1040/1040-SR Lines

| Line | What to Report |
|------|----------------|
| 6a | Net benefits (Box 5 of all SSA-1099 and RRB-1099) |
| 6b | Taxable amount (from worksheet) |
| 6c | Check if using lump-sum election method |
| 6d | Check if MFS and lived apart all year |

## Lump-Sum Election

If you received a lump-sum payment in 2025 for an earlier year, you may be able to lower your taxable benefits by using the lump-sum election method.

### How It Works
1. Complete Worksheet 1 (regular method)
2. Complete Worksheet 2 or 3 (for lump-sum for years after/before 1993)
3. Complete Worksheet 4 (compare methods)
4. If lump-sum method gives lower taxable amount, check box on line 6c

### When to Use
- SSA-1099 shows benefits received for prior years (indicated by asterisk in Box 3)
- Prior year income was lower than current year
- May result in lower overall taxable benefits

**Do NOT file amended return for prior year** - make the election on current year return only.

## Repayments

### Repayment Less Than Gross Benefits
- Subtract repayment from gross benefits
- Use net amount (Box 5) for all calculations

### Repayments More Than Gross Benefits
- None of your benefits are taxable
- May be able to deduct excess repayment or claim credit
- See "Repayments More Than Gross Benefits" section of Pub 915

## Withholding Options

You can have federal income tax withheld from benefits:
- File **Form W-4V** with SSA or RRB
- Choose withholding rate: 7%, 10%, 12%, or 22%
- Or make estimated tax payments (see [[Pub 505 - Tax Withholding and Estimated Tax]])

## Nonresident Aliens

- 85% of benefits taxed at 30% rate (unless treaty exemption)
- Receive Form SSA-1042S or RRB-1042S
- Treaty exemptions available for residents of: Canada, Germany, Egypt, Ireland, Israel, Italy, Japan, Romania, United Kingdom
- Swiss residents: 15% rate
- File Form RRB-1001 with RRB to claim treaty exemption for railroad benefits

## Special Situations

### When to Use Pub 915 Worksheets Instead of Form 1040 Worksheet
1. You contributed to a traditional IRA and are covered by a retirement plan at work (use Pub 590-A worksheets)
2. You exclude: qualified savings bond interest, adoption benefits, foreign earned income, or Puerto Rico/American Samoa income
3. You received a lump-sum payment for an earlier year (use Worksheets 2, 3, and 4)

### Children's Benefits
- Rules apply to benefits received by children
- Each person reports benefits they have legal right to receive
- If check made out in parent's name for child's benefit, allocate appropriately

### U.S. Citizens Residing Abroad
Exempt from U.S. tax on benefits if resident of: Canada, Germany, Egypt, Ireland, Italy (must also be citizen), Israel, Romania, United Kingdom

### Lawful Permanent Residents (Green Card Holders)
- Not subject to 30% withholding
- If tax withheld in error, file Form 1040 with Austin, TX service center
- Attach copy of SSA-1042S, green card, and signed declaration
