# Publication 514 - Foreign Tax Credit for Individuals

## Summary

IRS Publication 514 provides comprehensive guidance on the foreign tax credit for individuals. It covers how to choose between taking a credit or deduction for foreign taxes, what foreign taxes qualify, who can take the credit, how to figure the credit, and how to carry over unused foreign taxes to other tax years.

## Who Can Take the Credit

### U.S. Citizens
Taxed on worldwide income wherever they live. Normally entitled to take a credit for foreign taxes paid or accrued.

### Resident Aliens
Can take a credit for foreign taxes subject to the same general rules as U.S. citizens.

### Nonresident Aliens
Generally cannot take the credit, with exceptions:
- Bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for entire tax year
- Pay tax to foreign country/U.S. territory on foreign source income effectively connected with U.S. trade or business

## Foreign Taxes That Qualify

Four tests must be met:
1. **Tax must be imposed on you** - Cannot shift the right to claim the credit by contract
2. **You must have paid or accrued the tax** - Including through pass-through entities
3. **Tax must be the legal and actual foreign tax liability** - Not the amount withheld if different
4. **Tax must be an income tax** - Or a tax in lieu of an income tax

### Taxes That Do NOT Qualify
- Taxes paid to sanctioned countries
- Taxes on foreign earned income exclusion amounts
- Taxes on foreign housing exclusion amounts
- Soak-up taxes (dependent on availability of credit)
- Penalties, interest, and fines
- Property taxes
- Taxes reduced by subsidies

## Credit vs. Deduction

In most cases, taking the credit is more advantageous because:
- Credit reduces U.S. tax dollar-for-dollar
- Can take credit even if not itemizing deductions
- Unused credits can be carried back and forward

**General Rule:** Must choose each year to take either credit OR deduction for ALL qualified foreign taxes.

## Form 1116 Requirements

File Form 1116 for each separate limit income category:
- Section 951A category income (GILTI)
- Passive category income
- Foreign branch category income
- General category income
- Section 901(j) income
- Certain income re-sourced by treaty
- Lump-sum distributions (LSDs)

### Exemption from Form 1116
If all these conditions are met:
- Only passive category income
- Qualified foreign taxes ≤ $300 ($600 if married filing jointly)
- All foreign income and taxes reported on payee statement
- Elect this procedure

## Foreign Tax Credit Limitation

The credit cannot exceed:
```
Total U.S. Tax × (Foreign Source Taxable Income / Worldwide Taxable Income)
```

## What's New for 2025

- **New $6,000 deduction** for taxpayers (and spouses on joint returns) age 65+ (P.L. 119-21, §151(d)(5)(C))
  - Reported on Schedule 1-A (Form 1040), line 37
  - Must be removed from taxable income for FTC limitation calculation
  - Effective for tax years 2025-2028

- **Section 960(d)(4)** disallows credit for 10% of foreign taxes on GILTI-related amounts excluded under §959(a)
  - Applies to taxes paid/accrued after June 28, 2025
